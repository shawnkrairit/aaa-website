Doing Business in Cambodia

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Cambodia is located in Southeast Asia, occupying approximately 181,000 square kilometers of land on the Indochinese Peninsula. The country is strategically positioned in the heart of mainland Southeast Asia, bordered by Thailand to the west and northwest, Laos to the northeast, Vietnam to the east and southeast, and the Gulf of Thailand to the southwest. This geographic position provides Cambodia with direct access to major regional markets and important sea routes, positioning the country as a strategic crossroads for trade and commerce within the ASEAN region.

Phnom Penh, the capital and largest city, serves as Cambodia's primary economic, political, and cultural center. The city is located at the confluence of three rivers—the Mekong, Tonlé Sap, and Bassac rivers—which historically provided the foundation for the city's development as a major trading hub. Today, Phnom Penh concentrates most government institutions, corporate headquarters, foreign diplomatic missions, and international organization offices. The city's strategic riverine location continues to facilitate trade logistics and provides connectivity to Vietnam's southern coast through the Mekong waterway. Beyond the capital, major secondary cities including Siem Reap (famous for Angkor Wat archaeological sites), Sihanoukville (primary coastal port), and Battambang serve as regional economic centers with distinct sectoral focuses.

Cambodia's population of approximately 17 million people represents one of Southeast Asia's youngest demographic profiles, with a median age of approximately 26 years. The population is predominantly ethnically Khmer, comprising approximately 90 percent of the population, with significant Vietnamese, Chinese, and other ethnic minority communities. Khmer is the official national language and primary business language, though English proficiency has expanded significantly in business and government sectors, particularly among younger workers and in major commercial centers. The country maintains a tropical monsoon climate characterized by two distinct seasons: the rainy season from May to October brings heavy precipitation supporting agricultural activities and potentially disrupting logistics, while the dry season from November to April provides optimal conditions for construction, manufacturing operations, and business travel.

1.2 Political System & Government

Cambodia operates as a constitutional monarchy with a bicameral legislature, combining monarchical traditions with democratic institutions. His Majesty King Norodom Sihamoni serves as the ceremonial and constitutional head of state, holding significant cultural and symbolic importance in Cambodian society. The Prime Minister functions as the head of government and chief executive, overseeing day-to-day governmental administration and policy implementation. The Prime Minister is appointed by the King and typically represents the political party holding the majority in the National Assembly.

Cambodia's legislative body, the Parliament, consists of two chambers. The National Assembly comprises 125 members elected through proportional representation for five-year terms, providing a representative body with responsibility for enacting legislation, approving government budgets, and exercising parliamentary oversight. The Senate comprises 61 members serving staggered six-year terms, with members selected through various appointment mechanisms including royal appointment and selection by the National Assembly. The bicameral structure creates a system of legislative checks and balances, with both chambers required to approve significant legislation.

The Royal Government of Cambodia maintains a ministerial structure with specialized departments overseeing commerce, industry, labor, finance, education, health, and other critical sectors. The Council for the Development of Cambodia (CDC), established through royal decree, functions as Cambodia's investment promotion authority and "One-Stop Service" center for investment. The CDC operates under the direct supervision of the Prime Minister and serves as the primary interface between foreign investors and government agencies. The CDC's primary responsibilities include investment project evaluation, registration of Qualified Investment Projects (QIPs), provision of investment incentives, dispute resolution through an administrative mechanism, and provision of guidance regarding incentive eligibility and benefits. This institutional framework demonstrates Cambodia's commitment to facilitating foreign investment through streamlined procedures and dedicated administrative resources focused on investor support.

1.3 Economy Overview

Cambodia's economy has demonstrated sustained resilience and growth potential despite regional and global economic challenges. The World Bank projects Cambodia's real GDP growth at 4.3 percent for 2026, representing a deceleration from historical growth rates but remaining significantly higher than the global average growth rate of 2.6 percent and competitive with the East Asia and Pacific regional average of 4.4 percent. The International Monetary Fund provides a marginally more conservative projection of approximately 4.0 percent growth in 2026, reflecting concerns regarding external demand volatility, export market dynamics, and global trade uncertainties. Importantly, both international financial institutions project a reacceleration to approximately 5.1 percent growth in 2027 and beyond, suggesting that 2026 represents a cyclical slowdown within a broader framework of sustained medium-term expansion.

Cambodia's economic growth is supported by diversified sectoral contributions. Manufacturing remains a significant driver, with garment and footwear production historically representing Cambodia's primary export industry, though the sector faces evolving competitive dynamics and trade policy uncertainties. Food and agricultural processing represents a growing manufacturing subsector, leveraging Cambodia's agricultural base to add value and diversify exports. Tourism constitutes a major revenue source and employer, with international visitor arrivals projected to rebound strongly from pandemic-affected levels. The services sector, including financial services, telecommunications, transportation, and hospitality, provides growing employment and value creation. The agricultural sector remains important to the rural economy and food security, though productivity gains and value addition remain development priorities.

Recent investment activity demonstrates strong foreign investor confidence. In February 2026 alone, the Council for the Development of Cambodia registered 42 investment projects representing combined approved capital of USD 166 million and generating approximately 24,000 new employment opportunities across multiple sectors. These projects span manufacturing (garment and footwear, food processing, automotive components, electronics), renewable energy development, agroindustry, timber products, specialty chemicals, and other sectors. The continued flow of foreign direct investment across diverse industries reflects investor recognition of Cambodia's stable investment framework, available incentive structures, and strategic position within regional value chains. Cambodia's membership in regional trade agreements, including the ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA) and bilateral agreements with major trading partners, provides preferential market access supporting export-oriented investment.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Cambodia's infrastructure has undergone significant modernization in recent years, though development remains ongoing. The country maintains an expanding network of national highways connecting Phnom Penh with provincial centers and major border crossings. The primary national airport is Phnom Penh International Airport, which handles domestic and international commercial flights, though regional airports in Siem Reap and Sihanoukville provide secondary aviation capacity. The port of Sihanoukville represents Cambodia's primary maritime access point, serving as the country's only commercial deep-water seaport and a critical node for international trade logistics.

Telecommunications infrastructure has expanded substantially, with multiple licensed telecommunications operators providing mobile cellular, fixed-line, and internet services. Mobile penetration has achieved widespread coverage, particularly in urban areas, and continues to expand in rural regions. Internet connectivity through 4G/5G mobile networks and fixed broadband has improved access to digital services, e-commerce platforms, and business applications. The National Bank of Cambodia has promoted digital payment infrastructure and financial inclusion initiatives, increasing access to banking services and reducing cash-based transaction dependency.

Power generation and distribution infrastructure continues development, with the Electricité du Cambodge (EdC) operating as the national utility. The country relies on a mix of hydroelectric power from the Mekong River, thermal generation, and increasing renewable energy capacity. While power generation capacity has expanded significantly, occasional supply constraints in peak seasons warrant consideration for power-intensive manufacturing operations. The government has prioritized renewable energy development as part of its climate commitment and energy security objectives, with opportunities for foreign investment in solar, wind, and other renewable projects.

Water supply and sanitation infrastructure in Phnom Penh is managed by the Phnom Penh Water Supply Authority (PPWSA) and operates to internationally competitive standards. Provincial water supply systems vary in development level, with modern systems in major cities and lower service levels in smaller towns. Waste management infrastructure has expanded but continues development, with private waste management companies operating alongside government systems.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Cambodia's legal system is grounded in civil law traditions, which emphasize codified legislation and administrative regulations as primary sources of law, in contrast to common law systems that rely heavily on judicial precedent. The foundation of this system was substantially reformed following Cambodia's transition to democracy in the 1990s. The Constitution of the Kingdom of Cambodia, adopted in 1993 and amended in 2008, serves as the supreme law establishing the framework for government, fundamental rights, property protections, and contractual relationships essential to commercial activity. The constitution provides a foundation for legal predictability and investor protections, establishing that property rights are secure, contracts are enforceable, and dispute resolution mechanisms exist for commercial conflicts.

The primary commercial law governing business organizations and commercial transactions is the Law on Commercial Enterprises, promulgated in 2005 and repeatedly amended to reflect evolving business practices. This law establishes the legal structures available for business organization (partnerships, limited companies, sole proprietorships), registration procedures, governance requirements, financial reporting obligations, and liability frameworks. The Law on Commercial Enterprises provides the regulatory foundation for most foreign direct investment, as it authorizes the corporate structures through which foreign investors typically conduct business and specifies the requirements for establishing and maintaining such entities.

The legal system has undergone substantial modernization to attract and facilitate foreign investment. Key legislative instruments complementing the commercial framework include the Law on Investment (establishing the investment promotion framework and incentive eligibility criteria), the Labour Law (governing employment relationships, working conditions, compensation, dispute resolution), the Law on Patents and Industrial Designs (protecting intellectual property rights), tax laws administered through the Law on Taxation (covering corporate income tax, value-added tax, withholding taxes, and other revenue sources), and banking regulations administered through the Law on Banking and Financial Institutions. This modernized legal framework provides foreign investors with clearly defined rights, obligations, and remedies comparable to those available in developed economies.

The court system comprises specialized divisions addressing civil disputes, criminal matters, administrative controversies, and commercial conflicts. The Supreme Court serves as the highest judicial authority, with provincial and municipal courts addressing disputes at local levels. Commercial cases are typically heard by civil courts with commercial law expertise. The court system provides formal dispute resolution through litigation, though foreign investors should be aware that judicial processes can involve extended timelines, and familiarity with specific judges' interpretations of law may vary. Importantly, many commercial contracts in Cambodia include arbitration clauses providing for private dispute resolution through arbitration institutions including the International Court of Arbitration (ICA), the American Arbitration Association (AAA), or other international arbitration forums. This availability of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms provides foreign investors with predictable, neutral forums for resolving significant commercial disputes while avoiding potentially unpredictable outcomes in litigation.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

Cambodia maintains one of the most liberal foreign investment policies in Southeast Asia, explicitly permitting 100 percent foreign ownership of companies across the vast majority of business sectors. This liberal policy eliminates requirements for mandatory local partners, minimum local equity participation, or technology transfer mandates that characterize investment regulations in some neighboring countries. Foreign investors enjoy national treatment in most sectors, meaning that foreign-owned companies receive the same legal and regulatory treatment as locally-owned enterprises. This equality of treatment applies to taxation, business licensing, labor relations, property rights, and access to government services.

However, certain sensitive sectors maintain restrictions on foreign ownership or require government authorization for foreign investment. These restricted sectors include cigarette and tobacco product manufacturing (limited to joint ventures with government authorization), motion picture production (requiring government cultural review), rice milling (subject to local equity participation requirements), and gemstone mining and processing (requiring prior authorization and potentially limiting foreign equity). Additionally, sectors involving national security concerns (defense contracting, telecommunications infrastructure), natural resource extraction (forestry exploitation), and regulated industries (insurance, banking) may have specific restrictions or special authorization requirements. Foreign investors proposing activities in potentially restricted sectors should conduct preliminary regulatory review with the Council for the Development of Cambodia or relevant sector ministries to clarify specific ownership restrictions and authorization procedures.

The Law on Investment, last substantively amended in 2015, provides the primary framework for foreign investment regulation and establishes eligibility for Qualified Investment Project (QIP) status, which provides significant tax incentives and other benefits. The law defines investment broadly to include capital contribution to establish new enterprises, capital increase in existing enterprises, acquisition of business shares or equity stakes, and leasing of land or property for business purposes. Foreign investors pursuing QIP status must obtain formal approval from the Council for the Development of Cambodia, documenting the investment project, capital commitments, employment projections, and sectoral focus. QIP approval triggers access to incentive benefits including corporate income tax holidays, exemptions from prepayment and minimum tax, and relief on import duties for capital equipment.

Foreign investors in Cambodia also benefit from protections available through international law. Cambodia is signatory to numerous bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with other countries that provide investor protections including fair and equitable treatment, protection against arbitrary expropriation without compensation, and access to international arbitration for disputes with the government. The bilateral investment treaty network provides an important layer of protection for significant foreign investments, offering recourse to international arbitration administered by the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID) or other international forums should disputes arise with the Cambodian government. The availability of international investment protections has historically enhanced investor confidence and supported sustained flows of foreign direct investment.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Foreign nationals seeking to conduct business or work in Cambodia must navigate Cambodia's immigration framework, which establishes visa requirements and immigration procedures. The primary visa category for business purposes is the Ordinary Visa of E-class (commonly referred to as the Business Visa or EB Visa), which is issued to foreign investors, businesspeople, and professional service providers intending to establish, operate, or manage a business enterprise in Cambodia. The E-class visa is initially valid for 30 days but can be extended for one month, three months, six months, or up to one year at a time through application to the Department of Immigration under the Ministry of Interior. Extensions are typically processed at immigration offices in Phnom Penh, provincial capitals, and border crossings.

The Cambodian government reduced the E-class visa fee from USD 42 to USD 35 effective January 1, 2025, representing a significant reduction that lowers administrative costs for business travelers and foreign entrepreneurs. The reduced fee applies to both initial issuance and extension of the E-class business visa. Foreign nationals holding an E-class visa with evidence of an approved investment in a Cambodian business (typically demonstrated through investment documentation and business registration certificates) are authorized to reside in Cambodia, conduct business operations, and manage their business enterprises. A minimum suggested investment of USD 50,000 has been established as guidance for demonstrating business viability, though actual investment amounts may vary based on business type and circumstances.

Critically, the E-class business visa does not automatically authorize foreign nationals to engage in employment or work activities. The visa authorizes management of one's own business enterprise or investment portfolio but does not permit employment with another Cambodian company, work as a contracted service provider, or engagement in professional service delivery on behalf of third parties. Any foreign national working in Cambodia—whether as an employee of a Cambodian company, as a contracted service provider, or as a professional advisor—must separately obtain a work permit and employment card, which are distinct from the business visa. This separation of business authorization and employment authorization reflects the government's intention to regulate labor market access while maintaining investment openness.

Foreign nationals intending to reside in Cambodia for extended periods may also obtain a Temporary Residence Permit (TRP), which permits long-term residence for periods up to one year and is renewable. The TRP is available to foreign nationals demonstrating legitimate grounds for residence including business operations, employment, family connection, or retirement. TRP holders enjoy similar rights to E-class visa holders regarding residence and business conduct but may have different procedural requirements for extension and documentation.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Cambodia's property and land ownership framework historically prohibited direct foreign ownership of land, based on provisions of the Constitution and the Law on Land. Under this framework, foreign individuals and foreign-controlled companies could not hold title to land and were limited to usufruct rights (long-term use rights) of up to 99 years. However, the regulatory framework has evolved, and foreign legal entities (companies) with Cambodian registration and investment project approval have obtained certain rights to acquire and hold land for business purposes, particularly through special economic zone development and government-approved projects.

The land tenure system comprises state-owned land (controlled by government), privately-owned land (held by individuals and legal entities), and communal land (held by indigenous communities). Foreign nationals cannot hold direct title to privately-owned land, but foreign-registered companies may acquire long-term usufruct rights (typically 50-99 years) or lease land from state entities for project implementation. The process of acquiring such rights typically requires government approval and is subject to specific conditions regarding use, investment commitments, and compliance with land law provisions.

Foreign investors establishing manufacturing facilities or special projects typically structure land acquisition through long-term leases from state entities or land concessions, which provide sufficient security of tenure for industrial and commercial operations. Land in established Special Economic Zones is available through SEZ management authorities, with lease terms of 50-99 years being standard for manufacturing and logistics facilities. Title security is enhanced by registration with the Land Management and Urban Planning Ministry, which maintains a cadastral system recording property descriptions, ownership, and encumbrances.

For residential or commercial real estate investment, foreign nationals may acquire condominium units in buildings with appropriate foreign ownership designation, as the Law on Condo permits foreign ownership of individual condominium units within buildings where such foreign ownership is specifically authorized. However, foreign nationals cannot own individual houses or residential land. This distinction between condominiums (which may be foreign-owned) and houses (which cannot be) reflects the government's policy of maintaining land ownership by Cambodian citizens while permitting foreign investment in specified real estate formats.

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Cambodia's legal system provides multiple mechanisms for resolving commercial disputes, each with distinct characteristics, procedural requirements, and enforceability implications. Understanding the available dispute resolution mechanisms is essential for foreign investors, as the choice of mechanism significantly impacts dispute outcomes and resolution timelines.

Litigation through the civil courts represents the formal governmental dispute resolution mechanism. Commercial disputes are heard by civil courts with commercial law expertise, typically located in Phnom Penh and major provincial capitals. The civil court system follows civil law procedures, emphasizing written pleadings, document submission, and judicial investigation of facts. Court proceedings involve submission of claims, respondent replies, presentation of evidence, and judicial pronouncements. The Cambodian civil court system has been modernized to address commercial disputes more efficiently, though proceedings typically require 18-36 months for first-instance resolution, with appeal possibilities extending timelines further. Importantly, civil court judgments are subject to appeal to appellate courts and, in some circumstances, to the Supreme Court. Foreign investors should be aware that while the civil court system provides formal dispute resolution, judicial efficiency can vary, and predictability depends partly on judicial experience with specific legal issues.

Alternative dispute resolution mechanisms, particularly international commercial arbitration, are widely available and frequently preferred for cross-border disputes. Most significant commercial contracts in Cambodia include arbitration clauses selecting specific arbitration institutions and procedures. International commercial arbitration through institutions including the International Court of Arbitration (ICA), the Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC), the American Arbitration Association (AAA), or the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID—for investor-state disputes) provides neutral, expert dispute resolution outside the Cambodian court system. Arbitration awards are enforceable through the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards, to which Cambodia is a signatory, facilitating enforcement of arbitration awards in Cambodia and other signatory countries.

Mediation and conciliation services are available through governmental and private service providers, offering opportunities for negotiated resolution prior to formal adjudication. The Ministry of Labour and Vocational Training provides mediation services for labor disputes. Private mediation services are offered by international law firms and specialized dispute resolution providers. Negotiated settlement mechanisms, while not creating formal legal precedent, frequently resolve disputes more efficiently and preserve business relationships compared to adversarial litigation or arbitration.

For disputes arising under specific regulatory domains, administrative remedy procedures may be available. Tax disputes, for example, may be appealed through administrative procedures to the General Department of Taxation and subsequently to the Ministry of Economy and Finance prior to litigation. Similarly, intellectual property disputes may involve administrative proceedings before the Department of Intellectual Property or Department of Industrial Property prior to judicial resolution. Foreign investors should understand the administrative remedy procedures available in their specific regulatory domain, as exhaustion of administrative remedies may be a prerequisite to judicial resolution.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Cambodia offers investment opportunities across diverse economic sectors, each with distinct characteristics, growth drivers, and incentive availability. Understanding sectoral opportunities, competitive dynamics, and regulatory environments is essential for investors targeting specific industries.

Manufacturing and Industrial Production remains a cornerstone of Cambodia's investment environment. Garment and footwear manufacturing has historically represented Cambodia's largest industrial sector, supported by competitive labor costs, preferential trade access, and established supply chain relationships. However, the sector faces evolving dynamics as automation, trade policy shifts, and regional competition reshape the garment industry. Investors in garment and footwear manufacturing should recognize both historical competitive advantages and emerging challenges, including pressure from higher-wage competitors and technological change. Food and beverage processing represents a growing manufacturing opportunity, as Cambodia develops value-added production from agricultural commodities. Processors converting rice, fish, cassava, and other agricultural inputs into packaged consumer products, export-quality ingredients, and industrial inputs benefit from raw material availability, labor cost advantages, and downstream market opportunities. Automotive component manufacturing has expanded as global automotive supply chains diversify away from single-source concentrations, with Cambodia offering cost advantages and willing participation in regional automotive ecosystems.

Renewable Energy development represents a priority sector for government investment incentives and private foreign investment. Cambodia has committed to renewable energy targets as part of national climate pledges and energy security objectives. Solar power development, particularly utility-scale solar farms and rooftop installations, benefits from substantial solar irradiance, declining equipment costs, and government incentive frameworks. Hydroelectric power continues to offer opportunities for dam development and power generation, though environmental and social considerations warrant careful planning. Biomass and agricultural waste utilization for energy generation aligns with agricultural development priorities and waste management objectives. Wind power potential exists in specific geographic regions, though development remains early-stage. Renewable energy investors benefit from accelerated depreciation, tax holidays, and exempt import duties for equipment, making renewable energy projects economically attractive.

Tourism and Hospitality represent major sectoral opportunities, with Cambodia's cultural heritage, natural attractions, and recovering tourism flows driving growth. Hotel and resort development, particularly in established destinations (Siem Reap, Sihanoukville, Phnom Penh), responds to rising visitor arrivals and accommodation demand. Ecotourism development in less-developed regions offers opportunities for sustainable tourism investment. Adventure tourism, agritourism, and niche hospitality segments offer specialized opportunities. Tourism-related infrastructure (restaurants, tour operators, travel services) provides complementary investment opportunities. Tourism investors benefit from government support through the Ministry of Tourism and various incentive programs.

Technology and Digital Services present emerging opportunities as Cambodia develops its digital economy. Business process outsourcing (BPO) and software development benefit from labor cost advantages and increasing technical workforce capabilities. Financial technology (fintech) development aligns with financial inclusion objectives and payment system modernization. E-commerce platform development and digital retail services reflect growing consumer internet usage and online shopping adoption. Information technology infrastructure development, including data centers and cloud services, supports Cambodia's digital transformation. Technology investors benefit from intellectual property protections, tax incentives, and government support for digital sector development.

Agribusiness and Agricultural Processing capitalize on Cambodia's agricultural base and export market opportunities. Rice farming, milling, and export represent traditional agricultural focus areas. Cassava processing into starch, ethanol, and other products leverages significant cassava cultivation. Tropical fruit production and processing (mangoes, pineapples, bananas) targets export markets with growing demand. Rubber cultivation and processing maintains historical importance. Agricultural mechanization and technology service provision supports productivity improvements. Agribusiness investors benefit from agricultural incentives, export market access, and land availability.

Infrastructure and Logistics offer substantial opportunities as Cambodia develops transportation networks and logistics capacity. Port facility development and management at Sihanoukville and river ports provides strategic trade infrastructure investment opportunities. Road and rail transportation infrastructure development, including management and operation of toll roads and transport corridors, creates long-term concession and investment opportunities. Warehouse and distribution center development supports growing logistics demand. Special economic zones and industrial parks offer managed infrastructure investment opportunities. Infrastructure investors benefit from government concession frameworks, toll revenue potential, and long-term revenue streams.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

Cambodia's government has established comprehensive incentive frameworks designed to attract foreign investment in priority sectors and promote economic development. Understanding available incentive mechanisms and eligibility requirements is essential for investors seeking to optimize investment returns and reduce operational costs.

Qualified Investment Project (QIP) Status represents the primary mechanism through which Cambodia delivers investment incentives. QIP status provides access to multiple tax and duty benefits, including corporate income tax holidays, import duty exemptions, and tax incentive modifications. QIP projects must be approved by the Council for the Development of Cambodia and registered in the Investment Project Register. Eligibility criteria for QIP status include investment size (typically exceeding USD 10 million or creating more than 500 employment opportunities, though smaller projects in priority sectors may qualify), contribution to employment, technology transfer, export orientation, or regional development objectives. Once QIP status is approved, the project receives significant cost reductions through tax benefits and duty exemptions, substantially enhancing project financial returns.

The Corporate Income Tax Holiday constitutes the primary incentive benefit, with approved QIPs receiving exemption from corporate income tax for periods ranging from five to nine years depending on project characteristics, investment location, and sectoral priority. Projects satisfying specific criteria (minimum USD 30 million investment, 500+ jobs created, advanced technology transfer, domestic resource utilization) may obtain nine-year holidays. Projects in less-developed provinces or targeting strategic sectors receive differential holiday periods based on development priorities. After the tax holiday expires, projects return to normal corporate income tax obligations at the 20 percent standard rate for medium and large taxpayers. The tax holiday benefit is substantial, effectively reducing corporate tax costs to zero during the holiday period and enabling reinvestment of profits or expansion of operations.

Import Duty Exemptions apply to machinery, equipment, and materials essential for QIP implementation. Machinery and equipment imported for project establishment, capital improvements, and replacement of worn or obsolete equipment are exempt from normal import duties when imported during the project's investment period. This exemption substantially reduces capital acquisition costs, as duty rates on machinery and equipment typically range from 5-15 percent of equipment value. Materials and raw materials may also receive duty exemptions when imported as essential project inputs, further reducing operating costs.

Special Economic Zone (SEZ) Incentives provide an alternative framework for investment promotion. SEZs are designated geographic areas with enhanced incentive packages administered by zone management authorities. SEZ incentives typically include nine-year corporate income tax holidays, import duty exemptions on machinery and equipment, simplified administrative procedures for licensing and customs clearance, and dedicated infrastructure support. SEZs provide attractive environments for manufacturing-oriented foreign investment, as enterprises can access standardized facilities, pre-developed infrastructure (power, water, roads, telecommunications), and dedicated administrative support. Cambodia maintains multiple SEZs including Sihanoukville Autonomous Port Authority zones, Phnom Penh Special Economic Zone, and provincial SEZ locations, each offering locational advantages and specialized infrastructure.

Other Incentive Benefits complement the core tax and duty mechanisms. These include exemptions from prepayment of corporate income tax (enabling better cash flow management), exemptions from minimum tax obligations (ensuring companies in loss positions or reduced profitability situations don't face minimum tax burdens), exemptions from export duties on products, and relief on stamp duties on contracts establishing or implementing investment projects. Additionally, the government provides accelerated depreciation schedules for capital assets in specific sectors, enabling faster cost recovery and tax deduction of asset costs.

Sector-Specific Incentive Guidance emphasizes government priorities in renewable energy, telecommunications infrastructure, agribusiness, logistics, and tourism. Renewable energy projects receive prioritized fast-track approval processes, enhanced incentive packages, and long-term power purchase agreement frameworks. High-technology manufacturing projects in specified industries receive enhanced incentive consideration. Regional development objectives prioritize investment in less-developed provinces, with incentive enhancements for projects in designated development areas. Foreign investors should consult with the Council for the Development of Cambodia to confirm sector-specific incentive guidance and project eligibility before finalizing investment decisions.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

Special Economic Zones (SEZs) constitute a cornerstone of Cambodia's investment promotion strategy, providing designated geographic areas with enhanced legal frameworks, infrastructure support, and incentive packages designed to attract manufacturing and logistics investment. Understanding SEZ characteristics, incentive offerings, and operational frameworks is essential for manufacturing-oriented investors.

Characteristics and Benefits of SEZs include dedicated industrial infrastructure (power generation with backup capacity, water supply systems, telecommunications backbone, road networks within zones), streamlined administrative procedures (single-window clearance for business registration, licensing, and customs processing), enhanced security and safety management, dedicated customs facilities enabling efficient import/export of goods, and often transportation logistics facilities (container storage, warehousing, distribution capacity). SEZ development follows international standards, providing facilities comparable to those in developed industrial economies while maintaining cost advantages relative to developed countries.

Sihanoukville Autonomous Port Authority (SAPA) operates Cambodia's most established SEZ complex, located at the Sihanoukville seaport. SAPA SEZs include the original industrial park, expanded zones, and specialized facilities for port-dependent logistics and manufacturing. Location at a deep-water seaport provides direct shipping access for export-oriented manufacturers and reduces logistics costs for international trade. SAPA SEZs have attracted diverse manufacturers including garment producers, food processors, packaging companies, and light manufacturers leveraging port proximity for export efficiency.

Phnom Penh Special Economic Zone (PPSEOZ) located on the outskirts of Phnom Penh provides access to the capital city's labor, supplier, and logistics advantages. Proximity to Phnom Penh enables enterprises to access qualified workforce, government services, international business services (accounting, legal, consulting), banking facilities, and consumer markets. PPSEOZ development has attracted technology enterprises, food processing facilities, manufacturing, and business support services.

Provincial Special Economic Zones located in Kampong Chhnang, Kampong Speu, Svay Rieng, Bavet, Ream, and other provincial locations provide opportunities for investment in less-developed regions while maintaining infrastructure and incentive support. Provincial SEZs offer lower land costs relative to Phnom Penh or Sihanoukville, proximity to specific resource bases (agricultural areas, timber resources), or strategic border locations. Provincial SEZ development supports government objectives of regional economic development and employment generation in less-developed areas.

Incentive Packages Available in SEZs include nine-year corporate income tax holidays from the date of commencement of commercial operations, import duty exemptions on machinery and equipment used in SEZ enterprises, simplified customs procedures for import and export goods, reduced licensing requirements compared to off-zone locations, and often preferential treatment for utility services (power, water, telecommunications). Enterprises operating in SEZs maintain access to government incentives available for QIP status, subject to integration of SEZ-specific benefits, creating comprehensive incentive packages.

SEZ Operating Frameworks require enterprises to maintain operations within designated SEZ boundaries, comply with SEZ-specific regulations administered by zone management authorities, maintain documentation of inputs and outputs for customs purposes, and comply with special export control requirements for certain products. Labor regulations, tax obligations, and corporate law generally apply within SEZs, though simplified procedures are available for specified administrative processes. Foreign ownership is permitted in SEZs with the same liberal treatment available elsewhere in Cambodia.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Cambodia's integration into international trade agreements and regional economic communities provides preferential market access for Cambodian exports while offering foreign investors access to expanded markets through Cambodia-based operations. Understanding trade agreement benefits and utilization requirements is important for export-oriented manufacturers and service providers.

ASEAN Membership and Free Trade Area (AFTA) provides Cambodia with preferential tariff access to markets throughout Southeast Asia. The ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA) Common Effective Preferential Tariff (CEPT) scheme provides zero or near-zero tariff access for most product categories when traded between ASEAN members. This preferential treatment substantially reduces tariff barriers for Cambodia-based exporters selling to other ASEAN economies, enhancing the competitiveness of Cambodian products and the attractiveness of Cambodia as a manufacturing base for regional distribution. Manufacturers targeting regional markets benefit from AFTA preferential treatment, which reduces their product costs and enhances market competitiveness relative to non-ASEAN sources. The ASEAN Economic Community (AEC) framework further facilitates movement of goods, services, investment, and professional workers within the region, creating an integrated market of over 600 million consumers.

Bilateral Trade Agreements supplement ASEAN arrangements. Cambodia maintains bilateral free trade or preferential trade agreements with major trading partners including China, Japan, South Korea, and others. These bilateral agreements provide preferential tariff rates for Cambodian exports to signatory countries, supporting Cambodia-based exporters. For example, Cambodia's preferential agreement with China provides tariff reductions on agricultural products, textiles, and other Cambodian exports, supporting export competitiveness. Investors in Cambodia-based export-oriented operations should review applicable bilateral agreements to determine tariff benefits available for their products.

Generalized System of Preferences (GSP) provides Cambodia with preferential tariff access to developed countries including the United States, European Union, and others. GSP status eliminates or substantially reduces tariffs for eligible Cambodian products exported to GSP-granting countries, benefiting Cambodia-based exporters in textile and apparel, agricultural products, and other categories. However, GSP benefits are subject to compliance with labor standards, environmental protections, and other eligibility requirements, which warrant attention from investors in GSP-benefiting sectors.

Everything But Arms (EBA) Status with the European Union provides Cambodia with duty-free, quota-free access to the EU market for all products except arms and munitions. EBA status represents Cambodia's most valuable preferential trade arrangement, providing duty-free access to one of the world's largest consumer markets (450+ million consumers) for virtually all products. EBA benefits support Cambodia-based exports of garments, footwear, agricultural products, and other categories to the EU market. However, EBA benefits are contingent on compliance with international labor standards, human rights conventions, and rule of origin requirements, creating obligations for enterprises utilizing EBA benefits.

Rules of Origin Requirements apply across preferential trade agreements, requiring that products meet specified domestic content, processing, or value-addition thresholds to qualify for preferential treatment. Rules of origin for textile and apparel products typically require that fabrics be produced in the exporting country (yarn-forward requirements) to obtain preferential treatment. Manufacturers must understand applicable rules of origin when planning production and sourcing strategies, as failure to meet rules of origin requirements results in loss of preferential tariff treatment.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Cambodia maintains a modern banking and financial system that supports business operations, working capital financing, investment capital mobilization, and international trade transactions. The National Bank of Cambodia regulates the banking sector and maintains regulatory oversight ensuring financial system stability and sound banking practices.

Foreign Exchange Regime and Currency Convertibility in Cambodia is exceptionally liberal, with no restrictions on foreign exchange transactions, unrestricted foreign exchange conversions, no taxation on foreign exchange conversions, and full convertibility of the Cambodian Riel. This open foreign exchange policy enables businesses to manage currency exposure, repatriate profits, and conduct international transactions without foreign exchange restrictions. The stable exchange rate close to 4,000 KHR per USD since the early 2000s provides currency predictability and reduces exchange rate risk for long-term business planning. The highly dollarized economy, where the US dollar functions alongside the Cambodian Riel as a primary medium of exchange and store of value, further reduces currency uncertainty and facilitates business transactions in a familiar currency.

Banking Sector Structure and Services includes multiple commercial banks, development banks, microfinance institutions, and financial service providers. International banks including CIMB, DBS Bank, Bank of Tokyo-Mitsubishi UFJ, and others operate in Cambodia, providing international-standard banking services. Domestic commercial banks including Canadia Bank, Acleda Bank, and others provide full-service commercial banking. Foreign banks may establish subsidiary operations or branches in Cambodia, with 100 percent foreign ownership of banking subsidiaries permitted. Banks licensed to operate in Cambodia are authorized to conduct credit operations, collect deposits, provide payment and settlement services, process transactions in both Cambodian Riel and US dollars, and support international trade transactions.

Business Credit and Working Capital Financing are available from commercial banks through conventional lending mechanisms including term loans, revolving credit facilities, asset-based lending, and equipment financing. Banks provide short-term working capital financing to support inventory acquisition, accounts receivable management, and operational cash flow. Medium-term credit facilities support capital equipment acquisition and operational expansion. Banks typically require security interests in business assets or personal guarantees and conduct credit analysis based on business financial statements, cash flow projections, and borrower creditworthiness. Interest rates on commercial credit typically range from 8-12 percent annually depending on loan size, security, and borrower credit quality. Foreign investors should establish banking relationships early in business establishment to ensure credit availability for operational needs.

Trade Finance Services support international import and export transactions. Banks provide documentary credit (letters of credit), guarantees for performance obligations, trade credit insurance, and international payment services. Letters of credit issued by reputable Cambodian banks and confirmed by foreign correspondent banks facilitate international trade by providing payment security to exporters and financing to importers. Export credit financing supports Cambodian exporters' working capital needs prior to customer payment. Trade credit insurance protects against customer default risk and currency fluctuation for international transactions. These trade finance services facilitate Cambodia-based exporters' market access and support import-oriented manufacturers' supply chain management.

Capital Mobilization and Investment Financing services support investment project financing through medium and long-term credit facilities. Banks provide project financing for manufacturing facilities, infrastructure development, real estate projects, and other capital-intensive initiatives. Project financing typically involves credit analysis of project cash flows and security interests in project assets. Syndicated financing for larger projects enables multiple banks to participate in credit provision. Banks also facilitate capital mobilization through securities issuance, with some Cambodian banks offering investment banking and securities services. The Cambodian Securities Exchange provides access to equity capital markets, though institutional depth remains developing.

Digital Banking and Payment Systems have expanded significantly, with mobile banking, online payment platforms, and digital wallet services increasingly available. The National Bank of Cambodia has promoted digital financial inclusion and digital payment infrastructure modernization. Digital banking services enable customers to conduct transactions through mobile applications and online platforms, enhancing convenience and reducing dependence on branch-based transactions. Digital payment systems facilitate business-to-business and business-to-consumer payments, supporting e-commerce and reducing cash dependency. Foreign investors should ensure familiarity with digital banking capabilities available through their chosen banking partners.

4. Business Organisations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Cambodia's legal framework, established through the Law on Commercial Enterprises, authorizes multiple organizational structures for business operations, each with distinct characteristics, liability frameworks, governance requirements, and suitability for different business purposes.

Limited Companies represent the most commonly utilized business structure for foreign investment in Cambodia. Limited companies are divided into two categories: Private Limited Companies and Public Limited Companies. Private Limited Companies may have between 2 and 30 shareholders and are suitable for the vast majority of commercial ventures, including subsidiary operations of foreign parent companies, joint ventures, and Cambodian business operations of foreign individuals. Notably, a single foreign investor may establish a "Single Member Limited Company," a specialized form of private limited company, permitting sole foreign ownership without requirement for additional shareholders. The key characteristic distinguishing limited companies is limited liability, whereby shareholders' liability for company debts and obligations is restricted to the extent of their capital contribution. Limited liability protection provides personal asset protection, enabling shareholders to conduct business through the company while protecting personal assets from business creditors' claims.

Public Limited Companies are authorized to issue securities to the public and are subject to enhanced regulatory requirements regarding disclosure, governance, and shareholder protections. Public limited companies are appropriate for larger enterprises planning significant capital mobilization or eventual public listings on the Cambodian Securities Exchange. Both limited company forms require comprehensive articles of incorporation establishing the company's structure, governance procedures, shareholder rights, dividend policies, and operational parameters. Limited companies must maintain registered offices in Cambodia, keep statutory records including shareholder registers and minutes of shareholder meetings, conduct annual shareholder meetings (or shareholder resolutions in lieu of meetings), and file annual financial statements with the Ministry of Commerce. The limited liability structure combined with institutional continuity, flexibility in governance, and established legal precedents makes limited companies the preferred vehicle for foreign direct investment in Cambodia.

General Partnerships and Limited Partnerships provide alternative organizational structures particularly suitable for smaller businesses where personal relationships and active management are central. In a General Partnership, all partners are authorized to manage day-to-day operations and personally guarantee the partnership's obligations, bearing unlimited personal liability for partnership debts. General partnerships are simple to establish and maintain, involve minimal administrative costs, and permit flexible profit sharing and management authority allocation. However, because general partners assume unlimited personal liability, the structure exposes partners' personal assets to business creditors. This liability exposure makes general partnerships most appropriate for service-based businesses, professional practices, and ventures where personal liability risk is manageable or where liability insurance is available.

Limited Partnerships require at least one general partner (bearing unlimited personal liability and administrative responsibility) combined with one or more limited partners who contribute capital but do not participate in management. Limited partners' liability is restricted to the extent of capital contribution, providing passive capital investors with liability protection while permitting their participation in the enterprise. This structure enables capital investment by individuals unwilling to assume unlimited personal liability while maintaining operational authority with actively managing general partners. Partnership agreements should clearly delineate management responsibilities, profit-sharing percentages, capital contribution requirements, decision-making procedures, and procedures for admitting new partners or withdrawing partners. Partnerships are less complex than corporations and may offer greater tax flexibility, though liability exposure for general partners and practical governance challenges warrant careful consideration.

Sole Proprietorships enable a single natural person to establish and operate an enterprise in their personal name. Sole proprietorships involve minimal registration requirements and administrative obligations compared to corporate structures, providing flexibility in operations and profit distribution. However, sole proprietorships do not provide liability protection; the proprietor personally bears all business debts, liabilities, and legal obligations. Personal assets are exposed to business creditors, and the proprietor's personal liability is unlimited. Sole proprietorship is appropriate for service-based businesses (consulting, accounting, legal services), small retail operations, and ventures where the individual owner's personal skills and reputation provide primary business value. Foreign nationals and foreign-registered entities cannot establish sole proprietorships in Cambodia, as sole proprietorship requires personal ownership by a natural person. Foreign business persons may establish sole proprietorships if holding Cambodian residence status, though most foreign investors prefer corporate structures providing liability protection and institutional continuity.

Branches of Foreign Companies represent a legal structure enabling a foreign company to establish and conduct operations in Cambodia without creating a separate legal entity. A branch is a representative office of the foreign parent company, not constituting an independent legal entity. Branches may conduct revenue-generating business activities (unlike representative offices, which cannot) but do not provide liability protection, as the parent company remains responsible for all branch debts and obligations. The branch structure is appropriate for foreign companies intending to conduct business in Cambodia while maintaining centralized control and accounting in the parent company. Registration and compliance procedures for branches are slightly simplified compared to establishing a subsidiary, though tax obligations and regulatory compliance requirements generally apply. Foreign companies establishing branches must designate a branch manager, maintain a registered office in Cambodia, and file branch financial statements with the Ministry of Commerce.

Representative Offices and Liaison Offices provide limited-scope operational structures for preliminary market exploration, liaison functions, and business development activities without comprehensive operational commitment. Representative offices may conduct market research, client relationship development, coordination functions, and liaison activities but cannot directly conduct revenue-generating business activities. This structure is appropriate for foreign companies in preliminary market assessment phases, seeking to establish presence without full operational commitment. Representative office registration is simplified compared to subsidiary or branch registration, reducing administrative burden and establishing minimal presence while testing market conditions.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Establishing a limited company in Cambodia involves a registration process administered by the Ministry of Commerce's Department of Business Registration. The process has been substantially streamlined through introduction of online registration systems and simplified procedures, enabling foreign investors to establish companies within several days.

Preliminary Procedures involve selection of a company name that complies with naming conventions (not misleading, not identical to existing companies, in Khmer characters with optional English translation), identification of a registered office location within Cambodia, and preparation of articles of incorporation. The registered office must be a physical location in Cambodia where the company maintains statutory records and receives official correspondence, though the office need not be the principal business location. Articles of incorporation are the foundational governance document establishing the company's structure, shareholder rights, director authority, dividend policies, and operational parameters.

Documentation Requirements for company registration include the completed online registration application form, articles of incorporation (in Khmer language with English translation), identification documents for all shareholders (passports for foreign nationals, national ID or equivalent for Cambodian citizens), identification and authorization documents for directors, and proof of paid-up capital. Capital contribution may be made in cash (deposited with a commercial bank and evidenced through bank statements) or through contribution of assets (valued by the company and documented in the articles of incorporation). The minimum capital requirement for a private limited company is KHR 1 million (approximately USD 250) per shareholder, an extremely minimal threshold enabling business establishment with minimal capital commitment.

Online Registration Process through the Department of Business Registration proceeds as follows: (1) submission of required documents through the online registration portal with payment of registration fees (approximately USD 35-50 for a private limited company); (2) document review by Ministry of Commerce staff to ensure compliance with requirements; (3) issuance of a Certificate of Incorporation upon approval, which constitutes formal recognition of the company's legal existence; and (4) optional registration with provincial authorities for tax identification and VAT registration purposes. The online registration process typically completes within 1-3 business days, enabling rapid company establishment.

Post-Registration Procedures include obtaining a tax identification number from the General Department of Taxation (for companies engaging in taxable activities), registering for VAT if expected annual turnover exceeds the KHR 250 million threshold, obtaining business licenses for regulated activities (if applicable), registering with the Ministry of Labour and Vocational Training if employing workers, and establishing business bank accounts. Foreign companies proposing to utilize government incentives must separately apply for Qualified Investment Project (QIP) status with the Council for the Development of Cambodia, submitting detailed investment project plans, capital commitment documentation, and employment projections.

Costs and Timelines for company registration are modest and favorable compared to many jurisdictions. Registration fees are approximately USD 35-50, with minimal additional costs for document preparation and professional assistance. Registration timelines of 1-3 business days enable rapid business establishment, facilitating swift market entry for foreign investors. Professional assistance from Cambodian law firms or corporate service providers is available and advisable, with typical costs of USD 200-500 for full establishment services including document preparation, registration, and post-registration compliance.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

Cambodia's legal framework establishes mandatory governance requirements for limited companies, establishing structural protections for shareholders and creditors while defining management authority and accountability mechanisms.

Shareholder Meetings represent the primary governance mechanism through which shareholders exercise ownership authority. Annual shareholder meetings are mandatory, held within 120 days following the close of the company's financial year, to approve annual financial statements, elect directors, declare dividends, and address other shareholder matters. Meetings may be held physically or, increasingly, through virtual means. Shareholder resolutions approving specified matters (annual financial statements, director elections, major transactions, amendment of articles of incorporation) require approval by shareholders holding majority voting power (50% or more of shares). Amendments to articles of incorporation and fundamental changes to company operations require higher approval thresholds (typically 75% of voting power) as specified in the articles of incorporation.

Directors and Management Authority are established through the articles of incorporation, which define director qualifications, appointment procedures, term lengths, and authority boundaries. Each company must have at least one director, who may be a natural person of any nationality and is not required to be a Cambodian resident. Multiple directors form a board of directors, which meets periodically to address strategic and operational matters. Directors owe fiduciary duties to the company and shareholders, requiring that they act in the company's best interest, avoid conflicts of interest, and exercise reasonable care in management decisions. Directors may be held personally liable for breaches of fiduciary duty or violation of law. Regular director meetings document the company's management and governance, with meeting minutes maintained as statutory records.

Financial Reporting and Accounting are governed by Cambodian accounting standards and tax regulations. Companies must maintain accounting records documenting all financial transactions, with records retained for minimum five years. Annual financial statements must be prepared within 120 days of the financial year's close and filed with the Ministry of Commerce. Financial statements must include a balance sheet, income statement, and statement of changes in equity, prepared in accordance with Cambodian accounting principles. For medium and large taxpayers, annual financial statements must be audited by licensed auditors. Small taxpayers may be exempt from audit requirements if turnover or assets remain below specified thresholds.

Dividend Distributions are authorized through shareholder resolutions approving the allocation of profits. Companies may distribute dividends from retained earnings or current year profits, but cannot distribute capital or make distributions if the company is insolvent or would become insolvent through the distribution. Dividend distributions to foreign shareholders are subject to withholding tax at applicable rates (typically 10% for most categories of income), with the company responsible for remitting withheld taxes.

Dispute Resolution and Minority Shareholder Protections enable shareholders to challenge director decisions or seek remedies for breaches of fiduciary duty through litigation or arbitration. The articles of incorporation may establish arbitration procedures for internal company disputes, providing faster, more private resolution than litigation. Minority shareholders may petition the courts for remedies including director removal, company dissolution, or appointment of provisional management in cases of director misconduct or abuse of authority.

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Cambodia's Labour Law establishes the legal framework governing employment relationships, working conditions, compensation, benefits, dispute resolution, and employer obligations. Understanding labour law requirements is essential for any enterprise with employees.

Employment Relationship Definition encompasses all categories of workers including full-time employees, part-time employees, fixed-term contract workers, and domestic workers. Employment relationships are established through employment contracts or understood agreements between employer and worker. Employment contracts are advised to reduce disputes and establish clear terms, including position description, compensation, benefits, working hours, duration (for fixed-term contracts), and termination provisions. While oral contracts are legally recognized, written contracts significantly facilitate dispute resolution and demonstrate employer compliance with formal requirements.

Working Hours and Conditions are established at 48 hours per week as the legal maximum, typically organized as 8 hours per day. Overtime work beyond 48 hours is permitted with additional compensation at rates of 150% of regular wages for overtime, 200% for night work, and 300% for Sunday and holiday work. The Labour Law establishes mandatory provisions regarding workplace health and safety, rest days (minimum one day per week, typically Sunday), annual paid leave (minimum 1.5 days per month or 18 days annually), and paid public holidays (minimum 11 days per year).

Minimum Wage Requirements vary by sector. The garment, textile, footwear, travel products, and bags manufacturing sectors are subject to sector-specific minimum wage established annually by the Ministry of Labour and Vocational Training. Effective January 1, 2026, the minimum wage in these sectors is USD 210 per month for regular workers and USD 208 for probationary workers. In addition to base minimum wage, covered sector workers are entitled to mandatory allowances including USD 7 monthly transportation bonus, USD 10 attendance bonus, USD 0.50 daily meal subsidy, and seniority bonuses ranging from USD 2-11 monthly based on tenure. These mandatory allowances increase overall labor costs substantially beyond the base minimum wage.

Other economic sectors not covered by specific sector minimum wage requirements apply the general minimum wage determined by provincial authorities or negotiated between employers and workers. The Labour Law establishes a framework requiring minimum compensation sufficient to meet workers' basic living standards and maintaining that employers cannot unilaterally reduce wages below agreed levels.

Social Security and Employer Contributions require employers to contribute to the National Social Security Fund (NSSF) at rates established annually (currently approximately 10.5% of wages for employer contribution, plus worker contributions withheld from wages). These contributions cover social insurance, health insurance, and employment injury protection. Employers are responsible for remitting both employer and employee contributions monthly to the NSSF, with non-compliance resulting in penalties and potential legal action.

Termination and Severance Requirements are established through the Labour Law and employment contracts. Employers may terminate employees for "just cause" (serious misconduct, repeated violations, incompetence) without severance obligations. Termination without just cause requires severance compensation based on tenure: 10 days' wages for less than six months' service, 30 days' wages for six months to two years' service, and 60 days' wages for more than two years' service. During the probationary period (typically up to three months), either party may terminate the employment relationship without severance.

Dispute Resolution for labour disputes occurs through conciliation and arbitration mechanisms administered by the Ministry of Labour and Vocational Training. Labour disputes are typically addressed through conciliation procedures before the Labour Dispute Resolution Council, with arbitration available if conciliation fails. Workplace committees, where applicable, provide initial dispute resolution forums. Employees retain rights to pursue labour dispute remedies and cannot be disciplined or dismissed for submitting claims or complaints regarding labour law violations.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Cambodia has established a legal framework for intellectual property protection administered through multiple government departments and aligned with international standards. The framework includes mechanisms for protecting trademarks, patents, copyrights, and industrial designs.

Trademark Protection is governed by the Law on Marks, Trade Names and Acts of Unfair Competition, enacted in 2002. Trademarks are protected through a registration system administered by the Department of Intellectual Property under the Ministry of Commerce. Cambodia operates a "first-to-file" trademark system, meaning that priority rights are assigned to the first person to file a trademark application, regardless of prior use. Trademark applications are examined for absolute grounds of refusal (non-distinctive marks, deceptive marks, offensive marks) and registered marks are published for potential opposition by third parties. Registered trademarks receive protection against infringement through administrative remedies (cease-and-desist, cancellation proceedings) and judicial remedies (injunctions, damages). Trademark registrations remain valid for 10 years from the date of registration and are renewable indefinitely for successive 10-year periods upon payment of renewal fees.

Patent Protection is governed by the Law on Patents, Utility Model Certificates and Industrial Design, promulgated in 2003. The law provides for patent protection for inventions, utility model certificates for minor inventions, and industrial design registrations. Patents are granted for inventions satisfying requirements of novelty (not previously disclosed), non-obviousness (not evident from prior knowledge), and industrial applicability (capable of being manufactured or used in industry). Patent prosecution involves application filing, examination by the Department of Industrial Property under the Ministry of Industry, Science, Technology and Innovation, and issuance of patents upon approval. Patents are initially granted for a 20-year term from the filing date, subject to continued payment of maintenance fees. Cambodia has entered into bilateral agreements with the United States Patent and Trademark Office enabling expedited patent grant for applications with corresponding US patents.

Copyright Protection is governed by the Law on Copyright and Related Rights. Copyright protections are automatic upon creation of literary, artistic, musical, and other creative works; registration is not required for copyright protection to exist. Copyright works are protected against reproduction, distribution, public performance, and derivative work creation. Copyright protection extends for the author's lifetime plus 50 years following death (or 50 years from publication for works of corporate authorship). Cambodian copyright law provides remedies for infringement including injunctions, damages, seizure of infringing materials, and criminal penalties in cases of commercial infringement.

Industrial Design Protection is available through registration with the Department of Industrial Property. Industrial designs protecting the visual appearance of products (shapes, patterns, surface designs) are protected through registration lasting 15 years. Design registration requires that designs be novel and non-obvious relative to prior disclosed designs.

Enforcement Mechanisms include administrative enforcement through intellectual property departments, judicial enforcement through civil courts, and customs enforcement through border authorities. In 2024, Cambodia established the Intellectual Property Rights Recordation System enabling trademark, geographical indication, and copyright owners to record their intellectual property rights with customs authorities for direct access by border enforcement personnel. This system streamlines customs interventions against counterfeit goods and parallel imports, enabling rapid identification and seizure of infringing products at borders. While Cambodia's intellectual property enforcement environment continues developing, the combination of statutory protections, international agreements, enhanced customs procedures, and judicial remedies provides meaningful protection for registered intellectual property. Foreign investors should register intellectual property promptly upon business establishment and consider customs recordation to maximize enforcement effectiveness.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Cambodia's tax system is administered through the General Department of Taxation under the Ministry of Economy and Finance and is governed by the Law on Taxation and related tax legislation. The system comprises corporate income tax, personal income tax, value-added tax, withholding taxes, excise duties, import duties, stamp duties, and various other taxes. The tax system is designed to generate government revenue while promoting investment and economic growth through incentive mechanisms for priority sectors. Understanding tax structure, rates, compliance procedures, and incentive availability is essential for business planning and operational compliance.

Taxpayer Classification divides companies into categories for tax administration purposes. Large and medium taxpayers are those exceeding specified asset or revenue thresholds (typically annual turnover exceeding KHR 2 billion or assets exceeding KHR 1 billion), subject to standard corporate income tax rates and comprehensive compliance requirements. Small taxpayers fall below these thresholds and are subject to simplified taxation regimes and reduced compliance burdens. Different compliance procedures, filing deadlines, and audit procedures apply to large and small taxpayers, with simplified requirements for small taxpayers reducing administrative burden for smaller enterprises.

Tax Residency and Sourcing Rules establish that resident taxpayers are subject to taxation on worldwide income (income from all sources, regardless of source country), while non-residents are taxed only on Cambodia-sourced income. A company is considered a Cambodian resident if it is incorporated under Cambodian law or if it is managed and controlled from Cambodia (indicating central management location in Cambodia). These residency and sourcing rules have significant implications for foreign-sourced income, repatriated profits, and international taxation planning.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

Corporate income tax (CIT) applies to companies engaging in taxable business activities and earning assessable income within Cambodia. The tax is administered through annual corporate income tax filings by March 31 following the year of assessment.

CIT Rates for Standard Taxpayers are established at 20 percent of assessable profits for large and medium taxpayers. This rate applies to most business sectors. However, specialized industries are subject to higher rates: oil and gas extraction and certain mineral exploitation activities are subject to 30 percent CIT, reflecting the capital-intensive nature and significant government revenue interests in resource extraction. Insurance companies are taxed at 5 percent on gross premium income plus 20 percent on other income from non-insurance activities. The standard 20 percent rate is competitive with regional comparators and significantly lower than rates in developed economies, providing incentive for foreign investment.

Small Taxpayer Progressive Rates provide graduated taxation for smaller enterprises, with rates from 0 to 20 percent applied based on income levels. Specific progressive brackets establish lower rates for lower income levels, providing tax relief for smaller businesses. The progressive structure recognizes that smaller enterprises have fewer resources to bear tax burdens and encourages small business development. Once a small taxpayer exceeds the thresholds for medium/large taxpayer classification, standard 20 percent rates apply.

Assessable Income Calculation commences with total gross income from business operations, including revenue from business activities, rental income, interest income, and other income sources. From gross income, allowable deductions are subtracted including ordinary business expenses (materials, labor, utilities, rent), depreciation of capital assets, interest on business loans, bad debt provisions, and other business-related expenditures. Allowable deductions must be ordinary and necessary expenses incurred to generate business income, documented with supporting evidence, and not prohibited by tax law.

Depreciation Methods enable businesses to deduct the cost of capital assets over their useful lives. The law permits either straight-line depreciation (equal deduction each year) or declining-balance depreciation (larger deductions in earlier years). Specific asset classes have prescribed useful lives and depreciation methods: buildings typically 20 years, machinery 5-10 years, vehicles 5 years, and other assets as prescribed. Depreciation deductions reduce taxable income and improve cash flow during capital asset acquisition periods. Qualified Investment Projects may receive accelerated depreciation enabling faster cost recovery.

Capital Gains Taxation applies to gains from sale of capital assets. Capital gains are generally included in taxable income and subject to standard CIT rates. However, effective September 1, 2025, Cambodia implemented a capital gains tax (CGT) at a flat rate of 20 percent on gains from leases, securities, intellectual property, business goodwill, and foreign currency transactions. Effective January 1, 2026, capital gains tax extends to real estate transactions, applying a 20 percent rate to gains from real property dispositions. The capital gains tax creates a separate taxation regime for specified asset categories.

Loss Carryforwards enable companies experiencing losses in a tax year to offset losses against profits in subsequent years, typically over a five-year period. This provision prevents taxation of businesses during startup or development phases and reflects that business profitability fluctuates. Loss carryforwards improve cash flow management for growing businesses and reduce tax burdens as profitability is achieved.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

Personal income tax applies to individuals earning income from employment, business operations, or other sources within Cambodia. The system is administered through annual tax filings with the General Department of Taxation.

PIT Residency Rules establish that resident individuals (those with indefinite residence in Cambodia or residing in Cambodia 183 or more days in any calendar year) are subject to taxation on worldwide income, while non-residents are taxed only on Cambodia-sourced income. Foreign workers employed in Cambodia are typically classified as non-residents and subject to tax only on Cambodia-sourced employment income.

Employment Income Taxation is subject to progressive tax rates with higher rates applying to higher income levels. Standard progressive brackets are established through regulation, with specific rates determined annually. Employment income includes salary, wages, bonuses, and benefits provided by employers. The taxation system recognizes that employment income is earned income requiring personal effort and skill, distinct from passive income or investment returns.

Self-Employment and Business Income earned by sole proprietors or partners are subject to progressive taxation on net business income (total revenue less deductible business expenses). Self-employed individuals report business income annually and are subject to applicable progressive rates. Deductible expenses include ordinary business expenses, depreciation of business assets, employee wages, and other business-related expenditures. Self-employed individuals have greater flexibility in income structuring compared to employees but also carry greater compliance burdens and personal liability.

Tax Incentives and Relief for Foreign Workers may be available under bilateral tax treaties (Double Taxation Agreements—DTAs) between Cambodia and the worker's home country. DTAs often provide relief mechanisms preventing simultaneous taxation by Cambodia and the home country, including foreign earned income exclusions or tax credits reducing home country taxation. Foreign workers should consult qualified tax advisors regarding treaty benefits available based on their home country, as proper treaty utilization can substantially reduce combined tax burdens.

Personal Deductions are permitted for certain categories of personal expenses, including medical expenses, charitable contributions, and other specified items. Standard deductions are available, representing minimum necessary expenses recognized by the tax system.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

Value Added Tax (VAT) is Cambodia's primary indirect tax, assessed at 10 percent on the taxable value of supplies of goods and services within Cambodia. VAT is a consumption-based tax applied through the production, distribution, and retail chain, with intermediate businesses claiming input VAT credits for taxes paid on business inputs. The 0 percent VAT rate applies to exports of goods and services provided outside Cambodia, creating export neutrality and supporting export-oriented industries by removing VAT from export cost structures. VAT registration is required for businesses with annual turnover exceeding KHR 250 million (approximately USD 61,700). VAT-registered businesses must file monthly VAT returns by the 20th of the following month and remit VAT to the General Department of Taxation.

Import Duties and Tariffs apply to goods imported into Cambodia based on the Cambodian Trade Tariff Schedule, which follows the Harmonized Tariff System classification methodology. Duty rates vary by product category, typically ranging from 0 to 40 percent, with most industrial goods taxed at 0-15 percent and consumer products at higher rates. Qualified Investment Projects receive exemptions or reductions on import duties for capital equipment, machinery, and specified raw materials essential to project implementation. Import duty relief substantially reduces capital acquisition costs for manufacturing operations.

Excise Duties apply to selected goods including alcoholic beverages, tobacco products, and fuel. Excise tax rates vary by product, designed to discourage consumption of specified goods or generate government revenue from specific product categories. Effective April 1, 2024, Cambodia introduced a Tax on Personal Luxury (TPL) at 5 percent on supplies of alcoholic and tobacco products, supplementing standard VAT rates for these product categories. Excise and luxury taxes increase the effective tax rate on specified consumer products.

Stamp Duty applies to certain legal documents and transactions including contracts, deeds, agreements, and powers of attorney. Stamp duty rates vary based on transaction type and value, typically ranging from 0.1 to 4.8 percent of transaction value or a fixed amount based on document type. Investment project documentation may be exempted from stamp duty as an investment incentive benefit. Stamp duty compliance requires application of the appropriate duty rate, attachment of duty stamps or electronic recording of payment, and submission to tax authorities on specified documents.

Real Property Taxes apply to land and building ownership, assessed by municipal authorities and levied annually. Tax rates vary by property category and location, typically ranging from 0.1 to 0.5 percent of assessed property value. Property taxes provide ongoing revenue for local government and must be paid annually by property owners. Special Economic Zones and priority investment projects may receive property tax relief or exemptions as incentive benefits.

Vehicle Registration and Annual Taxes apply to automobiles, motorcycles, and other vehicles. Registration taxes are assessed at the time of vehicle registration, while annual vehicle taxes are assessed based on vehicle type and engine capacity. These taxes support road infrastructure maintenance and fund transportation agencies. Vehicle taxes are typically paid through vehicle licensing authorities.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

Qualified Investment Project (QIP) Incentives provide the primary mechanism through which Cambodia delivers investment tax benefits. QIP approval triggers multiple incentive benefits as discussed in Section 3.2.

Special Economic Zone (SEZ) Tax Incentives provide enhanced incentive packages for enterprises operating within designated SEZs, including nine-year corporate income tax holidays, import duty exemptions, and reduced stamp duties on SEZ-specific transactions. SEZ incentives supplement QIP benefits and create comprehensive incentive packages for manufacturing and logistics enterprises.

Sector-Specific Incentives emphasize government priorities in renewable energy, telecommunications, agribusiness, and tourism. These sectors receive expedited approval processes, enhanced depreciation allowances, and extended tax holiday periods. Investors in priority sectors should confirm sector-specific incentive guidance with the Council for the Development of Cambodia before finalizing investment commitments.

Investment Promotion Law Provisions provide additional incentive mechanisms including exemptions from prepayment of corporate income tax (improving cash flow management), exemptions from minimum tax obligations (protecting companies in loss or reduced profitability positions), exemptions from export duties on products, relief on stamp duties related to investment project establishment, and accelerated depreciation for capital assets in specific sectors.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Double Taxation Agreements (DTAs) between Cambodia and other countries provide relief mechanisms preventing simultaneous taxation by both jurisdictions. Cambodia has entered into DTAs with numerous countries including Australia, Belgium, Canada, China, Denmark, France, Germany, Hong Kong, India, Japan, Laos, Malaysia, Netherlands, Norway, Singapore, South Korea, Spain, Thailand, United Kingdom, United States, Vietnam, and others. These bilateral agreements typically provide:

  • Dividend Withholding Rate Reductions: Non-resident recipients of dividend payments may be entitled to withholding rate reductions from the standard 14 percent down to 10 percent or lower, depending on specific treaty provisions and shareholding percentages.

  • Interest and Royalty Withholding Reductions: Payments of interest on loans and royalties on intellectual property may be subject to reduced withholding rates (typically 10 percent) compared to the standard 14 percent rate for non-residents.

  • Foreign Earned Income Exclusions: Foreign employees working in Cambodia may be entitled to exclude specified amounts of foreign earned income from Cambodia taxation, based on treaty provisions and home country tax law.

  • Tax Credits: Double taxation agreements typically provide for tax credits, enabling taxpayers to claim credits in their home country for taxes paid to Cambodia, preventing simultaneous full taxation by both jurisdictions.

Foreign investors should review applicable bilateral tax treaty provisions with Cambodia to understand withholding rate benefits, foreign earned income treatment, and tax credit availability, as proper treaty utilization can substantially reduce international tax burdens.

Transfer Pricing Requirements apply to transactions between related entities (parent companies, subsidiaries, branches, and other affiliates). The Cambodia Tax Law incorporates transfer pricing principles requiring that related-party transactions be priced according to arm's length standards (the prices and terms that would apply between unrelated parties). Transfer pricing documentation is required to support that related-party pricing complies with arm's length principles. Failure to maintain proper transfer pricing documentation and ensure compliance with arm's length standards exposes businesses to transfer pricing adjustments and penalties.

Permanent Establishment (PE) Exposure is relevant for foreign companies conducting business in Cambodia without establishing formal subsidiaries. A foreign company is considered to have a permanent establishment in Cambodia if it maintains a fixed place of business (office, branch, manufacturing facility), has authority to bind the foreign company in Cambodia (through employees or agents), or maintains a dependent agent conducting business on the company's behalf. PE status triggers Cambodia tax obligations for the foreign company's Cambodia-sourced income, withholding tax obligations on payments from Cambodia operations, and potential worldwide income taxation if the PE is managed from Cambodia. Foreign companies should structure Cambodia operations carefully to understand PE implications and tax obligations.

5.7 Tax Compliance Snapshot

Key tax compliance obligations for businesses in Cambodia include:

  1. Corporate Income Tax Registration and Annual Filing: Companies engaging in business activities must register with the General Department of Taxation and file annual corporate income tax returns by March 31 following the year of assessment. Filing is required regardless of whether the company is profitable, with loss carryforwards available to offset future years' profits.

  2. Value-Added Tax Registration and Monthly Filing: Businesses with annual turnover exceeding KHR 250 million must register for VAT and file monthly VAT returns with supporting documentation. VAT remittance is due by the 20th of the following month. Proper input VAT documentation is essential for claiming input VAT credits.

  3. Withholding Tax Administration: Businesses making payments subject to withholding tax (dividend distributions, interest payments, professional service fees, etc.) must identify payment recipients' tax residency status, apply correct withholding rates, document withholding, maintain records, and remit withheld taxes to the General Department of Taxation on defined schedules. Failure to withhold and remit results in employer liability for unpaid withheld taxes plus penalties.

  4. Personal Income Tax Withholding on Employee Wages: Employers must withhold personal income tax from employee wages based on applicable progressive rates and remit withheld taxes monthly to the tax authority. Employers must also compute and remit employer and employee social security contributions to the National Social Security Fund.

  5. Import Duty Compliance: Businesses importing goods must ensure accurate tariff classification of imported goods, assessment of applicable duty rates, payment of import duties and VAT on imports, and proper customs documentation. QIP status provides duty exemptions or reductions for specified capital equipment and materials.

  6. Accounting Records and Documentation: Businesses must maintain comprehensive accounting records supporting all tax positions, with records retained for minimum five years. Records must be available for inspection by tax authorities and support all tax deductions and credits claimed. Annual financial statements must be prepared in accordance with Cambodian accounting principles and filed with the Ministry of Commerce.

  7. Tax Audit Response and Dispute Resolution: Tax authorities maintain authority to audit businesses' tax compliance, examine records, and assess additional taxes for identified non-compliance. Businesses are entitled to administrative appeal procedures before assessed taxes become final. Dispute resolution procedures enable tax authorities and taxpayers to address valuation disputes, allowable deduction disagreements, and other tax issues through administrative channels prior to litigation.

Proper tax compliance requires engagement of qualified accounting professionals, maintenance of detailed records, timely filing and payment of all applicable taxes, and regular review of tax law changes. The General Department of Taxation maintains offices throughout Cambodia and has increasingly implemented electronic tax administration systems enabling online tax filings and payments. Professional tax advisory services are available from international accounting firms, Cambodian accounting firms, and tax specialists, with typical costs ranging from USD 1,000 to 5,000+ annually depending on business complexity and scope of tax obligations.
