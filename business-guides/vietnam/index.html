Doing Business in Vietnam

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Vietnam is an S-shaped country located on the Indochina peninsula in Southeast Asia, occupying approximately 331,211.6 square kilometers. The nation stretches approximately 1,650 kilometers from north to south, with widths varying from about 50 kilometers at its narrowest point to 500 kilometers at its widest. Vietnam shares a land border of 4,550 kilometers with China to the north, Laos and Cambodia to the west, and the Eastern Sea (South China Sea) to the east and south. The country boasts a long and strategically important coastline of 3,260 kilometers, extending from Mong Cai in the north to Ha Tien in the southwest.

Vietnam's diverse topography reflects its geological and climatic history. The landscape comprises hills, mountains, river deltas, coastlines, and continental shelves shaped by monsoon weather patterns and tropical exposure. Two major river deltas dominate the country's agricultural landscape: the Red River Delta in the north (approximately 16,700 square kilometers) and the Mekong River Delta in the south (approximately 40,000 square kilometers), both featuring highly fertile arable land that has sustained Vietnamese agriculture and civilization for millennia.

The climate varies significantly across the country's regions. Northern Vietnam experiences a monsoonal, humid subtropical climate with four distinct seasons—spring, summer, autumn, and winter—characterized by dry winters and hot to mild summers. Central and southern Vietnam maintain a tropical monsoon climate with only two primary seasons: a rainy season and a dry season. Average annual temperatures in central regions around Hue reach approximately 25°C (77°F), while southern cities like Ho Chi Minh City average around 27°C (81°F) year-round, creating distinct business and operational conditions depending on location.

Vietnam's population as of March 2026 stands at approximately 102.2 million people, representing approximately 1.23 percent of the global population and ranking Vietnam as the 16th most populous nation worldwide. The population exhibits a relatively young but aging demographic profile, with a median age of 33.9 years. Urban areas account for 42.18 percent of the population (approximately 43.1 million people), reflecting ongoing urbanization trends as rural populations migrate to major cities, while 57.82 percent remain in rural areas.

The demographic structure reveals important trends for labor market planning and business operations. Vietnam's population growth rate in 2026 stands at 0.57 percent annually, reflecting declining fertility rates. The Total Fertility Rate (TFR) has fallen to 1.91 children per woman as of 2024, below the replacement level of 2.1, indicating an aging population with long-term implications for the labor market and social expenditure. Life expectancy at birth for both sexes combined is 72.2 years, indicating Vietnam's continued health and development improvements. The workforce benefits from relatively high adult literacy rates, with 94.52 percent of the adult population able to read and write (73.3 million persons), suggesting a capable and trainable labor force.

Dependency ratios provide additional context for business planning. The child dependency ratio stands at 36.4 percent, while the aged dependency ratio is 8.0 percent, indicating that Vietnam still maintains a favorable demographic dividend with more working-age population relative to dependents—though this advantage will gradually diminish over the coming decades. Population density reaches 330 persons per square kilometer (853 per square mile), with higher concentrations in urban centers and the Mekong and Red River deltas, creating distinct regional market characteristics.

1.2 Political System & Government

Vietnam operates as a one-party socialist state under the 1992 Constitution (as amended in 2013 and 2025), with governance structured around centralized state administration. The National Assembly serves as the supreme organ of state and the only body possessing constitutional and legislative power, comprising 498 members including representation for non-Party members. This unicameral parliament appoints the State President (Head of State), the Prime Minister (Head of Government), the Chief Justice of the Supreme People's Court, the head of the Supreme People's Procuracy, and the 21-member Government.

The State President functions as the Head of State, elected by the National Assembly from among its deputies to represent the Socialist Republic of Vietnam in both domestic and foreign affairs. The President holds twelve constitutionally defined powers, most significantly the authority to declare the promulgation of the Constitution and laws, to head the armed forces, and to chair the National Defence and Security Council. The Government serves as the executive organ of the National Assembly and the highest organ of state administration, responsible for fulfilling the political, economic, cultural, social, national defense, security, and external duties of the state under parliamentary oversight.

In January 2026, Tô Lâm was re-elected as General Secretary of the Communist Party for a five-year term by the party congress, maintaining continuity in political leadership. A landmark structural reform, effective July 1, 2025, abolished the district level of local administration through constitutional amendment, establishing a two-tier governmental system: central government, provincial government (Level 1), and commune government (Level 2). This administrative restructuring significantly simplifies business interactions with local authorities and clarifies jurisdictional responsibilities. Additionally, in a major institutional reform, the Ministry of Finance absorbed the Ministry of Planning and Investment in 2025, creating a consolidated national authority responsible for both fiscal management and foreign investment administration.

1.3 Economy Overview

Vietnam's economy has demonstrated resilience and sustained expansion, though with moderating growth trajectories amid global uncertainties. The World Bank projects Vietnam's real GDP growth at 6.5 percent for 2026, a modest decline from the 2025 projection of 6.8 percent. This deceleration reflects the full-year impact of evolving U.S. trade policies, the unwinding of government stimulus measures initiated in 2025, and persistent macroeconomic uncertainties in global markets. The International Monetary Fund similarly projects deceleration for 2026, attributing slower growth to tightening global financial conditions, evolving trade policy environments, and structural economic headwinds.

In contrast to international institutional forecasts, the Vietnamese government has established an ambitious growth target of at least 10 percent GDP expansion for 2026—substantially more optimistic than World Bank and IMF projections. This double-digit growth target reflects government confidence in economic fundamentals and planned policy initiatives but also highlights potential variance between official expectations and international institutional assessments. The apparent disconnect underscores the importance of diversified due diligence when evaluating Vietnam's medium-term economic trajectory. Vietnam's export-led growth model, historically a primary economic driver, faces increasing challenges from rapidly evolving global trade policies, population aging effects on the labor supply, tightening global financial conditions, and climate change impacts on agriculture and coastal infrastructure.

Early 2026 data demonstrates continued foreign investor confidence despite macroeconomic headwinds. New foreign direct investment registrations through February 2026 reached 620 newly licensed projects worth USD 3.54 billion, representing year-over-year increases of 20.2 percent in project count and 61.5 percent in registered capital compared to the same period in 2025. The Republic of Korea and Singapore emerged as the largest investors during this period, each registering over USD 1 billion in newly committed capital. These early 2026 FDI flows suggest sustained international investor interest in Vietnamese markets despite uncertainties, indicating confidence in Vietnam's long-term structural attractiveness as a manufacturing and service export platform.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Vietnam's infrastructure landscape has undergone substantial development in recent years, supporting both domestic economic activity and foreign investment operations. Major transportation networks including national highways, railways, and ports connect primary economic centers with regional trading partners. The Port of Ho Chi Minh City and the Port of Hai Phong rank among Southeast Asia's busiest container terminals, facilitating international trade flows. Domestic and international airport connectivity includes major hubs at Noi Bai International Airport (Hanoi), Tan Son Nhat International Airport (Ho Chi Minh City), and Da Nang International Airport, with significant expansion projects ongoing.

Telecommunications infrastructure has modernized substantially, with extensive 4G and emerging 5G coverage in urban centers and major industrial zones. Internet penetration rates exceed 70 percent nationally, with higher rates in urban areas. These communications improvements support digital commerce, remote work, and technology-enabled business operations critical to modern enterprises. Electricity generation capacity continues to expand through renewable energy investments and grid modernization initiatives, though power supply remains occasionally constrained during peak seasons, requiring business contingency planning.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Vietnam's legal system is based on civil law traditions and socialist principles, administered through a hierarchical structure of courts and administrative agencies. The Supreme People's Court stands at the apex of the judicial system, supported by provincial and district courts. The legislative framework comprises the Constitution as the supreme law, followed by laws enacted by the National Assembly, ordinances and resolutions, and subordinate decrees, circulars, and decisions issued by the Government and ministries. This multi-layered regulatory environment requires foreign investors to navigate both national-level legislation and provincial-level administrative implementations.

The Commercial Law and Enterprise Law form the foundation for business operations, establishing rights, obligations, and procedures for all enterprises operating in Vietnam. The amended Investment Law, effective March 1, 2026 (with certain provisions on the conditional business list taking effect July 1, 2026), represents a landmark modernization of the foreign investment framework. This law removes sector-specific licensing requirements for 38 conditional business lines, narrows the scope of projects requiring investment policy approval, and introduces a procedural change allowing certain foreign investors to establish an enterprise before obtaining an Investment Registration Certificate (IRC)—a significant administrative simplification.

The legal environment for foreign investors has substantially improved through recent reforms emphasizing transparency, efficiency, and reduced bureaucratic burden. The amended Investment Law introduces more open, transparent, and competitive institutional frameworks. Vietnam's commitment to international norms is demonstrated through its extensive network of bilateral and multilateral trade agreements, including membership in the World Trade Organization, participation in major Free Trade Agreements (CPTPP, RCEP), and the planned establishment of Free Trade Zones in Đà Nẵng, Hải Phòng, and Ho Chi Minh City during 2026. However, the legal system remains subject to significant executive and party influence, and foreign investors should recognize that regulatory interpretations may evolve as economic priorities shift.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

Vietnam has substantially liberalized its investment framework through the amended Investment Law effective March 1, 2026, reflecting a strategic policy shift toward greater openness and reduced regulatory barriers. The legislation removes sector-specific licensing requirements for 38 previously conditional business lines, significantly streamlining market entry procedures for businesses in those sectors. The amended law simultaneously narrows the scope of projects requiring formal investment policy approval from the reconstituted Ministry of Finance, reducing administrative delay and uncertainty for certain investor categories. A procedurally significant innovation allows certain foreign investors to legally establish an enterprise and commence initial operations before formally obtaining an Investment Registration Certificate (IRC), though IRC acquisition remains necessary for full tax benefits and long-term security. These procedural changes substantially reduce investor lead times and regulatory uncertainty compared to historical practices.

Vietnam's incentive framework has undergone fundamental restructuring, shifting from place-based (zone-based) incentives to criteria-based incentives directly tied to project characteristics, sectors, and contribution metrics. Historically, location within an industrial zone or special economic zone automatically conferred preferential corporate income tax rates of 10 percent for 15 years, four-year CIT exemptions, 50 percent CIT reduction for nine subsequent years, and import duty exemptions on capital equipment and raw materials. Under the reformed framework effective 2026, zone location alone no longer guarantees incentives; instead, projects must satisfy specific criteria related to technology sophistication, project scale, investment capital magnitude, export potential, or location in economically disadvantaged areas.

High-technology sectors have become the focus of Vietnam's investment incentive strategy. Projects in artificial intelligence, semiconductors, renewable energy, research and development, and software development qualify for preferential CIT rates of 10 percent for up to 15 years—the most generous available incentive tier. Vietnam's new Law on the Digital Technology Industry, effective January 1, 2026, establishes semiconductor products and AI-related businesses as specifically prioritized investment sectors, offering significant incentive policies to support the development of a Vietnamese semiconductor ecosystem and positioning Vietnam as a regional technology hub. These sectoral preferences reflect government recognition that Vietnam must transition from low-cost manufacturing toward higher-value technology development to sustain competitiveness and income growth as labor costs rise.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Vietnam's visa system offers several categories tailored to different business purposes, with processing times and requirements varying by visa type and port of entry. The most relevant categories for business persons include business visas (valid up to 12 months with single or multiple entries), temporary residence cards (typically for longer-term stays), and work visas linked to work permit applications. Visa processing typically requires 3-5 working days for standard applications through Vietnamese embassies and consulates, though expedited processing is available at additional cost. Recent regulatory updates (2026) emphasize streamlining visa procedures to facilitate legitimate business travel and investment activities.

Foreign nationals entering Vietnam must hold a valid passport (typically with at least six months validity beyond the intended departure date) and the appropriate visa. Vietnam does not automatically grant visa-free entry for most foreign nationalities; however, certain countries receive visa exemption or visa-on-arrival privileges for tourism purposes (typically 90 days). Business persons should not rely on tourism visas for business activities, as immigration authorities distinguish between tourism and business purposes. E-visa applications are available online for certain nationalities, facilitating faster processing and reducing in-person consulate visits.

Upon arrival, foreign investors receive a Temporary Resident Card (TRC) or Temporary Residence Certificate, which serves as the primary immigration document while in the country. The TRC validity correlates with the visa duration and, for business persons, is typically valid for periods ranging from one month to one year depending on visa category and visa-granting authorities' assessment. Visa extensions are possible in-country but typically require application through immigration offices with advance planning. Extended stays for business investors and their key personnel require appropriate work permits or investment-related documentation in addition to valid visas. Professional immigration services are widely available in Vietnam and can facilitate visa applications, extensions, and compliance with periodic visa renewals.

All foreign nationals employed in Vietnam are required to obtain a work permit, a separate document from the visa and Temporary Resident Card. The work permit constitutes a fundamental requirement of Vietnamese labor law and is valid for up to two years, with renewal procedures established for extended engagements. Critically, employers are directly responsible for initiating and managing the work permit application process on behalf of foreign employees; foreign nationals cannot apply independently. This corporate responsibility reflects Vietnamese labor policy emphasizing employer accountability for foreign workforce compliance.

Work permit eligibility criteria establish baseline qualifications and professional requirements designed to ensure that foreign workers fill genuine labor gaps in specialized roles. Applicants must be employed in a company or entity registered and legally recognized in Vietnam, be in good health satisfying the specific job demands, and maintain a clean criminal record in their home country without any national security offense history. Prospective foreign workers must additionally fit into statutory employment categories: managerial or executive roles, technical expert positions, or highly skilled technician slots requiring specialized expertise unavailable locally. Educational and experience thresholds establish that candidates should possess a university degree or higher qualification combined with at least two years of skilled work experience relevant to the assigned position in Vietnam. Alternatively, individuals with university degrees in specialized fields (finance, science, technology, innovation, national digital transformation) require a minimum of one year of relevant professional experience.

The application timeline demands strategic planning by employers and hiring organizations. Employer sponsorship must submit work permit applications at least 13 working days before the foreign worker's anticipated start date. Upon submission of complete and valid documentation, Vietnamese authorities typically issue the work permit within five working days. However, comprehensive preparation—including document legalization, consular authentication, and coordination with multiple government agencies—typically requires employers to initiate procedures 6-8 weeks before the projected employment commencement date. This extended timeline reflects bureaucratic coordination across the Department of Labour and Invalids, provincial authorities, and embassies or consulates in home countries. Official issuance fees range from VND 400,000 to VND 600,000 (approximately USD 16-24), varying by province, with Hanoi charging VND 400,000 and Ho Chi Minh City and Da Nang charging VND 600,000 respectively. Work-permit eligible foreign nationals must additionally maintain a valid Work Visa or Temporary Resident Card throughout their employment tenure.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Foreign individuals and foreign-invested enterprises can acquire rights to land use in Vietnam, though direct land ownership is restricted. Land use rights are granted for specific periods determined by land type: agricultural land up to 50 years, industrial and commercial land up to 70 years, and residential land up to 20 years (though residential leases may be renewable). Foreign investors must secure Investment Registration Certificates and satisfy sector-specific requirements to acquire land use rights through lease or transfer arrangements.

Foreign-invested enterprises can rent or lease land from Vietnamese landholders, government agencies, or state enterprises managing public land. Lease arrangements typically require formal contracts specifying terms, rental payments, lease duration, and conditions for renewal or termination. Long-term land leases remain the most practical mechanism for foreign investors requiring permanent facilities without undertaking complex direct ownership arrangements.

Real property transactions in Vietnam require careful attention to regulatory requirements, land classification, and local implementation practices. Foreign investors should engage qualified Vietnamese legal counsel to review land acquisition arrangements, verify title documentation, and ensure compliance with all applicable regulations before executing property agreements or transferring funds.

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Vietnam's judicial system provides primary mechanisms for commercial dispute resolution through civil courts, though alternative mechanisms including arbitration and mediation offer advantages for certain disputes. Commercial disputes between enterprises and individuals are adjudicated by provincial and district civil courts with specialized commercial divisions. Vietnamese courts apply Vietnamese law and follow civil law procedural systems distinct from common law countries' procedures, requiring careful navigation by foreign investors unfamiliar with Vietnamese judicial processes.

International commercial arbitration provides alternative dispute resolution mechanisms, with Vietnam having ratified the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (New York Convention, 1958). Arbitration conducted under major arbitral rules including ICC (International Chamber of Commerce), SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre), and UNCITRAL Rules provides enforceable dispute resolution alternatives avoiding Vietnamese court systems. Foreign investors frequently prefer arbitration clauses in international commercial contracts with Vietnamese counterparties, gaining greater predictability and enforceability compared to Vietnamese court proceedings.

Mediation and negotiation mechanisms, supported by governmental and private institutions, offer preliminary dispute resolution channels before resorting to litigation or arbitration. Many commercial disputes benefit from early mediation efforts addressing underlying business relationship issues before escalating to formal dispute proceedings. Professional dispute resolution services including mediation, conciliation, and arbitration support are available from established Vietnamese institutions and international arbitral centers with regional offices.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Vietnam's strategic economic position in Southeast Asia creates significant opportunities across diverse sectors. Manufacturing represents a primary investment sector, with particular strengths in electronics, textiles and apparel, footwear, and automotive components. Recent policy emphasis on technology and semiconductors creates emerging opportunities in high-value manufacturing segments moving beyond traditional low-cost labor advantages.

Agriculture and food processing leverage Vietnam's strong agricultural base and geographic advantages. Coffee, rice, seafood, and tropical fruits represent traditional export strengths, with opportunities for value-added processing, organic certification, and premium market development. Renewable energy—including solar, wind, and hydroelectric projects—represents a rapidly expanding sector supported by government targets for renewable energy transition.

Technology and software development sectors benefit from Vietnam's youthful, educated workforce and lower development costs compared to developed economies. Business process outsourcing, software development, digital services, and IT consulting create significant opportunities for foreign technology investors. E-commerce and digital economy sectors reflect Vietnam's rapid internet penetration and emerging consumer market development.

Tourism and hospitality sectors remain major foreign investment recipients, particularly in beach resorts, cultural heritage destinations, and adventure tourism. Real estate development—residential, commercial, and mixed-use projects—continues attracting significant foreign capital, particularly in primary urban centers and emerging secondary cities. Healthcare and education services represent growing opportunities as rising income levels increase demand for quality private healthcare and international educational institutions.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

High-technology sectors receive the most aggressive government incentive structures. Projects in artificial intelligence, semiconductors, renewable energy, research and development, and software development qualify for preferential CIT rates of 10 percent for up to 15 years. These incentive tiers recognize government prioritization of technology transition and represent the most favorable tax treatment available to foreign investors.

Projects meeting specified criteria related to investment scale, technology sophistication, export potential, or location in economically disadvantaged areas qualify for preferential CIT rates ranging from 10-15 percent for specified periods, typically 10-15 years. These criteria-based incentives replaced traditional zone-based automatic incentives, requiring project evaluation against specific technical and economic thresholds.

Employment and training incentives support enterprises creating substantial local employment or developing workforce capabilities through training programs. Corporate income tax exemptions or reductions may be available for specified periods to enterprises meeting employment thresholds or implementing approved training programs in priority sectors.

Research and development activities conducted in Vietnam attract incentive support including accelerated depreciation, enhanced research cost deductions, and potential CIT rate reductions for specialized R&D entities. These R&D incentives reflect government recognition that sustained economic development requires investment in technology innovation and knowledge creation beyond manufacturing process improvements.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

Free Trade Zones represent Vietnam's latest strategic economic development initiative, designed to create internationally competitive business environments supporting advanced manufacturing, logistics, financial services, and technology operations. The first FTZ facilities are expected to become operational in 2026 in Đà Nẵng, Hải Phòng, and Ho Chi Minh City, with more ambitious plans targeting 6-8 FTZs by 2030 and 8-10 FTZs meeting international standards by 2045. These zones are projected to contribute 15-20 percent of national GDP by 2045, indicating government commitment to transforming them into major economic drivers.

FTZs offer enhanced autonomy for businesses, reduced administrative procedures, expanded foreign exchange flexibility, and international business-standard operational frameworks. These zones provide exceptional competitive advantages for enterprises planning regional headquarters, international distribution centers, financial service operations, or advanced manufacturing activities requiring preferential procedures compared to standard Vietnam regulations.

Industrial Zones (IZs) and Special Economic Zones (SEZs) throughout Vietnam provide established infrastructure supporting manufacturing and export operations. These zones feature developed transportation networks, utility connections, telecommunications infrastructure, and customs clearance facilities reducing operational complexity for foreign enterprises. Traditional zone-based tax incentives have been substantially reduced under the 2026 reforms, with criteria-based incentives replacing automatic location-based benefits. However, industrial zones remain strategically important for enterprises requiring integrated manufacturing infrastructure and logistics connectivity.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Vietnam's participation in major trade agreements provides preferential market access supporting both imports and exports. The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP) provides preferential tariff access to 11 partner economies including Australia, Canada, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, New Zealand, Singapore, and others. CPTPP implementation benefits Vietnam through tariff reductions on exports while expanding market opportunities for businesses importing components from partner countries.

The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) connects Vietnam with ASEAN partners, China, Japan, South Korea, and Australia, representing the world's largest regional trade agreement. RCEP provisions facilitate regional supply chain integration and provide competitive advantages for enterprises participating in regional manufacturing networks. ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA) provisions eliminate tariffs on goods traded among ASEAN member states, supporting regional distribution and manufacturing coordination.

Vietnam's World Trade Organization (WTO) membership commits the country to non-discriminatory trade practices and gradual tariff reductions aligned with international standards. WTO participation ensures that Vietnam maintains comparable tariff structures with other WTO members, reducing protection for Vietnamese producers while providing market access benefits for foreign exporters competing in Vietnamese markets.

Bilateral trade agreements with individual countries including the United States (pending), Japan, South Korea, and EU member states provide additional preferential market access. These bilateral arrangements often include investment protection provisions, dispute resolution mechanisms, and specific sectoral provisions addressing tariff reduction or non-tariff barrier elimination.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Vietnam's banking system is administered by the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV), which sets monetary policy and regulatory frameworks while commercial banks operate under SBV oversight. All foreign exchange transactions—including buying, selling, lending, and transferring foreign currency—must be conducted exclusively through credit institutions or other financial institutions formally authorized by the State Bank of Vietnam. This regulatory requirement centralizes foreign exchange administration and provides transaction oversight, though it also necessitates that foreign investors establish banking relationships with authorized institutions for international fund transfers, currency conversion, and cross-border payments.

The SBV operates a managed floating exchange rate system where the central bank sets a central exchange rate daily based on a basket of major currencies, with the U.S. dollar playing a dominant role. The SBV permits the Vietnamese Dong (VND) to fluctuate within a narrow band around the determined central rate, adjusting the rate periodically to align with broader economic objectives including inflation management and export competitiveness. This managed float system provides greater exchange rate predictability than free float systems while maintaining periodic adjustment capacity to reflect economic fundamentals.

Recent regulatory developments in 2025-2026 have introduced more flexible frameworks for foreign-invested enterprises regarding currency management. The State Bank issued Circular No. 03/2025/TT-NHNN (effective June 16, 2025), providing detailed guidance on opening and use of Vietnamese Dong accounts by non-resident foreign investors engaging in indirect investment activities in Vietnam. This circular introduces a 12-month transitional period (by June 16, 2026) allowing companies previously exempt from certain regulations to open a Direct Investment Capital Account (DICA), facilitating repatriation of capital and profit flows. Simultaneously, the government issued Decree No. 329/2025/ND-CP establishing detailed regulations on banking operations, foreign exchange management, anti-money laundering, and anti-terrorism financing within special international financial centers.

Consumer currency exchange regulations, updated in 2026, impose stricter controls affecting both tourists and expatriate workers. General exchange agencies now can only "buy" foreign currency from individuals and cannot "sell" foreign currency to tourists. Foreign currency can only be sold to tourists in quarantine areas of international ports, a requirement that affects personal currency needs for returning residents and travelers. These restrictions underscore Vietnam's prudent foreign exchange management approach, though they impose practical constraints on individuals requiring currency conversion.

Commercial banking services available through foreign and domestic banks include corporate lending, trade finance, cash management, and deposit facilities. Foreign investors should establish primary banking relationships early in operations, as formal banking relationships facilitate transaction processing, regulatory compliance, and access to financing. Major international banks maintain operations in Vietnam and provide services consistent with international standards, while domestic banks offer competitive rates and Vietnam-specialized expertise.

4. Business Organisations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Vietnam's Enterprise Law (2020) establishes six primary legal business structures available to foreign and domestic investors, each with distinct liability characteristics, regulatory requirements, and operational flexibility. The selection of business structure fundamentally affects investor liability exposure, capital requirements, management complexity, and tax treatment, making informed structural choice critical to optimizing investment outcomes.

Limited Liability Company (LLC) represents the most prevalent structure selected by foreign investors due to its favorable combination of limited liability protection, operational flexibility, and modest regulatory burden. Limited liability companies can be structured as single-member LLCs (owned by one individual or organization) or multi-member LLCs with 2 to 50 members. In both configurations, members maintain limited liability for enterprise debts and obligations, with personal exposure capped at the amount of capital contributed to the enterprise. This liability limitation provides critical asset protection, preventing creditors from pursuing members' personal assets beyond their contributed capital. LLCs offer flexibility in profit distribution arrangements, management structure (choosing between member management or appointed external managers), and operational decision-making. These characteristics make LLCs particularly attractive for foreign investors implementing market-entry strategies or testing business concepts with controlled capital commitment.

Joint Stock Company (JSC) structures suit larger investment projects, enterprises planning future equity fundraising, or investors intending to pursue public equity markets. Joint stock companies divide ownership into freely transferable shares, facilitating capital-raising from multiple investors, employee share programs, and potential future public offerings. Like LLCs, shareholders in joint stock companies maintain limited liability restricted to shareholding value. JSCs require more formal governance structures including mandatory board of directors, shareholder meetings, and comprehensive record-keeping requirements compared to simpler LLC structures. JSCs are mandatory for enterprises planning to seek public equity markets or requiring venture capital financing from multiple institutional sources.

Sole Proprietorship represents the simplest business structure available, requiring minimal legal procedures and regulatory compliance. However, sole proprietors maintain unlimited personal liability for all business debts, obligations, and liabilities—meaning creditors can pursue personal assets, savings, property, and other holdings to satisfy business obligations. This unlimited liability exposure creates significant personal financial risk and makes sole proprietorships impractical for most substantive business undertakings. Sole proprietorships are primarily utilized by individual contractors, small service providers, or traders unwilling to undergo formal company registration.

Partnership structures enable multiple individuals or organizations to combine capital and expertise for common business purposes while sharing decision-making authority and economic benefits. Like sole proprietorships, partnerships generally impose unlimited liability on partners unless structured as limited partnerships with designated limited partners (passive capital providers bearing liability only to contributed amount) and general partners (managing operators bearing unlimited liability). Partnerships require detailed partnership agreements addressing capital contributions, profit-sharing arrangements, decision-making procedures, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

Representative Office structures allow foreign companies to establish limited Vietnam presence for liaison, marketing, and information-gathering purposes without conducting substantive profit-generating business activities. Representative offices cannot generate independent revenue, must remain directly controlled by parent foreign organizations, and face strict operational restrictions. Representative offices serve primarily as market-research and liaison facilities for foreign enterprises evaluating Vietnam market entry before committing to full company establishment.

Branch Office structures enable foreign companies to conduct commercial activities in Vietnam under foreign parent company names and liability, with the foreign parent maintaining direct operational and financial responsibility. Branch offices avoid separate entity registration and financing but provide less legal separation between foreign parent and Vietnam operations. Branches remain under direct parent company management and maintain fully transparent parent-subsidiary relationships with regulatory authorities.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Establishing a business entity in Vietnam requires obtaining multiple registrations and licenses depending on the entity type and intended business activities. The general registration process begins with selection of company name and verification of availability through the Department of Planning and Investment (now merged into the Ministry of Finance at provincial level). Foreign investors must obtain an Investment Registration Certificate (IRC) from provincial investment authorities before or concurrent with enterprise registration, establishing formal governmental recognition of the investment project.

The enterprise registration process requires submission of constituent documents including the Charter (for LLCs) or Charter and shareholders' agreement (for JSCs), identification of management personnel, declaration of registered capital, and documentation evidencing foreign investor identity and capital source. Provincial business registration offices process applications within specified timeframes, typically 3-5 working days for complete and compliant submissions, issuing an Enterprise Registration Certificate upon approval.

Tax registration occurs automatically upon enterprise registration for most enterprises, with the General Department of Taxation issuing a Tax Registration Certificate. However, VAT-taxable enterprises must separately register with tax authorities if automatic VAT registration does not occur. Statistical registration and social insurance registration for enterprises with employees follow enterprise establishment, with registration processes requiring submission of business plan information and employee documentation respectively.

Sector-specific licenses and permits may be required for enterprises operating in regulated industries including banking, insurance, telecommunications, energy, healthcare, pharmaceuticals, and other government-controlled or regulated sectors. License requirements vary substantially by sector and specific business activities, requiring careful preliminary assessment before committing capital to sectors subject to licensing or approval requirements.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

Vietnam's Enterprise Law establishes mandatory governance structures varying by entity type. Limited liability companies with multiple members require a Member Meeting (equivalent to shareholders' meeting in JSCs) functioning as the supreme decision-making body. LLCs may be managed by member-managers (members directly managing the company) or appointed professional managers (non-members managing on behalf of members). The selection between member management and external management substantially affects governance complexity and professional management oversight.

Joint stock companies require mandatory governance structures including a General Shareholders' Meeting (supreme decision-making body), a Board of Directors (managing board overseeing strategic direction and major decisions), and an internal control function (audit committee or internal audit department for enterprises of specified size or sector). These formal governance requirements provide more robust oversight compared to LLC structures and suit larger enterprises or those planning capital market access.

The Board of Directors typically comprises 3-11 members in JSCs, with members serving specified terms (typically two to four years) and eligibility for re-election. Board composition requirements establish that a minimum proportion of directors be independent (non-executive, non-shareholder) directors, depending on listed versus non-listed status and regulatory guidance. Management decisions require board approval, with major decisions (capital increases, related-party transactions, asset dispositions, acquisitions) requiring board authority with statutory threshold specifications.

Minutes and decision records must be maintained documenting governance decisions, shareholder/member approvals, and major management decisions. Foreign investors should familiarize themselves with Vietnamese corporate governance requirements and establish compliance procedures addressing board meeting frequency, decision documentation, and management authority delineation.

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Vietnam's labor market offers competitive wage structures alongside well-developed regulatory frameworks governing employment relationships, working conditions, and workplace safety. The standard working day is limited to eight hours, with a statutory 48-hour maximum working week, establishing the baseline for standard employment contracts. Overtime is permitted but heavily regulated: daily overtime cannot exceed 50 percent of standard working hours in a single day, monthly overtime is capped at 40 hours, and annual overtime is limited to 200 hours, though extension to 300 hours is possible in specific industries including textiles, electronics, and agriculture. These overtime limitations reflect labor policy balancing business operational needs with worker protection and fatigue management.

Minimum wage regulations establish four geographic wage zones reflecting regional cost-of-living variations and economic development disparities. Effective January 1, 2026, the new minimum wages are:

  • Zone 1 (including Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, Da Nang, and surrounding localities): USD 215 per month (or USD 1.03 per hour)
  • Zone 2 (including major provincial capitals): USD 191 per month (or USD 0.92 per hour)
  • Zone 3 (secondary provincial areas): USD 167 per month (or USD 0.81 per hour)
  • Zone 4 (remote and economically disadvantaged areas): USD 149 per month (or USD 0.72 per hour)

These 2026 rates represent increases of USD 10-14 per month compared to 2025 minimum wages, reflecting approximately 7.2 percent average annual wage growth. Foreign investors should note that minimum wage increases typically occur annually, with future increases announced in October-November for implementation on January 1 of the following year. The four-zone system reflects government policy to encourage regional development while acknowledging real differences in living costs and labor market conditions across Vietnam's diverse geography.

Labor regulations establish detailed requirements for employment contracts, worker compensation, workplace safety, social insurance contributions, and dispute resolution. Foreign enterprises must offer employment contracts meeting legal form requirements and containing mandatory provisions including position, compensation, working conditions, and termination terms. Employers are required to contribute to mandatory social insurance (providing retirement, health, and disability benefits), unemployment insurance, and work accident and occupational disease insurance. These employer contributions typically amount to approximately 17.5-18.5 percent of employee compensation, substantially impacting labor cost calculations. Foreign workers employed by Vietnamese enterprises or foreign enterprises with Vietnam operations are subject to the same labor law requirements as Vietnamese citizens, ensuring non-discriminatory treatment while requiring full compliance with Vietnamese labor protections.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Vietnam's intellectual property framework underwent significant modernization through amendments to the Law on Intellectual Property passed by the National Assembly on December 10, 2025, with major provisions taking effect April 1, 2026. These reforms represent a comprehensive modernization designed to support the digital economy while strengthening protection mechanisms and enabling IP commercialization. The amendments accelerate registration procedures by shortening publication, opposition, and examination stages for industrial property applications while introducing a new fast-track mechanism for eligible patent and trademark applications. These procedural improvements substantially reduce the timeline for obtaining enforceable IP rights, typically from 18-24 months to potentially 6-12 months depending on application complexity and office workload.

Vietnam's IP registration system operates on a first-to-file basis, administered by the Intellectual Property Office of Vietnam (IP Vietnam), which manages applications for trademarks, patents, industrial designs, layout-designs of integrated circuits, and geographical indications. The 2026 amendments expand industrial design protection to cover product parts and certain non-physical forms previously excluded, acknowledging the evolution of design innovation in digital and creative industries. More fundamentally, the amended framework shifts regulatory philosophy to treat patents, trademarks, and other IP as enterprise "knowledge assets" that can be valued, traded, contributed as capital, and used as collateral for financing purposes, rather than merely legal rights. This commercialization orientation aligns Vietnam's IP system with international practice and facilitates IP-backed financing mechanisms increasingly common in developed economies.

Enforcement mechanisms have been significantly strengthened under the 2026 amendments, particularly regarding online IP infringement. The revisions introduce expanded enforcement options designed to address digital economy challenges, including e-commerce platforms, digital content distribution, and online counterfeiting. These enforcement improvements aim to provide IP rights holders with practical remedies against digital infringement that plagued previous enforcement regimes under more restrictive IP statutes. Foreign investors relying on trademark or patent protection for competitive advantage benefit substantially from these enhanced procedures and enforcement mechanisms.

The IP registration timeline and costs have become more transparent and predictable under recent administrative improvements. Trademark registrations typically take 8-12 months from filing to grant (accelerated to 4-6 months through fast-track procedures for straightforward applications), while patent registrations range from 12-24 months depending on technological complexity and office examination workload. Official filing fees and registration charges remain modest by international standards, with trademark registrations costing approximately USD 100-150 and patent applications requiring USD 150-250 in official government fees, though professional legal fees for IP counsel typically substantially exceed government charges.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Vietnam's tax system comprises multiple layers of direct and indirect taxation administered by the General Department of Taxation (GDT) and provincial tax authorities. Direct taxes include corporate income tax (CIT), personal income tax (PIT), and land-related taxes. Indirect taxes include value-added tax (VAT), environmental protection tax (EPT), and customs duties. This multi-layered system requires careful compliance planning and professional tax guidance to optimize tax positions while maintaining regulatory compliance.

Vietnamese enterprises are required to obtain a Tax Registration Certificate (TRC) upon establishment or within specified timeframes, with the TRC number required for all business transactions, bank accounts, and import-export activities. Monthly or quarterly VAT declarations are required for all VAT-taxable enterprises, with annual CIT and PIT settlements conducted on calendar-year basis (declarations due by March 31 of following year for CIT; PIT compliance depends on individual employment status and payment timing).

Foreign investors should recognize that Vietnamese tax authorities conduct regular compliance reviews and audits, with enforcement intensity varying by region and taxpayer profile. Proper documentation, accurate calculation, and timely filing substantially reduce audit risk. Professional tax service providers and accounting firms familiar with Vietnamese requirements are widely available at reasonable cost and strongly recommended for foreign enterprises unfamiliar with Vietnamese tax procedures. Recent tax law reforms have emphasized simplified compliance procedures for qualifying SMEs, though substantive enterprises should maintain comprehensive documentation systems supporting all tax positions and deductions claimed.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

The standard corporate income tax (CIT) rate in Vietnam is 20 percent, applicable to all enterprises unless eligible for preferential rates or specific industry treatment. Vietnam's new Corporate Income Tax Law (Law No. 67/2025/QH15), effective October 1, 2025, introduced significant reforms to the tax rate structure, creating differentiated rates for small and medium enterprises to reduce administrative burden on smaller operations and encourage entrepreneurship.

Preferential Rates for Small and Medium Enterprises: Starting October 1, 2025, enterprises with annual revenue below VND 3 billion (approximately USD 120,000) qualify for a reduced CIT rate of 15 percent—providing five percentage point relief compared to the standard rate. Enterprises with annual revenue between VND 3 billion and VND 50 billion (USD 120,000 to USD 2,000,000) qualify for a 17 percent rate, providing three percentage point relief. These preferential rates effectively reduce tax burdens on emerging enterprises and support business formation at the small-to-medium enterprise scale. The revenue thresholds are assessed annually, with enterprises potentially transitioning between rate categories as they grow.

Special Industry Rates: Enterprises operating in the oil and gas industry face substantially higher CIT rates ranging from 25 to 50 percent depending on individual production-sharing contracts and project-specific fiscal terms negotiated with the government. These elevated rates reflect resource nationalism policies and government capture of resource rent, with rates varying significantly based on project profitability, depletion rates, and contract terms negotiated at project inception.

Effective Dates and Transition: The reformed CIT law with preferential SME rates became effective October 1, 2025, and applies to the 2025 tax year onwards. Enterprises should carefully evaluate whether they qualify for preferential rates based on 2025 revenue thresholds and structure their organizational expansion plans considering rate thresholds and timing of revenue growth.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

Vietnam's reformed Personal Income Tax Law, partially effective January 1, 2026 (with full implementation on July 1, 2026), substantially reduces tax burdens on individual workers and substantially simplifies the tax bracket structure. The new law reduces the complex seven-bracket PIT system to five brackets while retaining the 35 percent maximum marginal rate applied to high-income earners. The legislation raises the income threshold for the 35 percent top bracket to VND 100 million per month (increased from VND 80 million), creating additional relief for high-income professionals.

Increased Personal Deductions: The personal monthly deduction for standard employees increases substantially from VND 11 million to VND 15.5 million, representing approximately 41 percent deduction increase. Dependent deductions increase from VND 4.4 million to VND 6.2 million per dependent per month. These increased deductions directly reduce taxable income and provide meaningful relief across all income levels.

Tax Exemption Thresholds: Under the reformed structure, a single individual earning approximately VND 17 million per month will pay zero PIT. Each dependent qualifies for an additional VND 6.2 million monthly deduction, meaning a taxpayer with one dependent supporting VND 24 million in household income will also be exempt from PIT. These thresholds result in significant portions of the working population paying minimal or zero PIT.

Illustrative Tax Impact: To illustrate the reform's significance: An individual earning VND 50 million monthly with one dependent will pay approximately VND 1.84 million in PIT (compared to VND 4.3 million under previous law), representing approximately 57 percent reduction. An earner at VND 100 million monthly will see PIT decline by more than VND 5.5 million to approximately VND 12.5 million, reducing the effective tax rate from approximately 13-14 percent to approximately 12.5 percent.

Foreign Worker Taxation: Foreign workers employed in Vietnam are subject to the same PIT rates and brackets as Vietnamese citizens for income earned in Vietnam. Income earned from foreign sources may receive different treatment depending on employment contract terms and treaty provisions. The reformed PIT law benefits foreign employees identically to Vietnamese citizen employees, effectively reducing tax liabilities for expatriate workforces.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

Value-Added Tax (VAT) represents Vietnam's principal consumption tax, with a multi-tier rate structure ranging from 0 percent to 10 percent depending on goods and services classification. The standard VAT rate applies to most goods and services at 10 percent; however, a temporary government-sponsored reduction of two percentage points (from 10 to 8 percent) has been extended through December 31, 2026, benefiting specified goods and services. This temporary reduction provides cost relief for priority sectors including construction materials, healthcare services, and education services. The five percent VAT rate applies to certain essential goods including basic food items, medicines, and specific healthcare services. Zero percent VAT applies to eligible exports of goods and services, including international transportation and cross-border services to non-Vietnamese recipients.

Certain goods and services are fully exempt from VAT, including insurance and financial services, rental of non-commercial real property, certain healthcare and educational services, and specific cultural activities. Exempt goods and services do not generate VAT revenue for government but also do not permit input VAT credits, placing exempt providers at a VAT cost disadvantage compared to taxable suppliers.

Recent VAT Law Changes (Effective January 1, 2026): The amended VAT Law introduces significant changes to VAT administration and classification. Effective July 1, 2025, the VAT rate for foreign suppliers lacking a permanent establishment in Vietnam who conduct e-commerce or digital-based business activities with Vietnamese organizations and individuals increased from 5 to 10 percent, reflecting government efforts to equalize tax burdens between foreign and domestic digital service providers. Simultaneously, the annual revenue threshold triggering mandatory VAT registration for households and individuals increased substantially from VND 100 million to VND 500 million, effectively exempting smaller individual traders from formal VAT registration and compliance requirements.

Withholding Taxes: Vietnam's withholding tax framework establishes tax obligations on certain cross-border payments and domestic distributions, with rates varying by income type and recipient status. These withholding requirements are fundamental to Vietnam's international taxation regime and represent critical compliance obligations for any enterprise making cross-border payments or profit distributions.

  • Dividend Withholding Tax: Enterprises paying dividends to corporate shareholders incur zero percent withholding tax on dividends—effectively creating a single-layer corporate income tax system where corporate profits are taxed at the 20 percent CIT rate with no additional withholding on dividend distributions. Dividends distributed to individual shareholders face 5 percent withholding tax, creating a two-layer taxation effect where corporate profits are first taxed at 20 percent CIT and then dividends to individuals face additional 5 percent withholding. Foreign parent companies receiving dividends from Vietnamese subsidiaries qualify for the zero percent corporate rate provided they meet formal documentation and permanent establishment requirements.

  • Interest Withholding Tax: Interest paid by Vietnamese enterprises to non-resident lenders is subject to 10 percent withholding tax, regardless of lender status (corporate or individual). This withholding represents a final tax obligation on interest income and is collected by the Vietnamese obligor at source.

  • Royalty Withholding Tax: Royalties paid for intellectual property usage, patents, trademarks, and similar intangible property rights are subject to 10 percent withholding tax when paid to non-residents. This withholding applies to technology transfer payments, software licensing fees, and similar IP-related remittances.

Environmental Protection Tax (EPT): Goods manufactured domestically or imported into Vietnam that are deemed harmful to human health or the environment are subject to environmental protection tax, calculated as a percentage of commodity prices and added to tax liabilities. EPT rates vary by product type and environmental impact assessment, with higher rates on fossil fuels, packaging materials, and chemically intensive goods. EPT compliance requires careful product classification and assessment of whether manufactured or imported goods trigger EPT obligations.

Property Tax and Land Use Tax: Vietnamese enterprises using land are subject to annual land use tax calculated as a percentage of land use values. The tax varies significantly by location, land classification (industrial, commercial, residential), and regional economic tier. Land use tax represents an annual recurring obligation for enterprises maintaining physical premises in Vietnam. Additionally, transfer of land use rights triggers transfer tax obligations on appreciation value.

Customs Duties and Import Tariffs: Vietnam's tariff system applies differentiated rates based on goods classification and importer origin-country trade status. Most Favored Nation (MFN) tariff rates apply to goods from countries with MFN status (including WTO members). Special preferential tariff rates (typically 0-5 percent) apply to goods from Free Trade Agreement partners including ASEAN member states, China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and others depending on specific agreement terms. Ordinary tariff rates apply to goods from countries lacking MFN status, typically ranging from 5-40 percent depending on product sensitivity. Import tariff calculations require detailed commodity classification using the Vietnamese Harmonized Tariff Schedule and careful determination of applicable preferential rates based on rules of origin and importer eligibility.

Effective February 1, 2026, Circular 121 updated customs procedures to align with recently enacted laws and decrees, affecting customs documentation practices, internal controls, and compliance obligations particularly for processing, manufacturing, and export-oriented enterprises. Additionally, Decree 73/2025/ND-CP (issued March 31, 2025) reduced Most-Favored-Nation import tariff rates on agricultural commodities including corn, soybean meal, ethanol, frozen chicken drumsticks, in-shell pistachios, almonds, fresh apples, cherries, and raisins, benefiting importers of these specific goods.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

High-technology sectors receive preferential CIT rates of 10 percent for up to 15 years, representing the most generous available incentive tier. These rates apply to projects in artificial intelligence, semiconductors, renewable energy, research and development, and software development sectors prioritized under Vietnam's technology development strategy.

Projects meeting specified criteria related to investment scale, technology sophistication, export potential, or location in economically disadvantaged areas qualify for preferential CIT rates ranging from 10-15 percent for specified periods. These criteria-based incentives replaced traditional zone-based automatic incentives, requiring project evaluation against specific technical and economic thresholds.

Employment and training incentives support enterprises creating substantial local employment or developing workforce capabilities through training programs. Corporate income tax exemptions or reductions may be available for specified periods to enterprises meeting employment thresholds or implementing approved training programs in priority sectors.

Research and development activities conducted in Vietnam attract incentive support including accelerated depreciation, enhanced research cost deductions, and potential CIT rate reductions for specialized R&D entities. These R&D incentives reflect government recognition that sustained economic development requires investment in technology innovation and knowledge creation beyond manufacturing process improvements.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Vietnam maintains an extensive network of bilateral tax treaties with approximately 100 countries, many of which reduce withholding tax rates below statutory levels. Treaty benefits typically reduce dividend withholding to 5 percent (from the standard 10 percent for certain treaty partners), interest withholding to 5-10 percent (from 10 percent), and royalty withholding to 5-10 percent (from 10 percent). To benefit from treaty rates, the foreign recipient must provide a valid tax residence certificate demonstrating residence in a treaty partner country and comply with procedural requirements set by Vietnamese tax authorities. Treaty applications typically require 10-15 business days for processing and approval. Many treaties also provide exemptions for certain service payments and management fees under specific conditions.

Transfer pricing rules require that transactions between related parties (parent companies, subsidiaries, branches, and affiliates) be conducted at arm's length prices reflecting prices between unrelated parties for comparable transactions. Vietnam's transfer pricing regulations, amended in recent years to align with OECD guidelines, require documentation supporting transfer prices and permit Vietnamese tax authorities to adjust transfer prices deemed non-arm's length. Large enterprises with substantial related-party transactions should develop comprehensive transfer pricing documentation and analysis supporting pricing methodology and comparable transaction benchmarking.

Base erosion and profit shifting (BEPS) initiatives have led to expanded reporting requirements and documentation obligations for large multinational enterprises. Country-by-country reporting (CbCR) requires large multinational groups to provide annual reports detailing financial and tax information by country. Additionally, transfer pricing documentation must support the economic substance and arm's length nature of inter-company transactions. Foreign investors should ensure transfer pricing strategies comply with Vietnamese transfer pricing regulations and international BEPS guidance to minimize audit risk and assessment exposure.
