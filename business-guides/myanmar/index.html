Doing Business in Myanmar

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Myanmar, officially the Myanmar Union, is located in Southeast Asia in the Indochina Peninsula, bordered by Bangladesh, India, China, Laos, and Thailand. The country covers a total land area of 653,290 square kilometres, making it the largest country in mainland Southeast Asia. Myanmar's geography is characterized by diverse terrain, including the Irrawaddy Delta in the south, the dry zone in central Myanmar, and mountainous regions in the north and east. The country's strategic location along major regional trade routes has historically made it an important economic corridor in the region.

The nation is served by major international ports and airports, with Yangon (Rangoon) remaining the primary commercial and transport hub, despite the relocation of the capital to Naypyidaw in 2006. Yangon continues to host the country's main airport, seaport, and serves as the financial and business centre where most foreign investment activity is concentrated. The country's major rivers, particularly the Irrawaddy, provide important transport and agricultural irrigation infrastructure. Geographically, Myanmar's population is heavily concentrated in the delta and lower delta regions, with urban centers including Yangon (population 5.92 million), Mandalay, and the capital Naypyidaw.

Myanmar has a population of approximately 55.1 million people as of March 2026, with a population density of 84 per square kilometre. The country is highly ethnically diverse, with the government recognizing 135 distinct ethnic groups and at least 108 ethnolinguistic communities. The Bamar comprise approximately 68% of the population, followed by the Shan (10%), Kayin (7%), Rakhine (4%), and overseas Chinese (3%). This ethnic diversity reflects Myanmar's complex social and political landscape and influences business culture, local markets, and regulatory considerations in different regions.

Demographically, Myanmar's median age is 30.4 years, indicating a relatively young population with significant workforce potential. Approximately 34.6% of the population is urban, with the remainder concentrated in rural agricultural areas. The majority of the population practises Buddhism (87.9%), with Christian (6.2%), Muslim (4.3%), and smaller populations of other religions comprising the remainder. Understanding this demographic composition is relevant for businesses operating in consumer markets, labour recruitment, and cultural considerations in commercial activities.

1.2 Political System & Government

Myanmar has been under military governance since February 2021, when the State Administration Council (SAC) dissolved the democratically elected government. On 31 July 2025, the SAC was itself dissolved and replaced by the State Security and Peace Commission (SSPC), though nearly all senior SSPC members are current or former military officers already subject to international sanctions. The military administration has consolidated control over executive, legislative, and judicial functions. Foreign investors and businesses should be aware of this political context and understand that the operating environment reflects decisions made by a military-led government.

The international community, including the United States, European Union, and United Kingdom, maintains comprehensive sanctions regimes targeting Myanmar's military leadership and certain sectors of the economy. These sanctions include targeted measures against senior officials and their associates, as well as sectoral restrictions on trade and investment in certain industries. The EU's current sanctions regime is set to remain in effect until at least 30 April 2026. Any foreign business considering investment in Myanmar must carefully assess sanctions compliance requirements and engage with qualified legal counsel regarding permissible business activities and jurisdictional restrictions on operations.

1.3 Economy Overview

Myanmar's economy faces significant structural challenges stemming from political instability, ongoing internal conflicts, international sanctions, and infrastructure limitations. The World Bank reported that Myanmar's real GDP contracted by approximately 2.0% in the fiscal year ending March 2026, with a projected modest rebound of 3.0% forecast for fiscal year 2026/27. This recovery is expected to be driven primarily by post-earthquake reconstruction efforts (following the March 2025 earthquake) and targeted economic stimulus measures, though actual growth remains subject to significant uncertainty related to ongoing political and security conditions.

Inflation remains elevated, with expectations that inflation will remain above 20% in the near term, which affects business operating costs, wage negotiations, and pricing strategies. The Myanmar Kyat has appreciated steadily throughout 2025 following a sharp depreciation in the prior year, with the Central Bank of Myanmar establishing the exchange rate at approximately MMK 3,658 per USD as of March 9, 2026. However, foreign exchange availability and volatility remain key considerations for businesses involved in import-export activities or repatriating profits.

Myanmar's agrifood sector remains a significant economic driver, contributing approximately 27% of gross value-added and representing 22% of employment within the manufacturing sector. The sector provides meaningful export income and supplies raw materials for domestic processing industries. Firms reported operating at higher capacity levels in October 2025 compared to earlier in the year, suggesting some stabilization in business confidence, though this must be understood within the broader context of limited growth and significant downside risks.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Myanmar's major infrastructure includes the Irrawaddy River, which provides important transport and agricultural irrigation capabilities. The primary commercial hub is Yangon, which houses the country's main airport (Yangon International Airport) and primary seaport. The relocation of the administrative capital to Naypyidaw in 2006 has diversified infrastructure investment, though Yangon remains the primary foreign investment location.

Transport connectivity includes road networks linking major urban centers, though road quality varies significantly between urban and rural areas. International connectivity is facilitated through Yangon International Airport, which offers connections to major regional and international destinations. The country has growing telecommunications infrastructure, though service quality and availability vary by region. Foreign investors should assess local infrastructure conditions relevant to their specific business operations and plan accordingly for logistics, communications, and operational support.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Myanmar's legal system is based on common law principles inherited from British colonial rule, combined with customary law traditions and civil law elements introduced by subsequent legislation. The legal framework for business is primarily governed by three key pieces of legislation: the Myanmar Companies Law (MCL), the Myanmar Investment Law (MIL) of 2016, and the Partnerships Act of 1932. Following the military takeover in 2021, the legal system has operated under oversight of the State Administration Council (now the SSPC), and various laws and regulations have been issued through notifications and directives that carry statutory force.

Myanmar's legal environment for foreign investors has evolved significantly with the enactment of the Myanmar Investment Law 2016, which liberalized certain aspects of foreign investment and established a framework for investment promotion and protection. However, the legal system itself exhibits characteristics typical of developing economies: enforcement mechanisms are sometimes inconsistent, court procedures can be lengthy, contract enforcement varies, and the written rule of law coexists with administrative decision-making that may not always follow strictly codified procedures. Additionally, several sectors remain restricted or prohibited for foreign investment, and certain industries require government approval through the Myanmar Investment Commission (MIC) or other regulatory bodies.

The 2025 Union Taxation Law, enacted on 31 March 2025 with effect from 1 April 2025, represents the most recent comprehensive fiscal legislation and governs corporate income tax, commercial tax, specific goods tax, and gemstone tax. Businesses must remain informed of ongoing regulatory changes, as the legal environment continues to evolve, and government notifications and ministry orders frequently introduce modifications to operational requirements, tax regulations, and investment procedures.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

The Myanmar Investment Law 2016 established a framework permitting foreign investment in certain sectors of the Myanmar economy. Foreign investors are permitted to own up to 35% equity in Myanmar-owned limited liability companies directly or indirectly, without affecting the company's status as a local entity. For wholly foreign-owned entities, foreign investors may establish foreign companies or branches in specific permitted sectors, subject to Myanmar Investment Commission approval.

Certain sectors of the economy remain restricted or prohibited for foreign investment, including defence-related industries, telecommunications, air transport, energy generation (with limited exceptions), broadcasting, print media, and trade in certain goods (precious stones, timber, pharmaceuticals). Many other sectors require Myanmar Investment Commission approval before foreign investment is permitted. Prospective investors must conduct thorough sector-specific research and engage with DICA (Directorate of Investment and Company Administration) and the Myanmar Investment Commission to determine whether their proposed investment falls within permitted categories.

Myanmar's Special Economic Zones (SEZs) offer significant investment incentives under the Myanmar Special Economic Zone Law 2014. Three SEZs are operational or in development: the Thilawa SEZ (operational), Kyaukpyu SEZ, and Dawei SEZ. Investors locating businesses in the Free Zone portion of an SEZ receive corporate income tax exemptions for the first 7 years of operation, followed by 50% tax reduction for an additional 5 years (with potential for further extensions). Investors in SEZ Promotion Zones receive corporate income tax exemptions for 5 years, followed by 50% reduction for 5 additional years. SEZ investors are also exempted from customs duties and other taxes on capital goods imports. Additionally, SEZ investors enjoy freedoms to repatriate profits from operations and receive protections against nationalization. Land lease terms in SEZs extend up to 75 years (with 50-year initial terms and 25-year renewal options). Tax deductions are available for staff training and research and development expenses. Investors seeking to maximize incentives should carefully evaluate SEZ location options.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Myanmar offers multiple visa categories for foreign nationals, with the most relevant categories for business persons being the Business Visa and tourist-related visas. The Business Visa is available in single-entry and multi-entry forms and is suitable for short-term visits for business purposes. Citizens of 50 countries are eligible to apply online for a 70-day single-entry business e-visa through the official Myanmar e-visa portal (evisa.moip.gov.mm). Citizens of 48 countries may alternatively obtain a business visa on arrival at Yangon International Airport, while nationals of all countries can apply for a business visa at a Myanmar embassy or consulate abroad.

For foreign nationals intending to remain in Myanmar beyond the initial 70-day business visa period, an Extension of Stay Permit is required to remove the visa time limitation. This permit is obtained through the Myanmar Immigration Office and is available in durations of three months, six months, or one year. The Extension of Stay Permit is not a separate visa category but rather an administrative permit that extends the validity of an existing visa and permits longer-term residence. Processing for stay permits typically requires 3–5 weeks from date of application.

Foreign nationals travelling on other visa categories (tourist, conventional, etc.) may also transition to a business visa or obtain an extension of stay if they secure employment or engage in business activities. However, it is important to note that visa requirements are subject to change, and prospective investors should verify current requirements with the Myanmar Immigration Office or a Myanmar embassy or consulate in their home country before travel. The Myanmar e-visa portal and official immigration authorities provide the most current visa information.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Any foreign national intending to engage in employment or provide services in Myanmar is legally required to obtain a work permit from the Myanmar Ministry of Labour, Employment and Social Security. This requirement applies to all sectors and all job positions, regardless of seniority, professional status, or employment duration. A work permit is distinct from a visa and serves as separate authorization to engage in gainful employment within Myanmar.

The work permit application process requires employer sponsorship. The employer must be duly registered with the Myanmar Ministry of Labour and must submit the work permit application on behalf of the prospective foreign employee. Required documentation typically includes the employee's passport, health certificates, relevant educational and professional credentials, an employment contract (using the mandatory Employment Contract Template issued by the Department of Labour), and evidence of the employer's registration and financial standing. The processing timeline is generally 3–5 weeks from submission to approval.

An important employment law principle in Myanmar is that local and foreign workers performing the same functions at the same level must receive equal compensation. This requirement applies across all employment classifications and is strictly enforced. Foreign workers and their employers must also comply with Myanmar's labour legislation, including provisions regarding working hours (maximum 48 hours per week, with a standard 8-hour workday), rest periods, safety standards, and mandatory employment contract requirements. Employment contracts must be established within 30 days of hire and must comply with the mandatory Employment Contract Template issued by the Department of Labour. The statutory minimum wage in Myanmar is MMK 6,800 per day for an eight-hour workday (approximately USD 3.25 at current exchange rates).

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Myanmar's legal system provides mechanisms for dispute resolution through courts and administrative procedures. Commercial disputes may be resolved through civil litigation in Myanmar courts, with proceedings following civil procedure rules. However, court processes can be lengthy, and enforcement of judgments may be inconsistent or delayed. Myanmar's courts apply law based on the Myanmar Companies Law, Myanmar Investment Law, and other applicable legislation, though administrative decision-making also plays a significant role in business matters.

For investors seeking dispute resolution alternatives, Myanmar does permit international arbitration. The country is party to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (New York Convention), facilitating enforcement of international arbitration awards in Myanmar. Many foreign investors and joint venture agreements include provisions for international arbitration, often conducted in neutral jurisdictions such as Singapore or Bangkok, to provide greater certainty and predictability in dispute outcomes compared to Myanmar domestic litigation.

Mediation and alternative dispute resolution procedures are increasingly recognized in Myanmar, though these remain less developed than in more established dispute resolution regimes. For international contracts and significant transactions, inclusion of arbitration clauses, dispute resolution procedures, and clear contractual specifications is recommended to manage dispute risks effectively.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Myanmar's economy offers opportunities across multiple sectors, with particular strength in agriculture and agrifood processing, energy and infrastructure, manufacturing and light industry, tourism and hospitality, and telecommunications. The agrifood sector contributes approximately 27% of gross value-added and represents significant export potential. Manufacturing sectors including garments, food processing, and assembly operations benefit from Myanmar's lower labour costs and geographic proximity to major Asian markets. Infrastructure development, particularly in energy generation, transportation, and telecommunications, represents a significant investment opportunity as the government prioritizes infrastructure modernization.

Tourism and hospitality sectors offer growth potential given Myanmar's cultural heritage and natural attractions, though political instability and international travel advisories have constrained this sector. Mining and natural resource extraction, particularly in jade and gemstones, represent significant sectoral opportunities, though these sectors face restrictions and require careful regulatory engagement. Real estate and commercial property development in urban centers, particularly Yangon and Mandalay, offer potential for investors with appropriate local partnerships.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

The Myanmar Investment Law 2016 provides the primary framework for investment promotion and protection. The Directorate of Investment and Company Administration (DICA) serves as the primary agency for investment registration and approval. The Myanmar Investment Commission oversees sector approvals and investment policies.

Special Economic Zones offer the most substantial investment incentives, including corporate income tax exemptions for initial periods (5-7 years) with 50% reduction for subsequent periods, exemptions from customs duties on capital equipment imports, freedom to repatriate profits, and protection against expropriation. Land lease terms in SEZs extend to 75 years, providing long-term security for investment.

The government periodically announces sectoral incentive programs and tax holidays for investments in priority sectors. Recent announcements have encouraged investment in manufacturing and agricultural processing. Businesses should engage with DICA and the Myanmar Investment Commission early in the planning process to understand available incentives and position investments to qualify for applicable programs.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

Myanmar operates three Special Economic Zones: the Thilawa SEZ (operational), the Kyaukpyu SEZ, and the Dawei SEZ. The Thilawa SEZ, located near Yangon, is the most developed and provides immediately available infrastructure and incentives. The Kyaukpyu SEZ and Dawei SEZ are in development phases and offer opportunities for investors in specific sectors aligned with government development plans.

SEZ investors benefit from corporate income tax exemptions in Free Zones (7 years) and Promotion Zones (5 years), with subsequent 50% reductions for additional periods. Customs duty exemptions apply to capital goods imports. Investors enjoy freedom to repatriate profits and protection against expropriation. Land lease terms extend to 75 years. Tax deductions apply for staff training and research and development expenses.

Businesses considering SEZ location should evaluate site-specific infrastructure, sector focus areas, and distance to end markets. The Thilawa SEZ's proximity to Yangon provides superior transport connectivity and logistics infrastructure compared to other SEZs.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Myanmar is a member of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) and benefits from ASEAN preferential trade agreements, including the ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA). These agreements provide tariff reduction or elimination for Myanmar exports to other ASEAN members. Myanmar is a founding member of ASEAN and participates in ASEAN+3 and Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) agreements, which provide expanded market access across East and Southeast Asia.

Myanmar participates in the World Trade Organization (WTO) as an observer but has not yet completed the accession process to become a full WTO member. Consequently, most-favoured-nation status is not automatically applied, and tariff treatment depends on bilateral trade relationships. The country maintains bilateral trade agreements with major trading partners including China, Thailand, and India.

Foreign investors should understand Myanmar's tariff regime and trade agreement status when planning import-export activities, supply chains, and market access strategies. ASEAN membership provides advantages for export-oriented manufacturing and regional distribution operations.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Myanmar's banking sector comprises the Central Bank of Myanmar as the regulatory authority, state-owned banks, and private domestic and foreign commercial banks. Foreign banks operate in Myanmar, including subsidiaries and branches of major international financial institutions. The Central Bank of Myanmar publishes daily reference exchange rates through its official forex portal (forex.cbm.gov.mm), which serves as the basis for official foreign exchange transactions.

Foreign exchange management in Myanmar is subject to regulatory controls administered by the Central Bank and the Foreign Exchange Supervisory Committee. As of January 2026, Myanmar implemented relaxed foreign exchange regulations through Notification No. 2/2026. Under current regulations, exporters of goods and services are required to convert only 15% of their foreign currency export earnings into Myanmar Kyat at official Central Bank reference exchange rates. This represents a reduction from the previous 25% mandatory conversion requirement and reflects governmental efforts to ease foreign exchange constraints and encourage foreign currency retention for business operations.

Individuals and businesses may remit foreign currency internationally up to USD 10,000 (or equivalent) per transaction without Central Bank approval. Remittances exceeding USD 10,000 require specific approval from the Central Bank's Foreign Exchange Supervisory Committee. The Committee has authority to approve foreign currency conversions, make exemptions to foreign exchange restrictions, and permit overseas transfers. Businesses engaged in import-export activities should factor foreign exchange management procedures into their operational planning, as conversion requirements, timing, and approval processes can affect cash flow and working capital management.

Myanmar's capital markets remain underdeveloped, with only the Yangon Stock Exchange providing a venue for securities trading. Corporate financing is primarily conducted through bank lending, direct investment, and retained earnings rather than through capital markets. Foreign investors should expect limited options for raising capital through debt or equity markets and should plan accordingly for financing structures.

4. Business Organisations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Myanmar recognizes multiple business entity types suitable for different investment scenarios: Limited Liability Companies (LLCs), Limited Companies (Private and Public), Partnerships (General and Limited), and Branch/Representative Offices of foreign companies.

The Limited Liability Company is the most common and flexible business structure for foreign investors in Myanmar. An LLC is a separate legal entity whose members are not personally liable for the entity's debts, obligations, or liabilities. The LLC structure combines elements of corporate limited liability with partnership-style operational flexibility. LLCs are governed by the Myanmar Companies Law and may be established by foreign investors, either as wholly foreign-owned entities or as joint ventures with local partners.

A wholly foreign-owned LLC provides complete operational control to the foreign investor and is ideal for investors seeking to establish independent subsidiaries or separate operating entities. Foreign-owned LLCs are subject to sector-specific restrictions and must obtain Myanmar Investment Commission approval for certain activities. An LLC established with both foreign and local equity ownership (a joint venture structure) allows foreign investors to own up to 35% equity without affecting the company's status as a local entity, providing local-foreign partnerships. LLCs provide significant operational flexibility in management structure, profit distribution, and decision-making authority, making them suitable for diverse business models.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Formation of a Limited Liability Company requires submission of incorporation documents to the Directorate of Investment and Company Administration (DICA), including the Memorandum and Articles of Association, identification documents for all members/shareholders, residential addresses, and proposed business activities. Applications may be submitted through the Myanmar Companies Online (MyCO) digital platform. Upon approval, a Certificate of Incorporation is issued within 3–5 working days. The company must then register for tax purposes, obtain sector-specific licenses, and register with the Ministry of Labour if it will employ workers. LLCs are subject to annual reporting and compliance requirements, including submission of audited financial statements (for larger entities) and corporate income tax filings.

A Private Limited Company is a corporate structure suitable for small to medium enterprises and is incorporated under the Myanmar Companies Law. A private limited company is a separate legal entity with limited liability protection for shareholders. A private limited company requires a minimum of one director and one shareholder and permits a maximum of 50 shareholders. This structure is appropriate for investors seeking corporate organization with limited liability but who do not require the operational flexibility of an LLC structure. Shares are not publicly traded, and transfers of shares require company approval. Both private and public limited companies must be incorporated through DICA with a Certificate of Incorporation. Directors must be appointed and registered with DICA. Shareholder agreements and company by-laws (Articles of Association) must be drafted and registered. Both structures are subject to annual reporting requirements, including financial statement audits for larger entities, tax compliance, and corporate governance requirements.

A Public Limited Company is suitable for larger enterprises and entities seeking to raise capital from public investment. A PLC requires a minimum of seven shareholders and a minimum paid-up capital of USD 50,000. Public companies may list on the Yangon Stock Exchange and issue shares to the public. However, the requirements and procedures for establishing a PLC are more stringent than for private limited companies, and the oversight of the Securities and Exchange Commission applies. PLCs are uncommon among foreign investors given Myanmar's limited capital markets development and the restrictions on foreign investment in certain sectors.

A Partnership in Myanmar is an association of two or more persons operating jointly for profit. Myanmar recognizes two partnership forms: General Partnerships and Limited Partnerships. In a General Partnership, all partners have unlimited personal liability for partnership debts and obligations, and all partners have equal management rights unless otherwise agreed. In a Limited Partnership, there are two classes of partners: general partners (with unlimited liability and management rights) and limited partners (with liability limited to their capital contribution, who do not participate in management). Partnerships in Myanmar operate under the Partnerships Act of 1932 and are simpler to establish than incorporated entities. Registration of a partnership is not mandatory under the Partnerships Act, though partnerships should register with local tax authorities for taxation and administrative purposes. Partnerships are generally dissolved when all partners agree to dissolution or when the partnership purpose has been achieved. Partnership interests are transferred through agreement of the partners, and creditors may have remedies against individual partners for partnership debts.

Partnerships are less commonly used by foreign investors compared to LLC or limited company structures, particularly for larger investments, as the unlimited liability exposure of general partners creates risk. However, partnerships may be suitable for small professional practices (such as consulting firms, professional service partnerships) or for investors seeking operational simplicity with fewer regulatory requirements. Foreign nationals may establish partnerships with local partners, subject to sector-specific restrictions and investment requirements.

A foreign company may establish a branch office or representative office in Myanmar to conduct business, represent the parent company, or undertake preliminary business development. A branch office is an extension of the foreign company with no separate legal status; the parent company remains directly liable for branch activities. A representative office is a limited presence used for liaison, marketing, or business development purposes and cannot conduct commercial transactions. Registration of a foreign company branch with DICA requires submission of the parent company's articles of association, certificate of incorporation (certified and translated into Myanmar), board resolutions authorizing the branch, and identification of branch management. The process is similar to local company registration, with approval and Certificate of Registration issued by DICA within 3–5 working days. The branch must appoint a local manager and is subject to Myanmar taxation on profits derived from Myanmar-source income. Foreign companies are subject to restrictions in certain sectors, and sectors like telecommunications, insurance, and energy require specific government approval. Branches remain directly liable for Myanmar operations, and parent company guarantees may be required by creditors, partners, or regulatory authorities.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

Myanmar companies are subject to corporate governance requirements established by the Myanmar Companies Law and supplemented by DICA guidelines and ministry notifications. Limited companies and Public Limited Companies must maintain a board of directors, with directors bearing fiduciary duties to the company and shareholders. Directors are responsible for business conduct, financial oversight, and regulatory compliance.

All incorporated entities must maintain registered offices in Myanmar, prepare annual financial statements, file annual corporate returns with DICA, and conduct shareholder/member meetings. Larger companies are required to have annual financial statement audits by certified public accountants. Changes in ownership, management, or company information must be reported to DICA. Companies must maintain statutory registers including shareholder registers, director registers, and meeting minutes.

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Myanmar's labour law framework is governed by multiple pieces of legislation rather than a single comprehensive employment code. The Employment and Skills Development Law (2013) and the Labour Organization Law (2011) form the primary statutory framework, supplemented by ministerial notifications and departmental rules issued by the Ministry of Labour. Key provisions include requirements for written employment contracts, maximum working hours of 48 per week with 8-hour standard days, minimum wage protections, workplace safety standards, and social security contributions.

The statutory minimum wage is set at MMK 6,800 per day for an eight-hour workday, though this applies to general employment and certain sectors may have different arrangements. Foreign and local workers must receive equal pay for equal work at the same level, a principle strictly applied. Employers must establish employment contracts using the mandatory Employment Contract Template within 30 days of hire. The template is issued by the Department of Labour and covers essential terms including wages, hours, duties, leave entitlements, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

Labour standards in Myanmar are enforced through the Ministry of Labour, which conducts workplace inspections and investigates complaints. Disputes between employees and employers may be resolved through internal grievance procedures or through formal complaints to labour dispute resolution bodies. Foreign employers should implement compliant HR practices, ensure proper contracting, maintain wage records, and provide safe working conditions. The labour law framework continues to evolve, and businesses should monitor announcements from the Ministry of Labour regarding new requirements or policy changes. Additionally, whilst Myanmar has a young population and significant labour supply, management of a foreign workforce may require patient engagement with local administrative procedures, cultural adaptation, and clear communication of expectations.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Myanmar's intellectual property framework has expanded significantly in recent years. The Intellectual Property Department (IPD), established in 2023, administers Myanmar's IP laws. Myanmar operates a first-to-file trademark registration system under which trademark protection is granted upon registration (rather than based on evidence of prior use). Trademark registrations are valid for 10 years from the filing date and may be renewed indefinitely. Trademark licenses must be recorded with the IPD.

The Patent Law, effective since 2020, protects inventions for a term of 20 years from the filing date. Myanmar also enforces an Industrial Design Law, which became operational on February 1, 2024. Copyright protection is available under the Myanmar Copyright Act (1914), though Myanmar has not acceded to major international copyright treaties such as the Berne Convention. Consequently, foreign copyright works receive limited protection unless Myanmar recognizes the work through bilateral arrangements. The copyright regime does not provide registration procedures; rights arise automatically upon creation.

However, prospective investors should understand that Myanmar's IP enforcement infrastructure remains nascent. The Intellectual Property Department is still in its formative stages, and enforcement mechanisms—including litigation before the IP Tribunal and administrative procedures—are not fully operational. In practice, IP enforcement through courts or administrative bodies can be slow, inconsistent, or limited in availability. Businesses should take additional precautions to protect their intellectual property, including entering into confidentiality and licensing agreements with local partners, implementing robust internal IP management procedures, and carefully structuring business relationships to minimize exposure of sensitive IP. Registration of IP rights with the IPD is important and recommended, but should be understood as creating a legal right rather than ensuring practical enforcement against infringement.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Myanmar's tax system is governed by the 2025 Union Taxation Law, enacted on 31 March 2025 and effective from 1 April 2025. The system comprises corporate income tax, commercial tax (a turnover tax on goods and services), specific goods tax, gemstone tax, withholding taxes, personal income tax, and various other taxes including stamp duty, land tax, and trade license fees. The financial year runs from 1 April to 31 March.

Key administrative features include quarterly corporate income tax payments, annual tax returns filed by 30 June, and maintenance of accounting records for a minimum of five years. The tax system has undergone recent modifications, and businesses should remain informed of regulatory changes through the tax authority, DICA, and ministry announcements.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

Myanmar's corporate income tax rate for the 2025-2026 financial year (1 April 2025 to 31 March 2026) is 22% for general business entities. This rate applies to resident companies (companies incorporated in Myanmar or with management and control in Myanmar) on their worldwide income and to non-resident companies on Myanmar-source income only. Certain sectors have different rates: companies listed on the Yangon Stock Exchange are taxed at 17%, while companies engaged in oil and gas exploration and production are subject to 25%.

Corporate income tax is assessed on net income calculated as gross revenue minus allowable deductions. Allowable deductions include operating expenses, cost of goods sold, depreciation of capital assets (using prescribed rates), and other ordinary business expenses. However, certain expenses are not deductible, including income taxes themselves, donations (except to approved charities), entertainment expenses above certain thresholds, penalties, fines, and losses from related-party transactions at non-arm's-length prices.

Corporate income tax must be paid quarterly in advance, with payments due within 10 days following the end of each quarter (quarter ending 30 June, 30 September, 31 December, and 31 March). An annual corporate income tax return must be filed with the tax authority by 30 June following the end of the financial year. The return must include detailed financial statements, computation of taxable income, depreciation schedules, and supporting documentation. Companies must maintain comprehensive accounting records for a minimum of five years for tax examination purposes.

Capital gains are subject to a separate capital gains tax regime. The general capital gains tax rate is 10% on gains from sale of business assets, though oil and gas sector gains face rates of 40-50%. Losses may be carried forward to subsequent years for offset against future capital gains.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

Personal income tax rates for the 2025-2026 financial year range from 0% to 25% using a progressive rate structure applied to resident individuals. Residents are taxed on worldwide income, including salary, business income, investment income, and rental income. Non-resident Myanmar citizens employed overseas are eligible for a preferential regime: they may be taxed at the lower of (1) the applicable progressive rates (0-25%) with deductions for personal relief and allowances, or (2) a flat rate of 2% without deductions. Foreign income (other than employment salary) received by non-resident Myanmar citizens is taxed at a flat 10% rate without allowances or relief.

Non-resident foreign nationals are subject to different treatment. Non-resident foreigners are taxed only on income derived from Myanmar sources. Non-resident foreigners are taxed at the greater of (1) the progressive personal income tax rates (0-25%), or (2) a flat rate of 35%. An important change effective from 1 October 2023 is that the previous tax exemption for non-resident foreign nationals on foreign salary income has been eliminated. Under the Amended Union Taxation Law 2023, Myanmar now imposes income tax on salary income earned by non-resident foreign nationals in foreign countries; however, foreign income taxes paid are permitted as credits against Myanmar tax liability.

Personal income tax is assessed on an annual basis. Resident individuals with income above prescribed thresholds must file annual personal income tax returns by 30 June following the close of the tax year. Deductions are available for personal relief (family members, dependents), pension contributions, donations, and certain other permitted expenses.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

Commercial Tax: Myanmar does not have a value-added tax (VAT) or goods and services tax (GST). Instead, Myanmar employs a Commercial Tax regime, which is a turnover tax levied on the value of goods sold and services provided. The standard commercial tax rate is 5%, applied to most goods and services. However, the rate varies by type of good or service: rates range from 0% to 15%, with specified goods and services subject to different rates.

The commercial tax system includes a comprehensive list of exempted goods and services. Specifically, 47 types of goods are fully exempted from commercial tax, and 34 categories of services are exempt, including life insurance, banking and financial services, microfinance, public transportation, publishing services, and certain educational activities. Internet services are taxed at a higher rate of 15% commercial tax. Small businesses with annual production and sales proceeds below MMK 50 million are exempt from commercial tax registration and filing, though they remain liable for tax if thresholds are exceeded.

Commercial tax is collected at each stage of production and distribution, and businesses are responsible for calculating, reporting, and remitting tax. Larger businesses are required to maintain invoices, sales records, and purchase documentation to substantiate tax calculations. Quarterly or annual tax returns must be filed depending on business size and categorization.

Withholding Taxes: Myanmar does not impose a withholding tax on dividends paid to shareholders (including foreign shareholders). This represents a significant advantage for investors seeking to distribute profits internationally. However, Myanmar does impose income tax on payments to foreign service providers, contractors, and recipients of certain types of income.

Specifically, tax withholding requirements apply to payments for services rendered by non-residents, interest, rental payments, royalties, and certain management fees. The withholding rate is generally 10% for non-residents on service income, though rates may vary by income type. Tax withheld from payments to non-resident recipients is treated as a final tax (not creditable against other Myanmar tax obligations), whereas tax withheld from payments to residents may be credited against their final tax liability. Tax paid in foreign jurisdictions by Myanmar residents on foreign-source income is permitted to be offset against Myanmar tax liability under tax credit provisions.

Employers must withhold personal income tax from wages and salaries paid to employees and remit the withheld amounts to tax authorities. Income tax withholding on employee compensation is mandatory and applies to all employment income above exempt thresholds.

Special Goods Tax: Myanmar imposes a specific goods tax on luxury and consumption goods, including vehicles, alcohol, tobacco, cosmetics, and certain electronics. Rates vary by product category, ranging from 10% to 50%, with higher rates on luxury items and goods considered harmful (alcohol and tobacco). Special goods tax is levied at the point of importation or manufacture.

Gemstone Tax: Myanmar imposes specific taxation on the extraction, export, and trading of gemstones, reflecting Myanmar's significant jade and ruby production. Rates and procedures are prescribed by government notifications.

Stamp Duty: Documentary stamp duties are imposed on certain legal instruments, including contracts, deeds, agreements, and official documents. Rates vary based on document type and value. Stamp duty is typically a percentage of the transaction value or a fixed amount based on document classification.

Land Tax and Property Tax: Owners of land and immovable property are subject to land tax assessed annually based on property value or rental income. Different rates apply to different categories of property (agricultural, residential, commercial).

Trade License Tax: All businesses are required to obtain an annual trade license from local municipal or township authorities. A trade license fee is assessed based on business type and anticipated turnover. Annual renewal is mandatory.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

Special Economic Zone Incentives: Investors locating businesses in SEZs receive significant tax benefits. Free Zone investors receive corporate income tax exemptions for 7 years followed by 50% reduction for 5 years. Promotion Zone investors receive exemptions for 5 years followed by 50% reduction for 5 years. All SEZ investors are exempted from customs duties on capital goods imports and enjoy freedom to repatriate profits with protection against expropriation.

Sectoral Incentives: The government periodically announces sectoral incentive programs for priority investments in manufacturing, agriculture, and infrastructure. Businesses should engage with DICA and the Myanmar Investment Commission to determine available incentives for their specific investment.

General Incentives: Tax deductions are available for staff training and research and development expenses. Capital allowances apply to capital asset depreciation using prescribed rates. Loss carryforward provisions permit offset of losses against future income.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Tax Residency: A company is considered a resident of Myanmar if it is incorporated in Myanmar or has its management and control located in Myanmar. Resident companies are taxed on worldwide income. Non-resident companies are taxed only on Myanmar-source income.

Foreign Tax Credits: Myanmar residents and companies are permitted to credit foreign income taxes paid against Myanmar tax liability. This provision addresses potential double taxation on foreign-source income.

Treaty Benefits: Myanmar is not party to many bilateral tax treaties, limiting treaty-based relief mechanisms. Investors should verify whether their home country has a tax treaty with Myanmar and whether specific treaty benefits apply to their investment structure.

Transfer Pricing: Related-party transactions are subject to arm's-length price requirements. Transactions at non-arm's-length prices are not permitted deductions, and the tax authority has authority to adjust transfer prices. Businesses with significant related-party transactions should implement transfer pricing documentation and analysis.

Foreign Exchange and Profit Repatriation: Myanmar does not impose withholding tax on dividends paid to foreign shareholders, permitting tax-efficient profit repatriation. However, foreign exchange regulations limit the amount of foreign currency that may be remitted internationally without Central Bank approval (USD 10,000 per transaction). Larger remittances require Central Bank approval through the Foreign Exchange Supervisory Committee.

6. Tax Compliance and Reporting

Tax compliance in Myanmar requires meticulous attention to filing deadlines, calculation methodologies, and documentation requirements. The financial year runs from 1 April to 31 March. Quarterly corporate income tax payments are due within 10 days following the end of each quarter (30 June, 30 September, 31 December, and 31 March). Annual corporate income tax returns must be filed by 30 June. Commercial tax returns (quarterly or annual, depending on entity classification) must be filed within prescribed timeframes. All businesses must maintain accounting records compliant with Myanmar accounting standards and retain documentation for a minimum of five years.

Documentation requirements include invoices (issued for all sales), purchase receipts, expense documentation, payroll records, bank statements, and contracts. Large companies (often defined by revenue thresholds) must have financial statements audited by certified public accountants. Tax authorities conduct routine audits and are empowered to assess additional tax, penalties, and interest for non-compliance, late filing, or underreported income. Tax disputes may be appealed through administrative procedures or, ultimately, through court proceedings.

Recent changes include the 2025 Union Taxation Law enacted 31 March 2025 (effective 1 April 2025), which clarified tax rates, revised some procedures, and introduced modifications to withholding tax treatment and capital gains taxation. The foreign exchange regulatory changes of January 2026 (allowing exporters to retain 85% of export earnings instead of converting 75%) affect cash flow and are important for import-export businesses. Tax amnesty programmes are periodically announced by the government and may provide opportunities for businesses with historical tax liabilities to settle obligations under specified terms.

Non-compliance with tax obligations carries significant penalties. Late payment of tax results in interest charges (typically monthly) and potential penalties. Underreporting of income or non-filing can result in substantial penalties and, in cases of willful evasion, criminal prosecution. Businesses should maintain professional tax advisory support and ensure scrupulous compliance with filing requirements, estimated payments, and documentation standards.
