Doing Business in Brunei Darussalam

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Brunei Darussalam, officially known as Brunei Darussalam (meaning "Brunei the Blessed"), is a sovereign state located on the north coast of the island of Borneo in Southeast Asia. The country occupies a small but strategically significant territory, with a total land area of approximately 5,765 square kilometers. It is divided into four districts: Brunei-Muara (which contains the capital, Bandar Seri Begawan), Belait, Tutong, and Temburong. The capital, Bandar Seri Begawan, serves as the nation's administrative, commercial, and cultural hub.

The country has a population of approximately 450,000 people, with a highly urbanized society concentrated primarily in the capital and surrounding areas. The population is predominantly Malay (67.4%), with significant Chinese (10.3%) and other ethnic communities. The official language is Malay, though English is widely spoken in business and government contexts. The state religion is Islam, enshrined in the national philosophy of "Melayu Islam Beraja" (Malay Islamic Monarchy), which shapes governance, legal frameworks, and social norms.

Brunei's strategic location on Borneo provides access to major sea routes and regional markets. The country is bordered by the Malaysian states of Sarawak and Sabah, and its proximity to major Southeast Asian markets—particularly Malaysia, Singapore, and Indonesia—makes it a natural gateway for regional trade and investment. The country also benefits from deep-water ports and modern transportation infrastructure that facilitate international commerce.

The climate is tropical, characterized by high humidity, abundant rainfall, and consistent temperatures year-round. The state maintains a high standard of living relative to other developing nations in the region, with per capita GDP among the highest in Asia-Pacific. Modern infrastructure, including well-developed port facilities, telecommunications networks, and transportation systems, supports business operations and international connectivity. The government has invested substantially in digital infrastructure, with widespread broadband coverage and growing e-commerce capabilities supporting business modernization.

1.2 Political System & Government

Brunei Darussalam is an absolute monarchy operating under a constitutional framework established by the 1959 Constitution. The Sultan of Brunei is the head of state, head of government, and supreme authority in all governmental matters. Since 1984, Sultan Hassanal Bolkiah has served as both Sultan and Prime Minister, holding portfolio control over the Ministries of Defense and Finance. The Sultan's authority is comprehensive, encompassing executive, legislative, and to some extent judicial powers, though the constitution provides certain checks and frameworks for governance.

The governance structure is guided by the national philosophy of "Melayu Islam Beraja," which integrates Malay cultural identity, Islamic principles, and the institution of the monarchy as the foundation of the state. This philosophy influences all aspects of government policy, legal development, and public administration. The government operates under a Council of Cabinet Ministers, led by the Sultan as Prime Minister, which includes senior ministers, ministers, and deputy ministers responsible for various portfolios including Home Affairs, Development, Industry and Primary Resources, Culture, Youth and Sports, and other critical departments. The Crown Prince serves as Senior Minister and is integral to the succession and governance structure.

Multiple advisory and specialized councils support executive governance. The Religious Council advises on matters of Islamic law and religious policy; the Privy Council addresses constitutional and legal matters; and the Council of Succession determines matters related to succession to the throne. These bodies ensure consultation and deliberation on major policy decisions while maintaining the Sultan's ultimate authority. The government is highly centralized, with decision-making authority concentrated at the national level and decentralized district-level administration supporting implementation of national policies.

Political parties are not permitted to operate in Brunei, and the state has been under emergency rule since 1962, though this operates largely in the background. Individual political participation is limited compared to democratic systems, but the government conducts regular consultations with stakeholders in the business community, civil society, and professional organizations. The government prioritizes stability, national development, and economic growth, positioning Brunei as a strategic partner in ASEAN affairs and a contributor to regional security and economic cooperation. The recent introduction of long-term visa options and employment policy reforms reflect the government's openness to attracting foreign talent and investment to support economic diversification objectives.

1.3 Economy Overview

Brunei's economy is heavily dependent on hydrocarbon exports, with crude oil and natural gas production accounting for approximately 65% of GDP and 95% of exports. Japan remains the primary export market for Brunei's petroleum products, followed by other Asian and global markets. The country's substantial per capita GDP—among the highest in the world—is supported not only by hydrocarbon revenues but also by substantial income from overseas investment managed through the Brunei Investment Agency and other sovereign wealth mechanisms. As of 2024, GDP growth reached 4.2%, the highest rate in 25 years, reflecting both improved global energy prices and growing contributions from non-hydrocarbon sectors.

The non-oil and gas sector has grown to account for 56.6% of GDP as of recent data, representing significant diversification progress. Non-oil and gas exports have surged from just 3% of total exports in 2017 to 61% in 2023, primarily driven by downstream petrochemical industries established in industrial parks. This diversification reflects the success of the government's long-term economic strategy under Wawasan Brunei 2035, which aims to create a dynamic, resilient economy less dependent on volatile hydrocarbon markets. Key growth sectors include downstream oil and gas processing, food production (particularly halal products), services, tourism and hospitality, and information and communications technology.

However, the fiscal situation presents challenges. The fiscal deficit widened to 13.0% of GDP in fiscal year 2024 and is projected to increase further to 13.9% in fiscal year 2025, driven by global energy price pressures and expanded government spending on economic diversification and social programs. The government manages this through its substantial sovereign wealth reserves and the General Reserve Fund, which serves as a long-term stabilization mechanism for future generations. The Brunei Darussalam Central Bank (BDCB) maintains monetary stability and implements prudent fiscal policies to support sustainable economic growth.

The currency is the Brunei Dollar (BND), which is pegged at parity to the Singapore Dollar (SGD) and is fully convertible. As of March 2026, the exchange rate is approximately 1 BND to 0.79 USD. The banking system is sophisticated, with both conventional and Islamic financial institutions operating under a dual regulatory framework. Inflation remains modest, and the country maintains strong macroeconomic stability despite external pressures. The government's focus on economic diversification, investment in human capital, and development of high-value industries positions Brunei for sustainable long-term growth beyond the hydrocarbon era.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Brunei maintains modern and well-developed infrastructure supporting business operations and international connectivity. The country's primary airport is Brunei International Airport (located in Bandar Seri Begawan), which offers direct international flights to major regional and global destinations including Malaysia, Singapore, Thailand, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and Middle Eastern cities. The airport handles both passenger and cargo operations, with modern facilities and efficient customs clearance procedures. Regional connectivity is further enhanced through proximity to other major regional airports and through road links with Malaysia.

Maritime connectivity is a significant strength. The Port of Muara, located in Brunei-Muara District, serves as the nation's principal deepwater port and is one of the most important ports in Southeast Asia. It handles general cargo, containers, bulk cargo, and liquid cargo operations, providing direct connections to major international shipping lines and serving the region's maritime trade. The ongoing development of Pulau Muara Besar (PMB) as a world-class deepwater container port and logistics hub will further enhance Brunei's maritime capabilities and positioning as a regional logistics center. This development, once complete, will offer significantly expanded container capacity and modern port infrastructure.

Telecommunications infrastructure is advanced and widely available. The country has extensive broadband coverage, with competitive internet service providers offering high-speed connectivity and mobile services. 4G and emerging 5G networks provide mobile connectivity to the vast majority of the population and business areas. Digital infrastructure supports e-commerce, online business operations, and digital government services. The government has invested in smart city initiatives and digital transformation to enhance business competitiveness and citizen services.

Road infrastructure within Brunei is modern and well-maintained, with a network of highways connecting the four districts and providing seamless access within the country. Cross-border road connections link Brunei to Malaysian states of Sarawak and Sabah, facilitating land-based trade and travel. Utilities including water, electricity, and sewage systems are reliable and meet international standards. The government maintains strict standards for infrastructure development, with significant investment in maintenance and upgrades to support both population growth and business expansion. Transportation logistics networks are efficient, and customs procedures at borders and ports operate under modern systems designed to facilitate trade while maintaining security and regulatory compliance.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Brunei operates a unique dual legal system comprising statutory laws derived from common law principles and a separate system of Syariah (Islamic) law. The statutory law system, which governs most commercial and civil matters, is based on English common law and equity principles as administered in England prior to April 25, 1951. This foundation provides familiarity to international businesses and investors accustomed to common law jurisdictions. Statutory laws are codified through various Acts and Orders administered by the government and interpreted by the civil courts, creating a predictable and comprehensible legal framework for commerce and dispute resolution.

The Syariah law system operates as a parallel, independent system with specific jurisdiction over matters of personal law affecting Muslims. Historically, Syariah courts held limited jurisdiction over marriage, divorce, inheritance, maintenance of dependents, and estates of deceased Muslims. However, the introduction of the Syariah Penal Code Order 2013 significantly expanded Syariah court jurisdiction to include serious criminal offenses. Syariah courts in Brunei consist of the Syariah Subordinate Courts (handling lower-level matters), the Syariah High Court (appellate jurisdiction), and the Syariah Appeal Court (final appellate body for Syariah matters). Importantly, Syariah courts have no jurisdiction over commercial disputes between businesses; all business-to-business disputes remain exclusively within the civil court system.

The civil court system is structured hierarchically with the Magistrate's Court handling lower-value disputes and criminal matters, the High Court managing significant civil and criminal cases, and the Supreme Court serving as the final appellate authority. The Supreme Court's decisions establish precedent and are binding on lower courts. Judges are appointed by the Sultan and are typically trained in both common law principles and Brunei-specific statutes. The legal profession comprises barristers and solicitors, both of whom can conduct advocacy in superior courts and provide legal services to clients. The legal system operates with procedural rules and evidentiary standards aligned with common law traditions, providing clarity and consistency for businesses engaged in litigation or dispute resolution.

The government maintains a clear separation between statutory and Syariah systems to avoid conflicts and ensure each system operates within its proper sphere. For foreign investors and businesses, this means that commercial disputes, contracts, and business-related legal matters will be determined under statutory law principles. However, foreign investors doing business in Brunei should be aware that Islamic principles and the Syariah framework inform broader government policy, particularly regarding sectors such as financial services, food and beverages, entertainment, and other areas where alignment with Islamic principles is important. Many foreign companies have successfully adapted their operations to comply with both statutory legal requirements and broader Islamic governance expectations.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

Brunei welcomes foreign direct investment and has implemented a policy framework designed to attract international capital and business expertise. There is no restriction on foreign ownership of companies incorporated in Brunei, meaning 100% foreign ownership is permitted. Foreign investors can establish wholly owned subsidiaries, branch offices, or representative offices to conduct business. However, certain requirements apply to ensure proper governance and local integration.

Under the Companies Act (Chapter 39), at least one of the two directors of a locally incorporated company must be a resident of Brunei, unless an exemption is granted by the appropriate authorities. This requirement ensures some degree of local presence and accountability. Foreigners may serve as company directors if at least one co-director is a Brunei resident. Foreign investors cannot establish sole proprietorships or partnerships in Brunei; business structures must be limited companies, branches, or representative offices. This requirement ensures clear legal liability and accountability structures aligned with corporate governance standards.

The Ministry of Finance and Economy, through the Brunei Economic Development Board (BEDB), actively promotes foreign direct investment in the economy. The government offers various investment incentives under the Investment Incentive Order 2001, supporting foreign enterprises in strategically important industrial and economic sectors. These incentives can include tax holidays, accelerated depreciation allowances, and other benefits for investments that contribute to economic diversification, employment creation, and technology transfer. Specific incentives are available for businesses in priority sectors including downstream oil and gas, halal manufacturing and food processing, tourism and hospitality, Islamic finance, and information and communications technology.

Recent policy reforms have enhanced the attractiveness of Brunei for foreign investors and expatriate professionals. In March 2025, Brunei introduced a new long-term, multi-entry visa for foreigners with family, business, or professional ties to the country. This visa category specifically includes provisions for business owners and high-demand professionals, extending renewable work permits up to age 65 (previously capped at age 60). These reforms reflect the government's commitment to attracting and retaining foreign talent necessary to support economic diversification and business growth. Foreign investors should engage with the BEDB or Ministry of Finance and Economy to understand current incentives, exemptions, and any sector-specific provisions that may apply to their proposed investments.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Foreign nationals seeking to work in Brunei must obtain appropriate immigration authorization and employment documentation. The primary mechanism is the work permit, issued by the Ministry of Home Affairs (specifically the Immigration and National Registration Department). Work permits are employment-specific, meaning they authorize a foreign national to work for a specific employer in a designated position and are typically tied to a defined employment term. The permit must be sponsored by the employing company, which serves as the responsible party for the foreign worker's presence and conduct in the country.

Work permit requirements vary based on the worker's skill level, occupation, and the employer's capacity. Generally, employers must demonstrate that the position cannot be adequately filled by Brunei citizens or permanent residents, and that the foreign worker possesses specialized skills or expertise that benefit the company and the broader economy. The immigration authorities conduct assessments of the labor market and the employer's training capacity for citizens. Priority sectors such as tourism, technology, professional services, and upstream/downstream oil and gas operations may have streamlined processes or more favorable criteria for work permit approval.

The 2025 immigration reforms introduced a significant change by extending work permit eligibility up to age 65 for foreign business owners and high-demand professionals. This extension provides greater continuity for senior management and specialized experts critical to business operations. Work permits are typically issued for defined periods (commonly 1-2 years) and are renewable subject to continued employment and satisfaction of all regulatory requirements. Foreign workers must maintain legal status throughout their employment; any changes in employment status or employer require new permit applications.

Complementing the work permit system, Brunei introduced a long-term visa category in March 2025 designed for foreigners with sustained business, professional, or family connections to the country. This five-year, multi-entry visa provides greater flexibility and residence stability compared to traditional business visit visas. The visa can be renewed and allows foreigners to maintain legal residence while conducting business, pursuing professional opportunities, or maintaining family connections. This visa option is particularly valuable for entrepreneurs, investors, and expatriate managers who plan extended stays in Brunei.

Foreign workers must comply with all statutory requirements including tax obligations (where applicable), social security contributions for citizens and permanent residents, employment law provisions, and immigration regulations. Employers are responsible for ensuring their foreign workers maintain valid work authorization at all times. Non-compliance can result in penalties for employers and deportation for workers. It is essential for foreign investors and companies to work closely with immigration counsel or the Ministry of Home Affairs to ensure proper documentation and ongoing compliance with evolving immigration regulations.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Foreign investors face significant restrictions on property and land ownership in Brunei, reflecting the government's policy prioritizing Brunei citizen ownership of real estate. Foreigners cannot own freehold land in Brunei. The only property ownership avenue available to non-citizens is leasehold purchase of strata-titled units (apartments and condominiums) under 99-year leases, with the possibility of renewal subject to government approval. Individual houses and land parcels remain restricted to Brunei citizens and permanent residents.

The strata title system allows foreigners to purchase units in multi-unit residential or commercial complexes where the building itself is divided into individual units with separate ownership. Upon purchase, a foreigner receives a long-term lease (up to 99 years) granting exclusive use and enjoyment of the unit, along with proportional rights to common areas. This leasehold interest can be sold, transferred, or mortgaged, though buyer approval from relevant authorities is typically required. Strata-titled properties in Bandar Seri Begawan and other developed areas provide viable investment or residence options for foreign nationals and investors.

Brunei implemented significant land law reforms effective August 1, 2025, through the Land Code (Amendment) Order 2025 (LCAO 2025). These reforms clarify ownership rights, address longstanding ambiguities regarding stateless permanent residents and foreign nationals, and prevent nominee arrangements that circumvent ownership restrictions. Under the new rules, Brunei citizens cannot hold land as nominees on behalf of foreigners or permanent residents. All non-citizens holding land through Power of Attorney or other arrangements were required to declare such holdings within 12 months (by August 1, 2026). These reforms strengthen the legal framework and ensure transparent ownership registration.

Commercial property ownership presents additional complexities. Foreigners may lease commercial spaces (offices, retail, warehouses) through conventional rental arrangements, but ownership of commercial land or buildings is generally restricted to citizens. However, foreign investors in priority sectors may qualify for land allocation within designated industrial parks or special economic zones under specific terms. For example, companies establishing operations in the Sungai Liang Industrial Park (SPARK) or Pulau Muara Besar (PMB) may lease dedicated industrial land for extended periods, effectively providing long-term secure tenure for business operations.

Prospective foreign investors should conduct due diligence on any property transaction and ensure compliance with land laws. Engagement with local legal counsel is essential to verify that any proposed acquisition comports with current regulations and that all necessary approvals are obtained. The Brunei Land Registry maintains records of all land transactions and registrations, and title verification is important before entering into any property agreement. The government's recent emphasis on clarifying and strengthening land ownership rules suggests an intention to prevent abuses while maintaining the core policy of prioritizing citizen property ownership.

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Brunei provides multiple mechanisms for business dispute resolution, including litigation through the civil courts, arbitration, and mediation. The choice of forum and procedure depends on the nature of the dispute, the parties' agreement, and the governing law of relevant contracts.

Civil Litigation: The standard mechanism for resolving business disputes is litigation through Brunei's civil courts. The High Court has jurisdiction over most commercial disputes, with the Supreme Court serving as the final appellate authority. Litigation follows civil procedure rules based on common law traditions, with discovery procedures, expert testimony, and established evidentiary standards. The courts apply statutory law and established case law precedent. Litigation can be time-consuming and costly, but the judicial system is generally competent and provides a transparent forum for dispute resolution. However, the courts can become congested with heavy caseloads, potentially extending timelines for resolution.

Arbitration: Brunei recognizes international arbitration as a mechanism for dispute resolution. The country is party to the Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (the New York Convention), which facilitates enforcement of arbitration awards. The Brunei Darussalam Arbitration Centre provides facilities and administrative support for domestic and international arbitration. Many commercial contracts in Brunei include arbitration clauses specifying arbitration as the dispute resolution mechanism. Arbitration offers advantages including confidentiality, expert decision-makers (arbitrators), flexibility in procedures, and enforceability of awards across jurisdictions that are signatories to the New York Convention. Arbitration is particularly attractive for international commercial disputes involving parties from multiple countries.

Mediation: The Brunei Mediation Bureau facilitates mediation and alternative dispute resolution processes. Mediation provides a consensual, confidential process in which a neutral third party assists disputing parties in reaching negotiated settlement. Mediation is less formal and typically faster and less expensive than litigation or arbitration. Many commercial relationships benefit from mediation, particularly where the parties wish to preserve ongoing business relationships. The government encourages mediation to alleviate court congestion and provide efficient, amicable resolution of disputes.

Choice of Law: Parties to contracts can typically specify the governing law applicable to their agreement. Many international contracts are governed by English law (a common choice among Brunei-based businesses), Singapore law, or Brunei law. Clarity on governing law is essential, as it determines which substantive legal rules apply to contract interpretation and dispute resolution. Parties can also specify the forum (courts, arbitration, mediation) in their contract, providing predictability and control over dispute procedures.

Foreign investors should structure their contracts carefully to specify dispute resolution mechanisms, governing law, and forum preferences. Engagement with legal counsel experienced in Brunei law is recommended when entering significant business transactions or relationships in the country. Understanding the available dispute resolution options and selecting mechanisms appropriate to the specific business context will help protect the investor's interests and provide a clear pathway to resolution if disputes arise.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Brunei's investment environment is shaped by its economic diversification strategy under Wawasan Brunei 2035, which identifies five priority sectors for development beyond traditional hydrocarbon industries. These sectors represent the primary focus of government incentives, infrastructure investment, and business promotion efforts.

Downstream Oil and Gas: Despite the government's diversification objectives, downstream oil and gas remains a critical economic sector. Brunei's established upstream extraction capabilities have been complemented by the development of downstream petrochemical and refining industries, primarily concentrated in the Sungai Liang Industrial Park. Companies involved in processing, refining, and value-added petrochemical production have demonstrated strong growth and export performance. The sector benefits from access to feedstocks, proximity to regional markets, and established infrastructure. International partnerships with multinational oil and gas companies provide technology transfer and market access. New downstream investments and expansion of existing facilities remain priority opportunities.

Halal Food Manufacturing and Processing: Brunei aspires to position itself as a regional hub for halal food production, capitalizing on growing global demand for Shariah-compliant products and the country's credibility as an Islamic nation. The halal food sector encompasses production of halal-certified meat and seafood products, processed foods, beverages, nutritional supplements, and specialty food items. Brunei's advantages include proximity to regional raw material sources, strong regulatory frameworks for halal certification, and access to markets within ASEAN, the Middle East, and Islamic diaspora communities globally. Government incentives and BEDB support for halal enterprises help reduce entry barriers. Investment opportunities range from production facilities to processing and value-added services.

Tourism and Hospitality: Brunei is developing its tourism sector as a significant economic contributor. The country offers natural attractions including pristine rainforests, unique biodiversity, heritage sites associated with Islamic architecture and culture, and adventure tourism opportunities (river cruises, diving, diving). The government has invested in hospitality infrastructure and is promoting Brunei as an emerging destination for regional and international travelers. Opportunities include hotel and resort development, tour operators, hospitality services, entertainment facilities, and related industries. The government's investment in tourism infrastructure and the introduction of extended visa options for tourists and business visitors are designed to increase visitor arrivals and tourism revenue.

Information and Communications Technology (ICT): Brunei is developing its ICT sector as a driver of economic diversification and digital transformation. The government has invested in broadband infrastructure, digital governance platforms, and e-commerce ecosystems. Private sector opportunities include software development, digital services, telecommunications, fintech, cybersecurity, and business process outsourcing. The country's geographic position, English-language proficiency, growing digital infrastructure, and regional market access make it attractive for ICT businesses. Government initiatives supporting digital entrepreneurship and innovation are expanding opportunities in this sector.

Islamic Finance: Brunei is positioning itself as a hub for Islamic financial services within Southeast Asia. The Islamic banking and finance sector, including Shariah-compliant banks, takaful (Islamic insurance) providers, sukuk (Islamic bonds), and investment dealers, has grown substantially. Islamic finance accounted for over BND13 billion (approximately USD10.3 billion) of market share in 2023, with Islamic banks and financial institutions representing over 95% of Islamic financial assets. The government's commitment to developing this sector, combined with Brunei's credibility as an Islamic nation, creates opportunities for financial service providers, investment firms, and supporting professional services. The upcoming second financial sector blueprint (to be released in Q1 2026) will likely outline additional development priorities and investment opportunities in Islamic finance.

Additional emerging sectors include renewable energy and sustainability initiatives, healthcare and wellness services, creative industries, and education. Brunei's commitment to sustainable development and environmental responsibility is creating opportunities in clean energy, green technology, and sustainability-focused businesses.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

The government of Brunei actively promotes foreign investment through a structured incentive framework and institutional support mechanisms. The Ministry of Finance and Economy, in collaboration with the Brunei Economic Development Board (BEDB), administers investment incentives and provides support to foreign investors and businesses.

Investment Incentive Order 2001: The primary statutory framework for investment incentives is the Investment Incentive Order 2001, which provides tax and other benefits for enterprises investing in strategically important industrial and economic sectors. The order allows the government to grant exemptions, reductions, or deferrals of taxes on corporate income and other taxes for approved investments. Incentives can include full or partial exemption from corporate income tax for defined periods (typically 5-10 years depending on the sector and investment scale), accelerated depreciation allowances for capital equipment, exemption of import duties on specified capital equipment and materials, and other fiscal benefits. The specific incentives granted depend on the sector, investment size, employment creation, technology contribution, and strategic importance of the project.

Tax Exemptions for New Companies: Newly incorporated companies benefit from automatic exemptions on the first BND100,000 of chargeable income during the first three consecutive years of assessment. This provision reduces the tax burden for startup enterprises and supports business establishment. Additionally, companies with gross sales or turnover not exceeding BND1 million annually are fully exempted from corporate income tax. These provisions create favorable conditions for small and medium enterprises (SMEs) and startup businesses.

Sectoral Support: The government provides sector-specific support for priority industries through dedicated programs and resources. For example, enterprises in the halal food industry receive support through BEDB initiatives, access to certification resources, and participation in international halal trade missions. Tourism sector investors may access infrastructure incentives and marketing support. ICT sector businesses benefit from broadband infrastructure investment and digital economy support programs. Islamic financial institutions receive support through regulatory development, Syariah governance frameworks, and international cooperation initiatives.

BEDB Services: The BEDB provides direct business support services including investment facilitation, business registration assistance, property/land allocation in industrial parks, and ongoing business support. The BEDB operates as a single-point-of-contact for foreign investors, streamlining procedures and providing guidance on regulatory compliance, tax matters, and business logistics. The agency can facilitate connections with government agencies, potential partners, and professional service providers.

Industrial Park Development and Incentives: Companies establishing operations in designated industrial parks (Sungai Liang Industrial Park, Pulau Muara Besar) may access land allocation incentives, utilities infrastructure at preferential rates, and simplified administrative procedures. These zones offer dedicated spaces for priority industries and provide a business-friendly environment with streamlined procedures relative to general commercial areas.

Foreign investors should engage with the BEDB early in the investment planning process to understand applicable incentives, filing requirements, and eligibility criteria. The availability and scope of incentives can be negotiated based on the specifics of the proposed investment and its strategic alignment with national development priorities.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

Brunei has developed designated industrial parks and special economic zones to support economic development and attract investment. These zones offer infrastructure, administrative support, and preferential terms designed to facilitate business operations in priority sectors.

Sungai Liang Industrial Park (SPARK): SPARK is a 271-hectare industrial park strategically positioned adjacent to Brunei's established oil and gas infrastructure on the western coast. The park is designed primarily to support downstream oil and gas, petrochemical, and related heavy industrial activities. SPARK offers world-class infrastructure including utilities (electricity, water, gas), transportation facilities, telecommunications networks, and waste management systems. The Sungai Liang Authority (SLA) administers the park and provides a single-point-of-access system for regulatory matters, streamlining business administration. Companies in SPARK benefit from proximity to feedstocks, access to technical expertise in the oil and gas sector, and connections to regional and international markets. The park's infrastructure and business-friendly environment have attracted significant investment from international petrochemical producers and related enterprises.

Pulau Muara Besar (PMB): PMB is a 955-hectare (2,360-acre) Greenfield island development located in Brunei Bay adjacent to the principal port city of Muara. The development is designed to establish a world-class deepwater container port and dynamic logistics and manufacturing hub serving the region. PMB will provide expanded port capacity for container handling, general cargo, and specialized cargo operations. The development includes facilities for petrochemical, oil, and gas sector industries, as well as logistics, warehousing, and manufacturing enterprises serving regional markets. Once completed, PMB will significantly enhance Brunei's position as a regional maritime and logistics hub. The development represents a long-term infrastructure investment designed to support trade facilitation and economic diversification objectives.

BIMP-EAGA Growth Area: Brunei is part of the Brunei Darussalam-Indonesia-Malaysia-Philippines East ASEAN Growth Area (BIMP-EAGA), a sub-regional economic cooperation framework. This growth area encompasses special economic zones and development initiatives across member countries, facilitating cross-border trade and investment. Brunei's participation provides access to a broader market and opportunities for regional integration of supply chains and value-added activities.

While Brunei does not have formally designated "free trade zones" in the traditional sense with separate tariff regimes, its membership in the RCEP and CPTPP (discussed below) provides duty-free or preferential tariff access to a vast regional market. The industrial parks and special economic zones effectively function as business hubs with streamlined procedures, infrastructure support, and preferential conditions that facilitate international commerce.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Brunei participates in multiple regional and bilateral trade agreements that substantially expand market access for businesses operating in the country. These agreements create preferential trading conditions and facilitate regional integration.

Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP): Brunei ratified RCEP on October 11, 2021, becoming a member of the world's largest trade bloc by population and economic size. RCEP comprises 15 member countries: Australia, Brunei, Cambodia, China, Indonesia, Japan, South Korea, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, New Zealand, the Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, and Vietnam. Together, RCEP members account for approximately 30% of the world's population (2.2 billion people) and 30% of global GDP (approximately USD29.7 trillion). The agreement provides comprehensive liberalization of trade in goods, services, and investment among members. Brunei-based businesses and exporters benefit from preferential tariff treatment when trading with RCEP member countries, facilitating market access across the broader Asia-Pacific region. The agreement reduces trade barriers and tariffs, increasing the competitiveness of Brunei-based products and services in member markets.

Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP): Brunei ratified the CPTPP in 2023, gaining access to a free trade agreement covering 11 countries: Australia, Brunei, Canada, Chile, Japan, Malaysia, Mexico, New Zealand, Peru, Singapore, and Vietnam. The CPTPP provides market access to approximately 600 million people with a combined GDP of USD13.5 trillion. The agreement eliminates tariffs on most manufactured and agricultural goods, reduces barriers to services trade, and facilitates investment across member countries. For businesses in Brunei, particularly those in halal food, tourism, and professional services, CPTPP membership provides expanded market opportunities. The agreement's intellectual property protections, investment provisions, and labor standards align with international best practices, creating a predictable framework for trade and investment.

ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA): As an ASEAN member, Brunei participates in AFTA, which eliminates tariffs on trade among the ten ASEAN member states (Brunei, Cambodia, Indonesia, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, the Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, and Vietnam). AFTA has been in effect since 1992 and has progressively reduced trade barriers among members. For Brunei-based businesses, AFTA access provides duty-free or preferential trading conditions within Southeast Asia, the world's most dynamic economic region.

ASEAN-China Free Trade Area (ACFTA): ASEAN, including Brunei, maintains a free trade agreement with China, providing preferential access to the Chinese market. This is significant for Brunei's agricultural products, halal goods, and manufactured items.

Additional Bilateral and Regional Agreements: Brunei maintains trade relationships and agreements with various countries and regional bodies, including ASEAN Plus Three (incorporating Japan, South Korea, and China) and various bilateral partnerships. These agreements further facilitate trade and investment.

The combination of RCEP, CPTPP, and ASEAN agreements provides Brunei-based businesses with broad preferential access to markets representing the majority of the world's population and economic activity. This positions Brunei as an attractive location for businesses seeking to access Asian markets and export to the Asia-Pacific region and beyond.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Brunei operates a sophisticated dual banking system comprising conventional banks and Islamic banks operating under integrated but separate regulatory frameworks. The central bank is the Brunei Darussalam Central Bank (BDCB), which oversees monetary policy, financial sector regulation, and banking supervision.

Banking Sector Structure: The banking sector includes both foreign and domestic banks offering comprehensive financial services. Conventional banks provide standard banking products including deposit accounts, lending services, payment and settlement services, and treasury operations. Islamic banks provide Shariah-compliant financial products and services based on Islamic financing principles. The sector is highly regulated, with banks required to maintain capital adequacy ratios, prudent lending practices, and comprehensive risk management systems. Banking regulations are generally aligned with international standards (Basel III capital standards, etc.), providing confidence in the stability and soundness of the financial system.

Islamic Finance Development: Islamic finance is a strategic priority for Brunei's financial sector development. Islamic finance assets in Brunei exceeded BND13 billion (approximately USD10.3 billion) in 2023, with Islamic banks and financial institutions accounting for over 95% of Islamic financial assets. The remaining assets are held by takaful (Islamic insurance) operators and sukuk (Islamic bond) programs. Brunei's dual regulatory structure includes a Syariah Financial Supervisory Board (SFSB) with responsibility for issuing Shariah rulings on financial matters and supervising Shariah compliance of Islamic financial institutions. This governance structure provides credibility and consistency for Islamic financial operations.

Globally, Islamic financial assets have surged 69% in the last five years, with projections for the industry to reach USD5.9 trillion by 2026. Brunei's position as an Islamic nation with credible Shariah governance structures positions it to capture a portion of this growing market. Government initiatives support development of Islamic banking, takaful services, sukuk markets, and Islamic investment products. The BDCB has identified Islamic and sustainable finance as key focus areas for the next five years and plans to release a comprehensive five-year financial sector blueprint in Q1 2026.

Banking Services for Businesses: Foreign investors and businesses operating in Brunei can access a full range of banking services including deposit accounts, trade financing, project financing, foreign exchange services, and treasury management. Banks provide letter of credit services, guarantees, and other trade finance instruments supporting international commerce. Working capital financing and term loans are available for business expansion and capital investments. Banking procedures are generally straightforward and aligned with international standards, though businesses should allow appropriate time for credit assessment and documentation.

Foreign Exchange and Currency: The Brunei Dollar (BND) is the official currency, pegged at parity to the Singapore Dollar (SGD). The BND is fully convertible to major international currencies. As of March 2026, the exchange rate is approximately 1 BND to 0.79 USD. Banks readily provide foreign exchange services for business transactions, allowing conversion between BND and other currencies. Companies with international operations can access hedging services to manage currency risk.

Investment and Capital Markets: Brunei's capital markets are developing but remain relatively modest in scale compared to major regional financial centers. The Brunei Darussalam Stock Exchange provides a venue for public securities trading, though trading volumes are limited. Companies typically access capital through bank financing rather than public markets. For investors seeking to invest in Brunei-based enterprises, private equity investments, venture capital, and direct business investment are more common than public securities offerings.

Payment and Settlement Systems: Brunei maintains modern payment and settlement systems supporting efficient business transactions. Banks provide electronic funds transfer, automated clearing house systems, and real-time gross settlement systems. Payments within Brunei and to regional destinations are facilitated through established banking networks and ASEAN payment infrastructure.

For foreign investors and businesses, Brunei's banking system provides a reliable, well-regulated environment for financial transactions, capital access, and business financing. The strong regulatory framework, integration with regional banking systems, and commitment to developing Islamic finance create confidence in financial stability and provide access to diverse financial products and services supporting business operations.

4. Business Organisations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Foreign investors in Brunei can establish business operations through several legal structures, each with distinct characteristics, governance requirements, and suitability for different business scenarios.

Limited Liability Companies (LLCs): The limited liability company is the most popular business structure for foreign investors in Brunei. An LLC is incorporated as a separate legal entity, distinct from its owners (shareholders), providing shareholders with limited liability for company debts and obligations. This means shareholders' personal assets are protected from company liabilities. An LLC in Brunei must have a minimum of two shareholders; there are no residency or nationality restrictions on shareholders, allowing 100% foreign ownership. Foreign shareholders can be either individuals or corporate entities (companies). The company must have a memorandum and articles of association governing its structure and operations, and must maintain a board of directors (minimum of two) with at least one resident director. An LLC can own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, and conduct all forms of commercial business.

Branch Offices: A branch office is an extension of a foreign parent company operating in Brunei and is not a separate legal entity. Rather, it represents the parent company conducting business under the parent's name and authority. A branch does not have its own legal identity; it is simply the parent company's establishment in Brunei. The branch is subject to the same taxation, accounting, and regulatory requirements as a local LLC, but profits are attributed directly to the parent company. At least one director of the branch must be a Brunei resident. Branches are appropriate for foreign companies seeking to establish a presence in Brunei while maintaining centralized governance and financial management with the parent company.

Representative Offices: A representative office is a minimal-presence structure through which a foreign company can maintain a presence in Brunei for liaison, market research, or business development purposes, without directly conducting commercial transactions. Representative offices cannot generate revenue directly; they serve informational and administrative functions. This structure is useful for foreign companies exploring market entry or conducting preliminary business development before establishing a more formal structure.

Sole Proprietorships and Partnerships: Sole proprietorships and partnerships are reserved exclusively for Brunei citizens or permanent residents and are not available to foreign nationals. Foreign investors cannot establish sole proprietorships or partnerships.

Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): Brunei's legal framework contemplates limited liability partnerships in certain contexts, though these are less common than LLCs. An LLP combines elements of partnerships (flexible governance structure) with limited liability protections (members are not personally liable for partnership debts). LLPs are available to both foreign and domestic investors, though documentation requirements and regulatory procedures may differ slightly from LLCs.

For most foreign investors, the limited liability company structure is preferred due to limited liability protections, flexibility in governance, ability to raise capital through additional shareholders, and compatibility with international corporate structures. Engagement with local legal and accounting professionals will help determine the optimal structure for a specific business situation.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Establishing a company in Brunei involves several steps administered by the Registry of Companies and Business Names (ROCBN) within the Ministry of Finance and Economy. The process is relatively straightforward and typically completed within 2-4 weeks.

Name Approval: The first step is securing approval of the proposed company name from the Registrar of Companies. The company name must be unique and not conflict with existing registered names. Names must comply with statutory requirements—they cannot be offensive, misleading, or contrary to public policy. The proposed name should be submitted to ROCBN for clearance before proceeding with formal incorporation documentation.

Documentation Preparation: The company must prepare incorporation documents including the Memorandum of Association (the company's constitutional document specifying its objectives, powers, and capital structure) and Articles of Association (governing the company's internal management, shareholder rights, and operational procedures). These documents must comply with the Companies Act and must clearly specify the company's registered office address in Brunei, the names and addresses of shareholders and directors, and the share capital structure. If the company has resident and non-resident directors, the documents must clearly identify their roles and responsibilities.

Submission and Registration: The completed incorporation documents, along with the application fee and supporting documentation (identity verification for all shareholders and directors), are submitted to ROCBN. The Registrar reviews the documents for compliance with statutory requirements. Upon approval and payment of registration fees, the company is issued a Certificate of Incorporation, which constitutes proof of company formation and establishment as a separate legal entity.

Post-Incorporation Compliance: Following incorporation, the company must complete several compliance steps. The company must register for tax purposes with the Inland Revenue Department and obtain a tax identification number. If the company will employ staff, it must register with the Department of Labour and obtain necessary employment authorization documentation. The company must also open a bank account in the company's name (typically required within a specified period). The registered office address must be established, and company records (including shareholder and director registers, accounting records, and minutes of shareholder/director meetings) must be maintained in accordance with the Companies Act.

Annual Filing Obligations: Companies must file annual returns with ROCBN within 28 days of the annual general meeting. The annual return includes updated information on directors, shareholders, company financial status, and registered office. Companies must maintain current and accurate information with the Registry at all times.

Business Registration: In addition to company registration, if the company will operate under a business name different from the corporate name, the business name must be separately registered with the Registry of Business Names. This registration is a distinct requirement from company incorporation.

The Brunei Economic Development Board (BEDB) can assist foreign investors with the registration process, providing guidance, document preparation support, and liaison with government agencies. Many foreign investors engage local legal counsel or business formation agents to manage the registration process and ensure compliance with all statutory requirements.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

Brunei companies are subject to governance requirements specified in the Companies Act and supplemented by sector-specific regulations for certain industries (banking, insurance, securities, etc.).

Board of Directors: A company must have a minimum of two directors, and at least one director must be a resident of Brunei. Directors can be individual persons or corporate entities (companies serving as director). Directors are responsible for managing the company's affairs, setting strategic direction, approving major transactions, and ensuring compliance with statutory obligations. Directors have fiduciary duties to the company, including duties of care, loyalty, and good faith. Directors are personally liable for certain statutory violations (such as failure to file required returns) and for gross negligence or fraud. Directors can be held liable to creditors and shareholders if they cause harm through breach of duty. Directors' meetings must be conducted periodically (at minimum annually, though best practice suggests quarterly), and minutes of meetings must be maintained.

Shareholder Meetings: Companies must hold an annual general meeting (AGM) within a specified period following the company's financial year-end, at which shareholders review and approve annual financial statements, elect directors, and address matters requiring shareholder approval. Shareholders holding specified percentages of shares can requisition extraordinary general meetings (EGMs) to address urgent matters. Shareholder meetings must follow procedural requirements regarding notice, quorum, and voting, as specified in the company's articles of association and the Companies Act.

Accounting and Financial Reporting: Companies must maintain complete and accurate accounting records documenting all financial transactions. These records must be retained for specified periods (typically 5-7 years). Companies must prepare annual financial statements in accordance with accounting standards established in Brunei (generally aligned with IFRS—International Financial Reporting Standards). Financial statements must be audited if the company meets specified size thresholds (typically if turnover exceeds BND1 million or assets exceed specified amounts). Audited financial statements must be filed with ROCBN as part of the annual return.

Regulatory Compliance: Companies must maintain compliance with all applicable statutory requirements including tax obligations (corporate income tax filing and payment), employment law compliance, environmental regulations, sector-specific regulations (if applicable), and immigration regulations (for any foreign workers). Non-compliance can result in penalties, director liability, and potential loss of business license.

Disclosure Obligations: Companies must disclose specified information to regulatory authorities and shareholders. Related party transactions, material changes in company structure or ownership, and certain financial matters must be disclosed. Public companies (if any exist or are established) would be subject to additional disclosure requirements comparable to securities exchange requirements.

Appointment of Company Secretary: Many companies appoint a company secretary responsible for maintaining company records, coordinating shareholder and director meetings, and ensuring compliance with corporate governance requirements. While this is often not mandatory for smaller private companies, it is considered best practice and facilitates administrative compliance.

Brunei does not maintain a separate corporate governance code comparable to common law jurisdictions like Australia or Singapore, but governance expectations are embedded in statutory requirements and professional standards. Foreign investors should ensure their governance structures comply with Companies Act requirements and embody internationally recognized governance best practices (board independence, conflict of interest management, transparent decision-making, etc.).

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Brunei's employment law is governed by the Employment Order 2009 (as amended), which establishes minimum terms and conditions of employment and protects worker rights. The Employment (Minimum Wage) Order 2023 further establishes minimum wage requirements for specific worker categories. The Department of Labour within the Ministry of Home Affairs administers and enforces employment law.

Scope and Coverage: The Employment Order 2009 applies to all persons employed under a contract of service (written or implied). However, it excludes seamen, domestic servants, and persons employed in managerial, executive, or confidential positions. The law establishes minimum statutory protections, and employers are encouraged to provide better terms and conditions than statutory minimums to attract and retain valued employees.

Minimum Wage: Brunei does not impose a single, unified minimum wage applicable to all workers. However, the Employment (Minimum Wage) Order 2023 (effective July 12, 2023) establishes minimum wage requirements that are being implemented in phases. The specific minimum wage depends on worker category, sector, and skill level. Employers must negotiate compensation with individual employees within the minimum wage framework, but cannot offer below-minimum rates. As of 2023-2026, the minimum wage varies by worker classification and is adjusted periodically. Employers should consult current Department of Labour guidelines for applicable minimum wages in their sector.

Working Hours and Rest: The Employment Order establishes maximum working hours and rest period requirements. Standard working hours are typically 48 hours per week, though specific arrangements can be negotiated. Workers are entitled to rest days (typically one day per week) and must receive overtime compensation for work beyond standard hours. Workers cannot be required to work excessive hours without appropriate compensation and rest periods.

Paid Leave and Holidays: Workers are entitled to paid annual leave (typically 15-20 days per year depending on tenure and company policy), paid public holidays, and specified paid leave for personal needs (medical appointments, family matters, etc.). The Employment Order specifies minimum paid leave entitlements, which employers can enhance. Workers are also entitled to paid sick leave for illness or medical treatment.

Maternity Leave and Benefits: Female workers are entitled to maternity leave benefits, including leave from work for pregnancy and childbirth and income support during the maternity period. The Employment Order specifies maternity leave periods and benefit amounts. Employers cannot discriminate against female workers based on pregnancy or maternity status.

Termination and Retrenchment: Employment can be terminated by either party with specified notice periods or payment in lieu of notice. Termination must follow proper procedures and cannot be arbitrary or discriminatory. Workers dismissed for misconduct or performance issues must receive notice and opportunity to respond to allegations. Retrenchment (involuntary termination for economic reasons) requires notice periods and severance compensation. Unfair dismissal claims can be brought before the Department of Labour or courts.

Work Permits and Foreign Workers: Foreign workers must obtain work permits and comply with employment authorization requirements. Employers are responsible for ensuring foreign workers maintain valid work authorization. Employment of unauthorized workers can result in penalties for employers and deportation for workers.

Social Security Contributions: Brunei citizens and permanent residents must contribute to national social security schemes including the National Retirement Scheme (SPK) (8.5% employee contribution from basic salary), Employees' Trust Fund (TAP) (5% employee contribution), and Supplemental Contributory Pension Scheme (SCP) (3.5% employee contribution). Employers must match these contributions. Foreign workers' social security obligations depend on residency status and visa classification.

Work Injury Compensation: Workers injured during employment are entitled to compensation through the employer's liability insurance or statutory compensation scheme. Employers must maintain insurance coverage for workplace injuries.

Non-Discrimination: Employment law prohibits discrimination based on race, gender, religion, disability, or other protected characteristics. Employers must provide equal pay for equal work and equal employment opportunities regardless of protected status.

Occupational Safety and Health: Employers must maintain safe working conditions, provide necessary safety equipment and training, and comply with occupational health standards. Major workplaces are subject to inspection by labor authorities.

Employment law in Brunei is generally favorable to workers while allowing business flexibility in compensation, work arrangements, and management. Foreign employers should ensure compliance with the Employment Order 2009, current minimum wage orders, and social security contribution requirements. Engagement with the Department of Labour or legal counsel can clarify specific obligations in the employer's industry or sector.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Brunei maintains a comprehensive intellectual property protection framework administered by the Brunei Intellectual Property Office (BruIPO), established in 2013. The country is party to multiple international intellectual property agreements, providing protection aligned with global standards.

Trademark Protection: Trademarks in Brunei must demonstrate distinctiveness and cannot be generic, descriptive, or in conflict with prior registered marks. They cannot be contrary to public policy or morality. Trademark registration is based on the application, examination, and publication system. An applicant files a trademark application (Form TM1) specifying the goods or services to be covered, organized by international classification. Following examination for absolute and relative grounds of refusal, the trademark is published for opposition. Upon approval, the trademark receives protection for an initial 10-year term and is renewable indefinitely in 10-year increments. Trademark protection is nationwide and provides the owner exclusive rights to use the mark on associated goods and services, with rights to control licensing and prevent unauthorized use by third parties.

Patent Protection: Patents protect inventions demonstrating novelty, non-obviousness, and utility. Patent protection in Brunei lasts up to 20 years from the filing date, subject to annual renewal fee payments. Brunei utilizes a registration system without substantive examination in some cases, though examination procedures are available. Patents provide the owner exclusive rights to make, use, and sell the patented invention, with the ability to license others or enforce rights against infringers. Brunei is party to the Patent Cooperation Treaty (since July 24, 2012), facilitating filing of international patent applications.

Industrial Designs: Industrial designs (ornamental features of articles) are protected for an initial 5-year term, with renewable protection for additional 10-year periods upon payment of renewal fees. Protection requires originality and distinctiveness. Industrial design registration provides exclusive rights to the design owner to use and license the design and to exclude others from unauthorized use.

Copyright: Copyright protection in Brunei applies to original creative works including literary works, artistic works, musical compositions, dramatic works, cinematographic films, and other original works. Copyright protection requires originality and lasts for the author's life plus 50 years (for individual authors). Brunei's IP regime provides for automatic copyright protection without formal registration, though registration provides additional evidentiary benefits. Copyright protection prohibits unauthorized reproduction, distribution, public display, and adaptation of works.

International Agreements: Brunei is party to the Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (since August 30, 2006), the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (since February 17, 2012), and the Madrid Protocol for international trademark registration (since January 6, 2017). These agreements provide reciprocal protection for Brunei nationals' intellectual property in member countries and provide protection for foreign nationals' intellectual property in Brunei. Brunei's participation in these agreements demonstrates commitment to intellectual property protection aligned with international standards.

Enforcement: BruIPO administers IP registration and provides support for enforcement of rights. IP infringement can be addressed through civil litigation in the courts, seeking injunctions to stop infringing activities and damages for losses. Criminal prosecution is available for certain serious infringements. BruIPO can facilitate administrative proceedings and investigations related to IP violations.

Practical Considerations for Businesses: Foreign businesses should register their trademarks, patents, and other intellectual property in Brunei to secure protection, particularly if they intend to market products or services in the country or use Brunei as a regional hub. Registration should be done promptly to establish priority dates. Businesses should monitor for infringement and be prepared to enforce rights through available legal mechanisms. Engagement with a local IP counsel or agent can facilitate efficient registration and enforcement of intellectual property rights in Brunei.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Brunei maintains a distinctive tax system characterized by the absence of personal income tax and a moderately levied corporate income tax rate. The government funds operations and services primarily through hydrocarbon revenues and investment returns rather than broad income taxation of individuals and corporations. This tax structure creates an attractive environment for expatriate professionals and businesses while requiring strict compliance with applicable corporate tax obligations.

Legal Framework: Brunei's tax law is governed by the Income Tax Act (Chapter 35), the Corporate Tax provisions, and various subsidiary orders and regulations administered by the Inland Revenue Department (IRD) within the Ministry of Finance and Economy. Tax law is based on statutory provisions with limited case law development relative to common law jurisdictions. The government regularly issues guidelines, circulars, and administrative rulings clarifying tax obligations and procedures. Tax administration follows a preceding year basis of assessment, meaning taxes assessed in a given year are based on income earned in the previous year. This contrasts with current year assessment systems used in some jurisdictions.

Fiscal Year: The fiscal year runs from January 1 to December 31. Companies must file tax returns and pay tax liabilities by June 30 of the year following the assessment year. For example, taxes on 2024 income are assessed in 2025 and must be paid by June 30, 2025.

Tax Administration: The Inland Revenue Department administers tax law, assesses and collects taxes, and has authority to conduct audits and investigations. Tax disputes are typically resolved through departmental appeals procedures or, if necessary, through the courts. The IRD maintains a One Common Portal (OCP) providing online services for business registration, licensing, tax filing, and related administrative matters.

Compliance Requirements: All businesses must register for tax purposes, maintain complete accounting records, file annual tax returns, and pay assessed taxes by statutory deadlines. Non-compliance can result in penalties, interest on unpaid taxes, and potential criminal prosecution. Businesses are required to maintain accounting records in English or Malay and should ensure internal accounting systems comply with statutory requirements.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

Standard Rate: The standard corporate income tax rate for resident companies in Brunei is 18.5%. This applies to companies incorporated in Brunei or foreign companies with permanent establishments in Brunei. Non-resident companies (branches of foreign companies) conducting business in Brunei are also subject to 18.5% tax on income derived from Brunei sources.

Oil and Gas Sector Exception: Companies engaged in the business of exploration and production of oil and gas are subject to a substantially higher tax rate of 55%. This higher rate reflects the government's policy of capturing significant resource rents from hydrocarbon production. Downstream oil and gas operations (refining, processing, petrochemicals) are subject to the standard 18.5% rate, not the 55% rate.

Tax Incentives for New Companies: Newly incorporated companies benefit from significant tax relief. Companies are granted exemption for the first BND100,000 of chargeable income during the first three consecutive years of assessment. This exemption effectively reduces tax liability for startup enterprises and new business ventures. Companies with gross annual sales or turnover not exceeding BND1 million are fully exempted from corporate income tax, creating favorable conditions for small businesses.

Investment Incentive Exemptions: Under the Investment Incentive Order 2001, companies making strategically important investments in priority sectors can qualify for tax incentives including full or partial exemption from corporate income tax for defined periods (typically 5-10 years), accelerated depreciation allowances for capital equipment, and exemption of import duties on specified capital equipment and materials. The scope of incentives depends on the sector, investment size, employment creation, and strategic importance of the project.

Tax Deductions: Companies can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses from gross income to determine taxable income. Deductible items include salaries and wages, rent, utilities, supplies, professional services, depreciation of business assets (using statutory depreciation rates), interest on business debt, and other reasonable business expenses. Personal expenses, capital expenditures (which are depreciated over time), and certain other items are not deductible.

Loss Carryforward: Companies suffering operational losses in a given year can carry forward losses to offset income in future years, subject to specific rules and limitations. This provision provides relief for cyclical businesses or startups during unprofitable periods.

Transfer Pricing: Brunei has not extensively developed transfer pricing documentation requirements, though multinational enterprises should ensure that transactions between related entities are priced on an arm's length basis consistent with OECD guidelines. Pricing of intercompany transactions is subject to scrutiny if it appears designed to shift income away from Brunei.

Tax Returns and Payment: Companies must file annual tax returns showing income, deductions, and tax liability by June 30 following the assessment year. Returns should be filed with the Inland Revenue Department using prescribed forms or through the online portal. Tax payment must be made by the same deadline. Late filing or payment incurs penalties and interest charges.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

No Personal Income Tax: Brunei does not impose personal income tax on individuals. This applies to both residents and non-residents and covers all forms of personal income including salaries, wages, bonuses, allowances, investment income, capital gains, and other individual income sources. This distinctive feature creates significant advantages for expatriate professionals and entrepreneurs who do not face individual income tax obligations on earnings in Brunei.

Residency Definition for Tax Purposes: A resident individual for tax purposes is defined as a person who resides in Brunei in the year preceding the year of assessment, except for temporary absences (such as brief business trips or vacations). Alternatively, a person who is physically present in or exercises employment in Brunei for 183 days or more during the preceding year is considered a resident. Non-residents are persons who do not meet these criteria; they are generally not subject to Brunei tax obligations except on Brunei-source income (and even then, without personal income tax, non-residents face minimal direct tax liability).

Mandatory Social Security Contributions: While personal income tax does not apply, Brunei citizens and permanent residents are required to make mandatory social security contributions to national pension and benefit schemes. These contributions are deducted from salary and are partially employer-funded. Foreign workers' social security contributions depend on visa and employment status; temporary residents and workers on specific visas may not be required to contribute to certain schemes. The contribution schemes include:

  • National Retirement Scheme (SPK): An employee contribution of 8.5% from basic salary, with an employer contribution of an equal amount. This scheme provides retirement, disability, and survivor benefits.

  • Employees' Trust Fund (TAP): A 5% employee contribution from salary, matched by the employer, providing a lump-sum benefit upon retirement or separation from employment.

  • Supplemental Contributory Pension Scheme (SCP): A 3.5% employee contribution, matched by the employer, providing supplemental retirement income.

These mandatory contributions are not considered tax but are statutory social security obligations. Employers are responsible for calculating, deducting, and remitting contributions to the appropriate authorities.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

Customs Duties and Import/Export Taxes: Brunei imposes customs duties on imported goods classified according to the Harmonized System of classification used internationally. Duty rates vary by product classification and origin. RCEP and CPTPP membership provides duty-free or preferential tariff treatment for goods originating from member countries. Export duties are not imposed on most goods; however, certain natural resource exports (particularly crude oil and natural gas) may be subject to resource rents or export taxes separate from income taxation.

Sales Tax/Value Added Tax (VAT): Brunei does not impose sales tax or value-added tax. This contrasts with many other jurisdictions where VAT is a significant revenue source. The absence of VAT creates a straightforward pricing environment for businesses and consumers.

Property and Land Tax: Brunei does not impose annual property or land value tax. Property ownership or rental does not trigger ongoing property tax obligations, though property transactions may involve transfer duties or registration fees.

Stamp Duty: Certain documents (including some contracts, property transfers, insurance policies, and other instruments) may be subject to stamp duty, a tax on document execution. The scope and rates of stamp duty vary based on the nature of the document. Properly stamped documents are required for evidence in legal proceedings.

Tax on Motor Vehicles: Motor vehicles are subject to import duty upon importation and may be subject to road tax obligations. Import duty varies based on vehicle type and engine size.

Professional Licenses and Business Permits: Certain professions and businesses require licenses or permits that involve administrative fees. These are regulatory fees rather than taxes but represent compliance costs for specific business activities.

Excise Duties and Specific Consumption Taxes: Brunei imposes excise duties on specific goods including alcoholic beverages, tobacco products, and petroleum products. These duties are collected through the distribution system and reflected in consumer prices. However, Brunei's Islamic principles restrict alcohol and tobacco sales and marketing in significant ways, limiting the market scope for these products.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

Brunei offers various tax incentives designed to attract investment in priority sectors and support business establishment and growth. These incentives operate primarily through the Investment Incentive Order 2001 and related government programs.

New Company Exemptions: As noted above, newly incorporated companies automatically receive exemption for the first BND100,000 of chargeable income during the first three consecutive years. This reduces tax liability for startups and newly established enterprises.

Small Business Exemption: Companies with gross annual sales or turnover not exceeding BND1 million are fully exempt from corporate income tax. This exemption makes small business operations more financially attractive.

Investment Incentive Order 2001 Incentives: The government grants discretionary incentives for strategically important investments. These incentives can include:

  • Tax Exemption Periods: Full or partial exemption from corporate income tax for defined periods (typically 5-10 years depending on sector and investment scale).

  • Accelerated Depreciation: Faster depreciation of capital equipment, reducing taxable income in early years of operation.

  • Import Duty Exemption: Exemption of import duties on specified capital equipment and materials for the approved investment.

  • Other Fiscal Benefits: Including loss carryforward extensions, reduced corporate tax rates for specific sectors, or other benefits negotiated based on the investment's strategic importance.

To qualify for Investment Incentive Order benefits, investors must apply to the Ministry of Finance and Economy or BEDB, demonstrating how the investment aligns with national development priorities. Incentives are more readily available for investments in priority sectors (downstream oil and gas, halal food, tourism, ICT, Islamic finance) and for investments that create significant employment, transfer technology, or contribute to economic diversification.

Sector-Specific Incentives: Halal industry enterprises, tourism businesses, ICT companies, and Islamic financial institutions may access additional sector-specific benefits administered through BEDB or relevant regulatory bodies. These can include infrastructure support, market access assistance, regulatory streamlining, and financial incentives.

Export Incentives: Businesses exporting goods or services may access benefits including duty drawback systems (recovery of import duties paid on inputs) and export credit guarantees supporting international sales.

Foreign investors should engage with the BEDB or Ministry of Finance and Economy early in investment planning to understand applicable incentives and to structure investments to maximize tax efficiency while complying with all statutory requirements.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Foreign Tax Credits and Double Taxation Relief: Brunei provides relief from double taxation through limited mechanisms. Individuals and companies paying tax in foreign countries on Brunei-source income may be eligible for foreign tax credits reducing Brunei tax liability. However, Brunei has not entered into comprehensive tax treaties with most major countries. The absence of a broad treaty network means that double taxation relief is limited, and businesses with operations in multiple countries may face cumulative tax burdens. Foreign investors should carefully structure cross-border transactions and consider treaty relief where available.

Tax Treaties: Brunei has entered into a limited number of bilateral tax treaties. Countries with which Brunei has tax treaties include Malaysia, Thailand, Bangladesh, and a few others. These treaties typically provide reduced withholding tax rates on dividend, interest, and royalty payments and provide relief from double taxation. Investors from treaty countries may benefit from preferential treatment. However, the limited treaty network means most foreign investors do not have treaty protection.

Withholding Taxes: Payments of interest, dividends, and certain royalties made by Brunei entities to foreign parties are subject to withholding taxes, typically at 10-15% rates depending on the payment type and whether a treaty applies. Treaty benefits can reduce withholding rates. Withholding tax compliance is essential; the paying entity is responsible for withholding and remitting taxes to the IRD.

Transfer Pricing and Related Party Transactions: While Brunei has not adopted comprehensive transfer pricing documentation requirements comparable to OECD standards, multinational enterprises should ensure that intercompany transactions are priced on an arm's length basis consistent with international norms. The IRD may challenge pricing of related-party transactions if they appear designed to shift income away from Brunei or avoid tax liability. Documentation supporting pricing decisions should be maintained and provided upon request.

Foreign Investment Income: Brunei's approach to taxing foreign investment income is simplified by the absence of personal income tax. Companies receive corporate tax treatment on worldwide income, but the structure of Brunei's tax system does not create significant incentives for income shifting or tax planning compared to jurisdictions with higher marginal tax rates. Nonetheless, businesses should understand how various types of income (operational income, investment income, capital gains) are taxed.

Reporting Obligations for Foreign Persons: Foreign investors and non-resident companies conducting business in Brunei or receiving Brunei-source income must register with the IRD and comply with filing and payment obligations. Non-residents earning Brunei-source income from specific sources (property rentals, business operations, etc.) must report this income and may have withholding tax obligations. The specific reporting requirements depend on the nature of the foreign person's Brunei activities.

Capital Flight and Currency Controls: Brunei maintains openness to international financial flows and does not impose capital controls or significant currency restrictions. The Brunei Dollar is fully convertible to major international currencies through the banking system. Repatriation of profits, dividends, and capital by foreign investors is permitted without restriction, subject to tax withholding and compliance with foreign exchange regulations administered by the BDCB.

For international businesses, Brunei presents a tax-efficient environment, particularly due to the absence of personal income tax and moderate corporate tax rates (18.5%). However, the limited treaty network and absence of comprehensive tax incentives for particular transaction types (compared to some regional competitors) may warrant consideration. Engagement with local tax counsel experienced in international transactions is advisable for investors with complex cross-border structures.
