Doing Business in Indonesia

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Indonesia is a vast Southeast Asian archipelago comprising over 17,000 islands stretched across the equator, making it the world's largest archipelagic state. With a total area of 1,904,569 square kilometers (735,358 square miles), Indonesia ranks as the 14th-largest country by area globally. The country extends approximately 3,200 miles (5,100 kilometers) from east to west and 1,100 miles (1,800 kilometers) from north to south, creating one of the world's most geographically diverse nations. Major islands include Sumatra, Java, Sulawesi, Kalimantan (Borneo), and Papua, each with distinct economic characteristics, resources, and development levels. Indonesia's strategic location astride major sea lanes connecting East Asia, South Asia, and Oceania makes it a crucial hub for regional trade and commerce, with significant shipping routes passing through the Malacca Strait and other maritime passages.

The country shares land borders with Papua New Guinea, Timor-Leste, and Malaysia, while maintaining maritime boundaries with seven additional nations including Australia, Singapore, the Philippines, and several others. These maritime borders have occasionally been subject to boundary disputes or require careful navigation for shipping and resource exploitation activities. Indonesia's equatorial location and vast size create significant geographic diversity, with tropical climates, dense forests covering approximately 50 percent of the land area, volcanic mountain ranges, and numerous water systems characterizing much of the territory. The archipelago experiences significant geological activity, including frequent seismic events and volcanic activity, which investors should factor into risk assessments for infrastructure and operations.

Indonesia's population is distributed unevenly across the islands, with Java accounting for the majority of the population despite comprising only approximately 7 percent of total land area. The western islands, particularly Java and Sumatra, are substantially more densely developed and economically integrated, while eastern regions including Papua, Sulawesi, and outlying islands remain less developed but offer frontier opportunities for resource extraction, agriculture, and infrastructure investment. Regional variations in infrastructure quality, internet connectivity, labor availability, and regulatory sophistication significantly affect business planning and investment decisions. Understanding these geographic and demographic variations is essential for foreign investors selecting investment locations and assessing operational complexity across different regions of Indonesia.

1.2 Political System & Government

Indonesia operates under a presidential system of government similar to that of the United States, where the president functions as both head of state and head of government, wielding substantial executive authority over the cabinet, military command, and legislative agenda. The president appoints cabinet ministers, approves legislation passed by Parliament, commands the armed forces, and exercises broad executive decree authority within constitutional bounds. Currently, President Prabowo Subianto, who was inaugurated on October 20, 2024, leads the executive branch with significant mandate for economic and political reforms. His 2026 state budget represents the first genuinely reflective of his administration's policy priorities and fiscal outlook, marking a transition from inherited budgets of previous administrations and signaling the government's commitment to infrastructure development, downstream industrialization, and strategic investment priorities.

The legislative branch operates through a bicameral system comprising the People's Consultative Assembly (Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat, or MPR), the People's Representative Council (Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat, or DPR), and the Regional Representative Council (Dewan Perwakilan Daerah, or DPD). The DPR, consisting of 560 members drawn from multiple political parties through proportional representation elections, holds primary authority to draft and pass laws, produce annual budgets in cooperation with the president, and oversee general political affairs. The MPR, which includes DPR members plus appointed regional representatives, holds authority to set or amend the Constitution and maintains the theoretical power to appoint or impeach the president, though impeachment remains exceptionally rare in practice. The DPD provides regional perspective with members appointed by local assemblies from each province.

Seven of eight parties represented in the 2024 House of Representatives comprise the "Advanced Indonesia coalition," commanding approximately 80 percent of parliamentary seats and providing substantial political stability for policy implementation and economic initiatives through 2026 and beyond. This coalition majority significantly strengthens the president's legislative agenda and reduces uncertainty regarding major economic or policy initiatives. Indonesia's democratic system, while subject to periodic criticism regarding judicial independence and press freedom, has demonstrated stability and peaceful transitions of power across multiple presidential administrations. The decentralized nature of governance, with significant authority delegated to provincial and municipal governments since the 2001 decentralization reforms, means that foreign investors must often navigate both national and regional regulatory frameworks for certain business matters. Political risk in Indonesia remains relatively modest compared to some regional peers, though investors should monitor developments regarding judicial independence, corruption enforcement, and policy continuity through periodic elections scheduled for 2029.

1.3 Economy Overview

Indonesia's economy stands as Southeast Asia's largest and the world's fourth-most populous nation's economy, characterized by abundant natural resources, a substantial domestic market of 287 million people, and significant manufacturing and service sector capabilities. The economy has demonstrated considerable resilience through 2025 and into 2026, with measured yet stable growth prospects supported by the International Monetary Fund and World Bank assessments. According to the IMF's January 2026 Article IV Consultation, Indonesia achieved economic growth of 5.0 percent in 2025 and is projected to expand by 5.1 percent in 2026, reflecting an increase of 0.2 percentage points from the IMF's October 2025 estimate. The World Bank similarly projects Indonesia's economy will maintain growth near 5.0 percent through 2026 and 2027, supported by strong domestic investment, robust export performance, and continued policy continuity under the Prabowo administration.

Indonesia's economy remains vulnerable to external shocks including fluctuations in commodity prices (particularly oil, palm oil, tin, nickel, and other minerals representing significant export revenue sources), exchange rate volatility affecting manufacturing competitiveness and foreign exchange stability, and global economic uncertainties that could reduce demand for Indonesian exports or trigger capital outflows. However, the underlying growth trajectory and market fundamentals support continued foreign investor confidence in medium-term opportunities. Indonesia's substantial domestic market of 287 million people, coupled with a median age of 30.7 years creating a growing working-age population and consumer base, continues to attract both domestic and foreign investment. The economy's sectors are diverse, ranging from agriculture and natural resource extraction to manufacturing, infrastructure development, digital economy, and services, providing multiple investment entry points for foreign capital.

The broader economic context for 2026 points to resilience rather than acceleration, with growth driven primarily by domestic demand, state-linked investment in infrastructure and key sectors, and stable policy frameworks established by the Prabowo administration. Monetary and fiscal policies have become increasingly accommodative, with stimulus measures designed to boost private credit and consumption while maintaining fiscal discipline and moderate inflation control. Realized foreign direct investment totaled approximately IDR 900.9 trillion (US$53–55 billion) through 2025, demonstrating sustained investor confidence despite global economic headwinds. Priority investment sectors include transport and logistics, telecommunications, mining, digital economy, renewable energy, electric vehicle manufacturing, pharmaceutical production, and food security initiatives. The government has set ambitious investment targets for 2026 aiming for double-digit growth supported by infrastructure spending, downstream industrialization, and strategic investments in energy transition and food systems.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Indonesia has undertaken substantial infrastructure investment through the Prabowo administration and preceding governments, with major initiatives including the expansion of port facilities, development of new toll highways, modernization of power generation and distribution systems, and enhancement of digital connectivity across the archipelago. The backbone network of toll roads connecting major cities on Java and extensions to Sumatra has significantly improved overland logistics and reduced transportation costs for industrial operations and regional distribution. Major ports including those in Jakarta, Surabaya, Semarang, and Batam handle substantial containerized cargo volumes, though capacity constraints during peak seasons and aging infrastructure in some facilities continue to present logistical challenges for importers and exporters.

Digital infrastructure has advanced substantially in recent years, with internet penetration reaching approximately 77 percent of the population as of 2025, supporting e-commerce, fintech development, and digital service delivery across the nation. Mobile broadband networks operated by major carriers including Telkom, Indosat, and Axiata provide 4G/LTE coverage in major population centers and increasingly in secondary cities and industrial zones. However, connectivity quality and speeds vary significantly between urban and rural areas, and businesses should assess infrastructure availability at specific operational locations. Indonesia's digital economy, projected to reach an estimated value of USD 146 billion by 2025, represents one of Southeast Asia's largest and fastest-growing digital markets, with opportunities in e-commerce, fintech, cloud infrastructure, and artificial intelligence applications.

Power infrastructure remains a critical consideration for manufacturing and processing operations, as electricity capacity and reliability vary regionally. State-owned electricity company PLN (Perusahaan Listrik Negara) operates the majority of generation and distribution, with supplemental capacity from independent power producers under long-term power purchase agreements. Rolling blackouts, though less frequent than in prior years, can still occur during peak demand periods in some regions, making backup generation capacity a practical requirement for operations requiring continuous power supply. Water infrastructure varies significantly by location, with major cities possessing municipal water systems of varying quality and reliability, while secondary cities and rural areas may require alternative water sourcing arrangements. Foreign investors establishing manufacturing or agricultural operations should conduct detailed infrastructure assessments for specific locations and budget for potential supplemental systems including backup power generation, water treatment, and waste management systems aligned with environmental standards.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Indonesia's legal system is based on civil law derived from the Dutch colonial legal tradition, substantially modified and adapted over the seventy years of independence since 1945. The legal framework operates through a hierarchical structure comprising the Constitution (UUD 1945, as amended through the Fourth Amendment of 2002), laws enacted by Parliament (undang-undang), government regulations issued by the president (peraturan pemerintah), presidential regulations (peraturan presiden), ministerial regulations (peraturan menteri), and local regulations established by provincial and municipal governments (peraturan daerah). The 1945 Constitution, as amended, establishes the fundamental legal and governmental structure, including provisions protecting property rights, contractual freedom, and the framework for foreign investment. This hierarchical legal structure means that lower-level regulations must conform to higher-level instruments, creating a cascade of legal instruments that foreign investors must understand and comply with across multiple levels of government.

The Directorate General of Taxes (Direktorat Jenderal Pajak) under the Ministry of Finance administers tax law and policy, while sector-specific regulators including Bank Indonesia, the Financial Services Authority (OJK—Otoritas Jasa Keuangan), and the Investment Coordinating Board (BKPM—Badan Koordinasi Penanaman Modal) oversee their respective domains with substantial regulatory authority. Indonesia's legal system provides constitutional protections for property rights, contractual freedom, and foreign investment, established through Law No. 25 of 2007 on Investment (amended multiple times, most recently through 2025 regulations) and related statutes addressing specific sectors and activities. The Indonesian legal system recognizes both written law (codified statutes and regulations) and, in limited contexts, customary law (adat), particularly regarding land ownership and use in certain regions. Foreign investors should understand that while the written legal framework generally provides clear rules and protection mechanisms, enforcement mechanisms and judicial efficiency can vary by jurisdiction and court level.

Courts are organized in a three-tier hierarchy comprising district courts (pengadilan negeri), high courts (pengadilan tinggi), and the Supreme Court (Mahkamah Agung). Specialized courts including commercial courts (pengadilan niaga), administrative courts (pengadilan tata usaha negara), and sharia courts (pengadilan agama in matters affecting Muslim parties) handle specific categories of disputes. Commercial courts have jurisdiction over bankruptcy, corporate disputes, and certain contract matters, and are generally considered to have developed expertise in complex commercial litigation. Foreign investors should be aware that although Indonesia's legal framework provides protections and procedural safeguards, enforcement mechanisms and judicial efficiency vary by jurisdiction and court level. Business disputes are often resolved through arbitration, particularly international commercial arbitration administered by institutions such as the Jakarta International Dispute Resolution Center (JICDC) or the Indonesia National Board of Arbitration (BANI—Badan Arbitrase Nasional Indonesia), both of which are recognized as legitimate dispute resolution mechanisms for international contracts under Indonesian law and international convention.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

Indonesia's foreign investment regime is governed by Law No. 25 of 2007 on Investment, as amended multiple times including recent amendments through 2025, which provides the fundamental legal framework for foreign direct investment and establishes the Investment Coordinating Board (BKPM) as the central authority for investment coordination and business licensing. The law explicitly welcomes foreign investment as a driver of economic growth, technology transfer, employment creation, and regional development, establishing a non-discriminatory legal framework in principle, though specific sectors face restrictions or conditions. Foreign investors must establish operations through a PT PMA (Penanaman Modal Asing—Foreign-Invested Limited Liability Company), a corporate entity incorporated under Indonesian law with Indonesian legal personality, which serves as the vehicle for conducting business operations.

The Negative Investment List (Daftar Negatif Investasi—DNI), updated periodically through Presidential Regulation, specifies sectors and activities closed to foreign investment or available only to foreign investors subject to specified conditions or equity caps. This list is regularly reviewed and updated to align with economic policy priorities, and investors must verify the status of intended business activities against the current Negative Investment List before committing to significant investment. Sectors historically restricted or limited include telecommunications, broadcasting, aviation, shipping, certain agricultural activities, and others where the government maintains strategic interest or protects local businesses. Some sectors may be available to foreign investors subject to conditions such as minimum investment thresholds, technology transfer requirements, local equity participation requirements, or operational constraints. Foreign investors contemplating entry into regulated sectors should engage Indonesian legal counsel early to assess restrictions and identify compliant structural alternatives.

Effective October 2, 2025, BKPM Regulation No. 5 of 2025 updated business licensing rules, capital requirements, and investment implementation procedures under the OSS-RBA (Online Single Submission—Risk-Based Approach) system, significantly reshaping the regulatory environment for foreign investment. A particularly significant regulatory change reduces the minimum paid-up capital requirement for PT PMA from IDR 10 billion to IDR 2.5 billion, substantially lowering the capital barrier for foreign investment in Indonesia, though the minimum investment value requirement of more than IDR 10 billion per 5-digit KBLI per project (including machinery, equipment, working capital, and other project costs) remains in effect. This regulatory change reflects deliberate government policy to facilitate foreign investor entry while maintaining substantial minimum investment thresholds. The regulation also introduces new capital lock-up requirements, prohibiting withdrawal of paid-up capital from company bank accounts for 12 months unless used strictly for operational or capital expenditure needs, creating operational planning considerations for foreign investors. These changes, while reducing entry barriers, create administrative and financial planning requirements that foreign investors must understand and prepare for in advance of market entry.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Indonesia provides multiple visa categories to accommodate different types of foreign entry and stay, administered by the Directorate General of Immigration (Imigrasi) under the Ministry of Law and Human Rights. The visa categories most relevant to business visitors and investors include the Business Visa (B211A), intended for short-term business activities and valid for 60 days with possible extension to 60 additional days; the Investor Visa (B211A), designed for foreign investors planning long-term business establishment and conferring longer validity; and the Visa on Arrival (VOA), available for citizens of approximately 92 countries, allowing a 30-day stay extendable to 60 days. Effective in 2026, Indonesia has expanded Visa on Arrival eligibility for additional nationalities while strengthening digital processing through online platforms (www.visa.kemlu.go.id) to streamline applications and reduce processing times to 24-48 hours in many cases. Foreign investors should note that visa processing timelines can vary, and applications should be submitted well in advance of intended travel, particularly during peak business travel seasons.

For longer-term residence, foreign nationals can obtain a Limited Stay Visa (KITAS—Kartu Izin Tinggal Terbatas), which can be extended annually and is typically obtained by transitioning from an initial temporary visa while in Indonesia or applying through an Indonesian sponsor entity. The KITAS is available for various purposes including investment, work, marriage to an Indonesian citizen, or family reunion, with the investor-class KITAS being the preferred vehicle for foreign entrepreneurs and business principals. Obtaining an Investor KITAS requires evidence of significant investment (minimum IDR 10 billion in approved sectors) or employment of Indonesian nationals. Immigration authorities have increased compliance monitoring for long-stay permits such as Investor KITAS and Work KITAS during 2025-2026, requiring applicants to maintain accurate documentation, regular status reporting, and compliance with residential requirements. Importantly, all visas must be sponsored by a locally licensed and incorporated Indonesian entity; foreign individuals cannot obtain visas independently, and must identify an Indonesian entity to serve as visa sponsor. Overstaying or working without proper visa authorization carries severe penalties including fines of IDR 500,000-10,000,000 or more depending on violation severity, deportation, and future entry bans that can significantly impact business operations and individual reputations.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Indonesia's land ownership regime is governed by the Basic Agrarian Law (Law No. 5 of 1960, as amended) and implementing regulations, which establishes a unique legal framework distinguishing ownership rights based on nationality and limiting foreign ownership of land. Under Indonesian law, all land is ultimately owned by the state; individuals and entities hold rights-of-use rather than absolute ownership. Indonesian citizens can hold ownership rights (Hak Milik) representing the strongest form of land tenure, while foreign nationals and foreign-invested companies are restricted to use rights, typically in the form of Hak Guna Usaha (Cultivation Rights) for agricultural land or Hak Guna Bangunan (Building Rights) for non-agricultural land. Cultivation Rights are granted for up to 35 years, renewable, and are suitable for agricultural, forestry, and mining operations. Building Rights are granted for up to 30 years, renewable, and are appropriate for manufacturing facilities, office buildings, warehouses, and hospitality properties.

Foreign-invested companies (PT PMA) can acquire Building Rights or Cultivation Rights through application to the Land Office (Kantor Pertanahan), which processes applications and issues rights certificates documenting the tenure arrangement. The process typically requires 30-60 days for applications submitted with complete documentation, though timeline extensions can occur if land documentation or ownership histories are disputed or unclear. Lease arrangements (Hak Sewa) of 25-30 year terms provide an alternative mechanism for securing land access, involving rental agreements with Indonesian landowners and avoiding the formal rights registration process, though with reduced legal security compared to formal rights registration. Foreign investors establishing manufacturing or commercial operations should engage land lawyers early to assess available land options, conduct due diligence on title and encumbrances, and navigate the formal rights application process. Disputes regarding land boundaries, prior claims, or community rights are not uncommon, particularly in less developed regions or areas with incomplete land records, making due diligence and community engagement essential components of land acquisition planning.

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Indonesia recognizes multiple mechanisms for resolution of commercial disputes, including litigation through the court system, arbitration through domestic and international institutions, negotiation and mediation, and in some contexts, customary dispute resolution processes. For international commercial disputes involving foreign parties, arbitration has become the preferred dispute resolution mechanism, as it provides neutral forum selection, protection against potential home-court bias in the domestic court system, and enforceability under international conventions. The Jakarta International Dispute Resolution Center (JICDC), established in 2018 as a specialized arbitration institution, administers international commercial arbitration cases using rules based on the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules, with proceedings conducted in English or Indonesian. The Indonesia National Board of Arbitration (BANI), established in 1977, similarly administers domestic and international commercial arbitration under its own rules, with substantially developed case law and arbitrator expertise in commercial disputes.

Indonesia is a signatory to the 1958 New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards, meaning that arbitral awards rendered in Indonesia or elsewhere are generally enforceable through Indonesian courts without substantive re-examination of the merits. This convention membership provides important protection for foreign investors concerned about enforcement of dispute resolution outcomes. The Indonesian court system also provides mechanisms for resolution of commercial disputes, with specialized commercial courts (Pengadilan Niaga) having jurisdiction over corporate disputes, breach of contract cases, and bankruptcy matters. Foreign investors must understand that court litigation in Indonesia is subject to potential delays, with cases at trial court level typically requiring 3-5 years or longer for resolution, though appellate processes provide additional remedies. Foreign investors establishing operations in Indonesia should consider arbitration clauses in major commercial contracts, with arbitration agreements to be governed by Indonesian law (as required for domestic enforcement) but conducted in English or another appropriate language.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Indonesia's economy encompasses diverse sectors offering distinct investment opportunities aligned with government development priorities, natural resource endowments, and market demand. The energy sector, including oil and gas, coal, and renewable energy, remains a significant investment area despite Indonesia's energy transition strategy favoring renewable sources. Downstream processing of natural resources—particularly the transformation of raw minerals including nickel, bauxite, and tin into higher-value products for battery manufacturing and industrial applications—has emerged as a priority sector with substantial government support and tax incentives. Electric vehicle manufacturing and battery production represent cutting-edge investment opportunities aligned with global electrification trends and ASEAN automotive integration, with government incentives and technical standards being actively developed for this nascent sector. Renewable energy generation, particularly solar photovoltaic and wind power projects, presents substantial opportunities given Indonesia's significant solar and wind resources and government targets for renewable energy expansion.

The digital economy, encompassing e-commerce platforms, fintech applications, cloud computing infrastructure, and artificial intelligence services, has emerged as one of Southeast Asia's largest and fastest-growing sectors, projected to reach USD 146 billion in value by 2025. This sector attracts substantial foreign investment from technology companies, venture capital funds, and established digital platforms expanding from neighboring markets. Food and beverage processing, pharmaceutical manufacturing, and agricultural value-added production align with Indonesia's agricultural endowments and food security priorities. The telecommunications, transport and logistics, and hospitality sectors continue to offer investment opportunities, though some sectors face regulatory restrictions or conditions affecting foreign ownership. Infrastructure development, particularly through special economic zones and the Nusantara capital relocation initiative, provides opportunities for construction, property development, and operational infrastructure services. Foreign investors should conduct sector-specific analysis to understand regulations, incentive eligibility, and market competitive dynamics before committing significant capital.

Manufacturing operations across diverse subsectors including automotive, electronics, textiles and apparel, machinery, and chemicals benefit from Indonesia's geographic location, labor cost advantages, and access to ASEAN markets through regional trade agreements. However, manufacturing investors should understand that labor regulations are strictly enforced, environmental compliance is increasingly rigorous (particularly regarding industrial waste and pollution), and supply chain logistics require careful planning given the archipelagic nature of the country. Strategic resource sectors including oil, gas, and mining remain significant but are subject to stringent environmental standards, community engagement requirements, and regulatory oversight through relevant sector ministries. The Nusantara IKN (Ibu Kota Nusantara) capital relocation project in East Kalimantan has spawned substantial infrastructure and property development opportunities, with government commitment to massive investment in new governmental facilities, worker housing, and supporting infrastructure creating long-term investment prospects in construction, property development, and commercial services.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

Indonesia's government maintains explicit policy frameworks welcoming foreign direct investment as a driver of economic growth, employment creation, and technology transfer, formalized through Law No. 25 of 2007 on Investment and administered by the Investment Coordinating Board (BKPM—Badan Koordinasi Penanaman Modal). The BKPM, now formally integrated into the Ministry of Investment, serves as the central body for investment coordination, policy formulation, business licensing facilitation, and investor support services. Foreign direct investment flows reached IDR 744.0 trillion (approximately USD 47 billion) in 2024, representing a robust increase of 13.8 percent from the prior year, with substantially higher flows realized through 2025 exceeding IDR 900 trillion (USD 53-55 billion).

The Indonesian government designates priority sectors for foreign investment that include downstream processing of natural resources (nickel, bauxite, tin for battery manufacturing), electric vehicle manufacturing and battery production, renewable energy generation and storage, digital economy and cloud technology infrastructure, food and beverage processing, pharmaceutical manufacturing, and major infrastructure projects associated with the Nusantara IKN capital relocation. Investment incentives available in 2026 include tax holiday programs ranging from 5 to 30 years depending on sector and location, investment allowance deductions enabling investors to deduct capital costs from taxable income, accelerated depreciation schedules for fixed assets, exemption from import duties on capital equipment and raw materials in specified sectors, and value-added tax exemptions on specific imported goods. Under PMK (Ministerial Regulation) No. 69/2024, the tax holiday grants corporate income tax reductions of up to 100 percent for domestic corporate taxpayers making new investments of at least IDR 100 billion, with exemption periods ranging from five to 20 years depending on investment size and sector. The Finance Ministry has signaled plans to extend tax holiday incentives into 2026, with adjustments to comply with global minimum tax rules, demonstrating continued government commitment to investment incentives despite OECD global minimum tax alignment requirements.

Infrastructure supporting foreign investors includes the Digital Business License System (PerBu Digital) under the OSS-RBA framework, enabling remote company registration and licensing for specified business activities from any location with internet access. The one-stop service approach through OSS aims to consolidate multiple licensing requirements into a single submission portal, reducing documentation burdens and processing timelines. Special Economic Zones (SEZs) distributed across Indonesia offer enhanced incentive packages including tax concessions, reduced import tariffs, and streamlined business licensing within designated geographic boundaries. These zones, established through government regulation and administered by zone authorities, typically offer 5-30 year corporate income tax holidays, import duty exemptions for capital goods and raw materials, VAT non-collection for domestic transactions, and simplified regulatory compliance procedures compared to operations outside zones.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

Indonesia has established multiple Special Economic Zones (Kawasan Ekonomi Khusus—KEK) across strategic locations designed to attract foreign investment, facilitate trade, and stimulate regional economic development. Major zones include the Batam Industrial Zone, serving as a gateway for electronics and light manufacturing; the Bintan Zone in Riau Islands; the Karimun Zone for manufacturing and logistics; and newer zones developed through the IKN relocation initiative and other strategic locations. These zones operate under dedicated regulatory frameworks distinct from general Indonesian law, providing streamlined licensing, enhanced tax incentives, and simplified customs procedures for zone-based operations. Foreign investors establishing manufacturing or logistics operations within zones can benefit from substantially accelerated licensing timelines, dedicated zone administration support, and certainty regarding incentive eligibility compared to operations outside zones.

SEZ incentive packages typically include 5-30 year corporate income tax holidays depending on investment sector and size, import duty exemptions for capital equipment, machinery, and raw materials for productive operations, value-added tax non-collection on goods and services purchased within the zone for zone operations, and reduced customs procedures for goods entering, exiting, and circulating within the zone. Goods imported into SEZs for production or re-export may benefit from VAT exemptions or non-collection, while companies may be eligible for import-duty exemptions on capital goods, machinery, and raw materials used in approved production activities. However, operations within SEZs must comply with zone regulations administered by zone authorities, maintain appropriate documentation for zone-related transactions, and adhere to operational requirements established by zone management, creating compliance obligations distinct from those applicable outside zones. Foreign investors should assess both the incentive benefits and administrative burdens of zone operations when evaluating site selection, as zones may offer substantial benefits for manufacturing and export-oriented operations but may be less advantageous for service businesses serving the domestic market.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Indonesia participates in multiple regional and bilateral trade agreements that significantly impact foreign investor market access and tariff considerations. The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) framework includes the ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA), establishing tariff reduction commitments among member states and preferential treatment for ASEAN-origin goods moving between member countries. The ASEAN Trade in Goods Agreement (ATIGA), effective since 2010, further liberalizes intra-ASEAN trade by reducing tariff and non-tariff barriers on most products, with substantially all tariffs on ASEAN products brought to zero by the early 2010s. Foreign investors establishing manufacturing operations in Indonesia can benefit from these agreements when sourcing inputs from ASEAN partners or exporting products to ASEAN markets, potentially reducing input costs and enhancing competitiveness in regional supply chains.

Major bilateral trade agreements provide additional preferential market access. Indonesia participates in the ASEAN Regional Forum Trade Agreement (ARFTA) with ASEAN's dialogue partners including Japan, South Korea, India, Australia, and others, establishing tariff reduction schedules for goods traded between parties. The Indonesia-Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (IJEPA), Indonesia-Australia Free Trade Agreement (IA-CEPA), and numerous other bilateral arrangements provide preferential tariff treatment for specified products, creating market access advantages for manufacturers operating in Indonesia. These agreements are particularly relevant for foreign investors in manufacturing sectors, as preferential tariffs can significantly improve cost competitiveness and market access compared to goods subjected to most-favored-nation (MFN) tariff rates. The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP), which entered into force on January 1, 2022 with Indonesia as a founding member, further integrates ASEAN with Japan, South Korea, China, Australia, and New Zealand, establishing substantially lower tariffs on goods traded between member economies over a 20-year implementation period.

Investors establishing supply chain operations spanning multiple countries should understand Indonesia's treaty network and how tariff preferences may affect production location decisions and sourcing strategies. Rules of origin requirements within these agreements mandate minimum local content thresholds or manufacturing processes to qualify for preferential tariffs, requiring investors to structure operations and source inputs to satisfy these requirements. Import tariffs on goods not subject to preferential agreements vary widely depending on the Harmonized System (HS) code classification, ranging from zero to over 100 percent for sensitive or protected sectors. Non-tariff barriers including technical standards, sanitary and phytosanitary requirements, and licensing restrictions apply to certain product categories, requiring importers and exporters to maintain compliance with evolving standards and obtain necessary certifications.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Indonesia's banking system is overseen by Bank Indonesia (BI), the central bank established under Law No. 23 of 1999 as amended by Law No. 4 of 2023, and the Financial Services Authority (OJK—Otoritas Jasa Keuangan), which supervises the broader financial services sector including banks, insurance companies, capital markets, and non-bank finance institutions. Bank Indonesia maintains authority over monetary policy, interest rate setting, banking sector liquidity management, and foreign exchange transactions and regulations. The Banking Law (Law No. 10 of 1998 as amended) establishes the regulatory framework for bank licensing, operations, and prudential standards. Indonesia's banking sector comprises state-owned banks (including Bank Nasional Indonesia, Bank Mandiri, Bank Rakyat Indonesia, and Bank Tabungan Negara), major private domestic banks (including Bank Central Asia, Bank Danamon, and others), foreign bank branches licensed to operate in Indonesia, and a growing number of digital banks offering fintech-enabled services.

On December 16, 2024, Bank Indonesia issued BI Regulation No. 9 of 2024 on Management of Foreign Exchange Traffic (Lalu Lintas Devisa—LLD), effective December 23, 2024, establishing comprehensive frameworks for managing foreign currency flows into and out of Indonesia. This regulation significantly enhanced Bank Indonesia's supervisory authorities concerning foreign exchange traffic, including authority to impose administrative sanctions for non-compliance with LLD reporting requirements, coordinate with relevant authorities on LLD matters, access relevant information systems, and implement enforcement measures against residents engaged in non-compliant LLD activities. Businesses engaged in export activities, foreign currency earnings, or international transactions must understand LLD reporting requirements, including reporting to Bank Indonesia of foreign currency transactions above specified thresholds, as Bank Indonesia increasingly monitors and enforces compliance to protect foreign exchange stability and macroeconomic resilience. Export proceeds must increasingly be converted to Rupiah within specified timeframes, though legitimate business purposes including operational expenses and cross-border transactions may be exempt from immediate conversion requirements upon authorization.

Foreign investors and businesses in Indonesia can maintain foreign currency accounts at authorized banks for legitimate business purposes, subject to regulations enforced through the LLD framework. The currency of choice for most business transactions is the Indonesian Rupiah (IDR), and foreign investors should be prepared to conduct most routine operations in Rupiah while utilizing foreign currency accounts for cross-border transactions and international payments. The exchange rate between the Rupiah and major currencies including the US Dollar, Euro, and Yen has demonstrated volatility, and investors should factor currency risk into financial planning and consider hedging strategies for substantial cross-border transactions. The OJK has enacted OJK Regulation No. 18 of 2025 on Transparency and Publication of Bank Reports, which came into force on August 4, 2025 with broader application beginning February 8, 2026, requiring enhanced transparency standards for banking sector reporting and financial system stability information. Interest rates set by Bank Indonesia have remained accommodative in 2024-2026, supporting credit growth and consumption, though monetary policy may shift depending on inflation pressures and macroeconomic developments.

4. Business Organisations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Indonesia provides several options for business entity structure, each with distinct characteristics regarding ownership, capitalization, governance, and liability implications. The two most common types for foreign investors are the PT PMA and PT PMDN, supplemented by representative offices for exploratory market activities and other specialized entities for particular business purposes.

PT PMA (Penanaman Modal Asing—Foreign Limited Liability Company) is the primary structure for foreign investors seeking to operate commercial activities in Indonesia. This structure allows 100 percent foreign ownership, subject to sector restrictions imposed by the Negative Investment List (Daftar Negatif Investasi). As of October 2, 2025, the minimum paid-up capital requirement has been reduced to IDR 2.5 billion (approximately USD 160,000), substantially lower than the previous IDR 10 billion requirement, making foreign investment more accessible to smaller companies and startups. However, investors should note that minimum investment value requirements of more than IDR 10 billion per 5-digit KBLI per project still apply, encompassing machinery, equipment, working capital, and other capital costs. PT PMA requires a minimum of two shareholders (individuals or legal entities, local or foreign), at least one regional director (domiciled in Indonesia, though not necessarily Indonesian national), and at least one commissioner. The structure provides limited liability protection, separating personal assets of shareholders from corporate obligations, and is the standard vehicle for permanent commercial operations, manufacturing, distribution, retail, services, and resource extraction activities. PT PMA must be incorporated through the Ministry of Law and Human Rights using the digital business licensing system (PerBu Digital) or traditional filing methods.

PT PMDN (Penanaman Modal Dalam Negeri—Domestic Limited Liability Company) is designed for Indonesian domestic investors and must be owned by Indonesian citizens or Indonesian legal entities as primary shareholders. While foreign nationals can technically hold minority interests in PT PMDN structures, control must remain with Indonesian parties through majority shareholding and majority directorship. This structure uses the same governance framework as PT PMA, including board of directors and commissioners, but serves primarily domestic investment purposes. Foreign investors considering PT PMDN participation should be aware that decision-making authority and control rest with Indonesian co-investors, creating governance constraints and limiting foreign investor authority. PT PMDN is commonly used when foreign investors partner with Indonesian parties for business ventures, with Indonesian partners maintaining legal control while foreign investors provide capital, technology, or operational expertise.

Representative Office (KPPA—Kantor Perwakilan Asing Bukan Cabang or K3PA—Kantor Perwakilan Asing) is appropriate for foreign companies seeking to explore the Indonesian market without engaging in direct commercial activities. Representative offices cannot directly undertake revenue-generating business activities, limiting them to market research, coordination with suppliers or customers, liaison activities, feasibility studies, and similar non-commercial functions. This structure requires minimal capitalization and provides a low-cost entry pathway for companies evaluating market opportunities before committing to permanent establishments. Representative offices must be registered with relevant authorities and must have at least one representative domiciled in Indonesia, typically a foreign expatriate or local professional hired specifically for that purpose.

Branch Office (Cabang—Kantor Cabang) represents an alternative to PT PMA for certain types of foreign enterprises, allowing direct operation of a branch of the foreign parent company rather than incorporation of a separate Indonesian legal entity. However, branch operations are available only for specific activities including banks, insurance companies, and certain other regulated sectors where sectoral laws permit branch establishment. Most commercial businesses utilize the PT PMA structure rather than branch operations due to limited availability of branch licensing.

Commandite Vennootschap (CV—Limited Partnership) remains available under Indonesian law but is generally not available to foreign investors and is predominantly used by small domestic enterprises. The CV structure provides one general partner with unlimited liability and one or more limited partners with liability limited to capital contributions, but this structure is uncommon in modern foreign investment contexts and is not recommended for foreign investors.

For the vast majority of foreign investors establishing permanent commercial operations in Indonesia in 2026, the PT PMA structure remains the preferred choice, offering clearly defined legal status, liability protection, access to all business sectors not expressly prohibited, capital requirements that have been substantially reduced, and comprehensive legal recognition under Indonesian law and international treaty frameworks.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Foreign investors establishing a PT PMA must navigate a multi-stage registration process involving corporate formation, business licensing, and tax registration. The initial step involves corporate registration with the Ministry of Law and Human Rights through the digital business licensing system (PerBu Digital) or traditional filing methods, submitting articles of association and incorporation documents specifying the company's name, registered office, shareholders, directors, commissioners, and business purposes. The corporate registration process typically requires 3-5 business days for processing when submitted through PerBu Digital, with approval generating the company registration certificate (Surat Tanda Daftar Perusahaan—STDP) and tax identification number (NPWP—Nomor Pokok Wajib Pajak).

Following corporate registration, the PT PMA must obtain business licensing (Izin Usaha) through the Risk-Based Online Single Submission (OSS-RBA) system, which consolidates multiple sector-specific licenses into a single submission portal. The OSS-RBA process requires submission of business plan documentation, facility descriptions, operational procedures, environmental impact assessments (for certain sectors), and proof of land/building rights. Processing timelines under OSS-RBA depend on the risk category assigned to the business, with low-risk businesses processed within 1-3 business days, medium-risk businesses within 5-10 business days, and high-risk businesses within 15-30 business days. Certain sectors including healthcare, aviation, telecommunications, and other regulated industries require additional approvals from sector-specific regulators, extending total licensing timelines. Foreign investors should budget for 2-8 weeks total for complete corporate and licensing establishment when factoring in potential document corrections and sector-specific approvals.

Following corporate registration and business licensing approval, the PT PMA must obtain tax registration from the Directorate General of Taxes, establishing the company's taxpayer status and obligation to file corporate income tax returns, value-added tax returns, and payroll tax reports. Bank account opening typically occurs in parallel with corporate registration, using the company registration certificate and articles of association as opening documentation. Employment authorization must be obtained if the company plans to employ foreign nationals, requiring separate applications to the Ministry of Manpower concurrent with company establishment. The comprehensive process, while simplified through the PerBu Digital system compared to legacy administrative requirements, demands careful coordination of multiple government agencies and meticulous document preparation to avoid delays or rejection.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

PT PMA companies operating in Indonesia must comply with corporate governance requirements established through company law, relevant financial regulations, and stock exchange regulations (if publicly listed). The Law on Limited Liability Companies (Law No. 8 of 1997 as amended by Law No. 40 of 2007) establishes the fundamental corporate governance framework, requiring each PT to maintain a board of directors responsible for daily operations and a board of commissioners responsible for oversight and supervision of directors. A general meeting of shareholders (Rapat Umum Pemegang Saham—RUPS) must be held at least annually to review company performance, approve financial statements, and make decisions on major corporate matters. The composition and structure of boards reflect the company's size and complexity, with larger or publicly-listed companies subject to more stringent governance requirements.

Foreign-controlled PT PMA companies must have at least one director residing in Indonesia, though this director can be a foreign expatriate or Indonesian professional employed as a director. The residency requirement, enforced through immigration and corporate records, obligates companies to maintain continuous directorship meeting this standard or face potential license suspension. Commissioners must similarly maintain appropriate qualifications, though detailed requirements vary by sector. Director and commissioner appointments, removals, and compensation must be documented through RUPS decisions and registered with appropriate authorities. The OJK has established enhanced corporate governance standards for certain sectors, particularly financial services and publicly-listed companies, including requirements for independent directors and commissioners, board committees addressing specific oversight functions, and regular governance disclosure and reporting.

Large foreign-invested companies, particularly those with significant employment or substantial sector importance, may be subject to government policy requirements regarding board composition, local professional development, technology transfer commitments, or other operational conditions established through investment licensing. Public companies (perusahaan terbuka) must comply with Indonesia Stock Exchange listing requirements, including extensive corporate governance standards, internal control requirements, independent auditor engagement, and quarterly/annual reporting to shareholders and the exchange. Foreign investors establishing PT PMA operations should establish corporate governance practices aligned with company size and regulatory sector requirements, maintaining clear documentation of board decisions, shareholder directives, and governance compliance.

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Indonesia's labor market is governed by the Labor Law (Law No. 13 of 2003 as amended) and numerous ministerial regulations issued by the Ministry of Manpower, creating a comprehensive framework addressing employment relationships, working conditions, wages, benefits, and dispute resolution. Normal working hours are set at 40 hours per week, which can be divided into eight hours per day for five working days or seven hours per day for six working days at employer discretion. Employees are entitled to a minimum rest break of 30 minutes after working four consecutive hours, and employers must provide approximately one hour of daily rest time as standard for employees working eight or more hours. Maximum overtime is limited to four hours per day and 18 hours per week, with overtime compensation calculated at premium rates typically 1.5 times to 2 times regular wages depending on timing (evening, weekend, or holiday overtime).

Indonesia's minimum wage system is decentralized, with each province setting its own wage floor reflecting regional economic conditions and cost of living variations across the archipelago. Employers must adhere to the applicable regional minimum wage, reviewed and adjusted annually by provincial governments typically effective January 1 of each year. In 2024-2025, the Ministry of Manpower introduced a sectoral minimum wage system through Minister of Manpower Regulation No. 16 of 2024, effective from 2025 and continuing through 2026, which applies different minimum wage floors to businesses in specific sectors based on work characteristics, risk profiles, specialization requirements, and physical demands. This sectoral approach allows higher wages for specialized or hazardous sectors while maintaining lower floors in less capital-intensive sectors. All agreed wage structures must be reported to the Ministry of Manpower for compliance monitoring, creating documentation burdens and regulatory exposure for employers with complex compensation structures.

Beyond minimum wage requirements, employers must provide mandatory benefits through four government-administered social security programs: worker health insurance (Jaminan Kesehatan Tenaga Kerja—BPJS Kesehatan), work accident insurance (Jaminan Kecelakaan Kerja—BPJS Ketenagakerjaan), disability and old age insurance (Jaminan Hari Tua—JHT), and death benefits (Jaminan Kematian—JK). Employer contribution percentages range from approximately 10-14 percent of wages depending on the programs, creating substantially elevated labor costs compared to stated wage rates. Termination of employment is heavily regulated, requiring valid cause (such as poor performance, disciplinary violations, restructuring, or health reasons) or payment of severance compensation meeting statutory minimums calculated based on years of service and final wage levels. Invalid termination or failure to provide required severance can trigger claims through labor courts resulting in significant financial liability and damage to company reputation. The Ministry of Manpower maintains active enforcement operations monitoring employer compliance with labor regulations, and violations can result in substantial penalties ranging from tens of millions to billions of Rupiah, forced remediation, and reputational damage affecting business operations.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Indonesia protects intellectual property rights through comprehensive statutory frameworks addressing patents, trademarks, copyrights, designs, and trade secrets, administered by the Directorate General of Intellectual Property (DGIP) under the Ministry of Law and Human Rights. Patents are regulated under the Patent Law (Law No. 13 of 2016 as amended), which recognizes two categories: regular patents protecting novel inventions involving inventive steps and industrial applicability (granted for 20 years), and simple patents for new inventions industrially applicable but lacking inventive steps (granted for 10 years). Patent applications are processed by DGIP within 30 months from filing for regular patents and normally approved for simple patents within 10 months, though applicants should anticipate longer timelines in practice, particularly for technical examinations. Patent prosecution timelines can extend to 3-5 years or longer if DGIP issues examination reports requiring substantive responses from applicants.

In October 2024, Indonesia enacted Law No. 65 of 2024, amending the 2016 Patent Law to align more closely with international standards and meet evolving technological needs. The amendment introduces simplified patent administration procedures to streamline filing processes and reduce bureaucratic barriers, making the patent system more accessible to both local and foreign applicants. These amendments facilitate more efficient patent prosecution, reduce administrative formalities, and align Indonesian practice with international norms, benefiting foreign investors seeking patent protection for technological innovations. Trademark protection is available under Indonesia's Trademark Law (Law No. 20 of 2016), which defines protected marks as signs including letters, numbers, compositions, symbols, figures, colors, combinations thereof, sounds, holograms, and three-dimensional forms used to distinguish goods and services. Trademark registrations typically require 3 to 6 months from filing, are valid for 10 years from registration date, and can be renewed indefinitely within 6 months preceding expiry. Copyright protection is automatic upon creation of literary, artistic, musical, and software works, with registration providing evidentiary benefits through the DGIP registry.

However, foreign investors should recognize that intellectual property protection challenges persist in Indonesia. The country remains on the United States Trade Representative's (USTR) Priority Watch List in the 2024 Special 301 Report, with widespread piracy and counterfeiting remaining pervasive—particularly in online marketplaces and through piracy-enabling devices and applications. Enforcement of IP rights often requires both administrative action through DGIP and civil litigation through specialized commercial courts, processes that can be time-consuming and require local legal expertise. Foreign companies should consider registering IP rights promptly upon market entry, monitoring for infringements actively, and maintaining documentation supporting originality and ownership claims. The ASEAN region has adopted the ASEAN Intellectual Property Rights Action Plan 2026–2030 (AIPRAP 2026-2030) as its strategic roadmap to strengthen intellectual property systems across the region and harness IP as a key enabler of innovation and growth, signaling commitment to strengthened IP protection across ASEAN jurisdictions including Indonesia.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Indonesia operates a comprehensive tax system administered by the Ministry of Finance through the Directorate General of Taxes (DGT—Direktorat Jenderal Pajak) and other tax authorities, generating substantial government revenue to fund public services, infrastructure investment, and social programs. Indonesia's tax system comprises direct taxes including corporate income tax (Pajak Penghasilan Badan—PPh Badan), personal income tax (Pajak Penghasilan Orang Pribadi—PPh OP), and capital gains tax, as well as indirect taxes including value-added tax (PPN), luxury goods tax (PPnBM), and various other taxes. The regulatory framework for taxation is established through the Tax Law (Law No. 8 of 1997 on Tax Procedures as amended), specific tax laws for each tax category, and numerous ministerial regulations issued by the Ministry of Finance and the Directorate General of Taxes providing detailed guidance on tax calculation, filing, and compliance procedures.

Indonesia has committed to alignment with international tax standards including base erosion and profit shifting (BEPS) initiatives and the OECD's global minimum tax agreement, establishing more comprehensive transfer pricing documentation requirements and reducing opportunities for tax avoidance through artificial profit shifting. Transfer pricing, arm's-length principle requirements, and country-by-country reporting obligations have been substantially strengthened in recent years, creating more stringent compliance requirements for multinational enterprises. The Indonesian tax system provides certain exemptions and incentives including tax holidays for priority sectors (as discussed in the investment environment section), investment allowance deductions, accelerated depreciation for capital equipment, and special tax regimes for special economic zones. Foreign investors should understand that while Indonesia's stated tax rates are relatively competitive compared to regional peers, effective tax rates after considering allowances, exemptions, and incentive provisions can be substantially lower for investors qualifying for incentive programs.

Compliance with Indonesian taxation is mandatory for all entities generating income in Indonesia, whether resident or non-resident, corporate or individual. Failure to comply with tax obligations carries substantial penalties including late-payment interest of 2 percent monthly, administrative penalties ranging from hundreds of millions to billions of Rupiah for material violations, and potential criminal sanctions including imprisonment for serious fraud or evasion. The DGT has invested substantially in digital compliance infrastructure including the e-SPT system for electronic tax return filing, digital payment platforms, and web-based services through the tax authority portal (www.pajak.go.id), modernizing tax administration and reducing opportunities for evasion while improving compliance monitoring capabilities.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

Indonesia levies corporate income tax at a standard statutory rate of 22 percent on corporate taxable income earned by all companies, whether foreign-owned or domestic. This rate has remained stable through 2025 and into 2026, as proposals for rate reductions have been shelved due to post-pandemic fiscal recovery requirements and substantial budget needs for infrastructure and development investments. The corporate tax rate of 22 percent applies to PT entities including both PT PMA (foreign-invested companies) and PT PMDN (domestic companies), calculated on worldwide income for resident companies and Indonesian-source income for non-residents. A company is considered a resident if incorporated under Indonesian law or managed and controlled from Indonesia, creating a test that foreign-owned PT PMA companies typically satisfy given that they are incorporated in Indonesia.

However, the standard rate is subject to important exceptions that can substantially reduce effective tax burdens for qualifying investors. Public companies (perusahaan terbuka) meeting a minimum free float requirement of 40 percent of shares listed on the Indonesia Stock Exchange and meeting other specified conditions are entitled to a 3 percent tax discount off the standard rate, resulting in an effective rate of 19 percent. This incentive encourages foreign investment in publicly-traded Indonesian companies and facilitates capital market development. Additionally, small enterprises classified as corporate taxpayers with annual turnover not exceeding IDR 4.8 billion (approximately USD 300,000) are entitled to a 50 percent tax discount applied proportionally to taxable income arising from gross turnover up to IDR 4.8 billion. This provides an effective rate of 11 percent on income within this threshold, supporting small business development and entrepreneurship, particularly for startup ventures and early-stage foreign investor operations.

Corporate taxes in Indonesia are enforced and administered by the Directorate General of Taxes (DGT) under the Ministry of Finance. Companies must register with the tax authority, obtain a Taxpayer Identification Number (NPWP—Nomor Pokok Wajib Pajak), and file annual corporate income tax returns (SPT Tahunan) reporting worldwide or Indonesian-source income depending on resident status. Losses may be carried forward for offset against future years' income for up to five years, providing relief for businesses experiencing operating losses in early years. Transfer pricing regulations align Indonesia with international standards, requiring controlled transactions between related parties to reflect arm's length pricing principles, with detailed contemporaneous documentation required supporting transfer pricing positions. The DGT has substantially expanded transfer pricing enforcement, with advance pricing agreements available to establish acceptable transfer pricing methodologies in advance of implementation, reducing compliance uncertainty for multinational enterprises.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

Individuals resident in Indonesia or earning Indonesian-source income face progressive personal income tax under the Individual Income Tax regime (Pajak Penghasilan Orang Pribadi—PPh OP), with rates ranging from 5 percent to 35 percent depending on income level and category. The non-taxable income threshold (Penghasilan Tidak Kena Pajak—PTKP) for 2026 is established at IDR 54,000,000 annually. This threshold includes an additional deduction of IDR 4,500,000 for a spouse/husband, and further deductions of IDR 4,500,000 per dependent child (limited to three children maximum), providing substantially larger exemptions for individuals with dependents. These thresholds are adjusted periodically to reflect inflation and economic conditions, and taxpayers should confirm current-year amounts with tax advisors.

Above the non-taxable threshold, progressive tax brackets apply, starting at 5 percent and increasing through intermediate brackets at 15 percent, 25 percent, and 30 percent, to a maximum marginal rate of 35 percent at the highest income levels. The 35 percent top marginal rate, introduced through progressive tax structure reforms, positions Indonesia's personal income tax system among the more progressive regimes in the Southeast Asian region, with higher income individuals subject to substantially elevated marginal rates. Non-resident individuals earning Indonesian-source income face a flat withholding tax of 20 percent on Indonesia-sourced income, applied on a final basis such that no further adjustment occurs through annual filing. The determination of resident status for personal income tax purposes follows specific criteria established by tax regulations, generally treating individuals physically present in Indonesia for 183 days or more during a 12-month period as residents subject to worldwide income taxation.

Foreign expatriates working in Indonesia on behalf of foreign-invested companies face complex tax planning considerations, with resident vs. non-resident status determination affecting significant tax burden variations. Employment income is subject to mandatory withholding (PPh 21—Monthly Income Tax) calculated by employers on each payroll cycle based on cumulative annual income and personal deductions. Proper withholding and reporting is essential to avoid compliance failures, as the employer bears primary responsibility for accurate withholding even if ultimately the employee bears the tax burden.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

Value Added Tax (VAT) is Indonesia's primary indirect tax applied to supplies of tangible and intangible goods and services. The statutory VAT rate is 12 percent, but the government has implemented an effective rate mechanism using an "Other Value as Tax Base" (Dasar Pengenaan Pajak Nilai Lain—DPP Nilai Lain) calculation resulting in an effective VAT rate of approximately 11 percent for most taxable goods and services. Luxury goods subject to government designation can face effective VAT rates of 12 percent. Exports of tangible goods, intangible goods, and services are zero-rated at 0 percent VAT, subject to specific limitations particularly affecting service exports. The VAT rate can be adjusted by Government Regulation within parameters of minimum 5 percent to maximum 15 percent following consultation with Parliament, though rate changes remain infrequent in practice. VAT registration is required once annual turnover reaches IDR 4.8 billion, and businesses must file monthly VAT returns, maintain detailed transaction records, and calculate VAT on output supplies less input VAT credits from purchases.

Sales Tax on Luxury Goods (PPnBM) imposes additional taxes on supplies of specific luxury goods including motor vehicles (particularly high-displacement vehicles), selected beverages (particularly alcohol), and other goods designated by government regulation. Luxury goods VAT rates can reach 12 percent as noted above, and PPnBM creates cumulative taxation on high-value consumer goods, effectively increasing the total tax burden on luxury purchases to 20 percent or higher in some cases.

Land Acquisition Tax (BPHTB) applies to acquisitions of land and buildings, calculated at a flat rate of 5 percent of the transaction value for buyers, with sellers paying income tax (PPh) at 2.5 percent on capital gains. Both buyer and seller must pay applicable taxes before notary signing of the acquisition deed (Akta Jual Beli—AJB). However, the government has granted transitional exemptions for certain property acquisitions, and foreign investors should verify current exemption eligibility for property transactions.

Stamp Duty applies to property and land-related documents at a standard fixed rate of IDR 10,000 (ten thousand Rupiah) per document under Law No. 10 of 2020 on Stamp Duty, effective January 1, 2021. While the nominal amount is minimal, stamp duty applies to numerous commercial documents creating procedural compliance requirements across transactions.

Land and Building Tax (PBB—Pajak Bumi dan Bangunan) is assessed annually by local tax authorities on the assessed value of land and buildings based on the Tax Object Value (Nilai Jual Objek Pajak—NJOP) established through periodic revaluation. Rates typically range from 0.3 to 1.0 percent of assessed value. This tax applies to all land and building owners, foreign and domestic, making it a regular ongoing obligation for property holding.

Vehicle and Motor Tax applies to ownership and operation of motor vehicles, with annual tax obligations based on vehicle type, engine capacity, and market value. Motor vehicle tax rates vary significantly by province but typically range from 1-2 percent of assessed vehicle value annually.

Import Tariffs apply to imported goods at various rates depending on the Harmonized System (HS) code classification, with rates ranging broadly from zero to over 100 percent for sensitive sectors. Foreign investors importing capital equipment may benefit from tariff exemptions or reductions under investment incentive schemes administered by BKPM for approved priority sectors.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

Indonesia provides multiple tax incentive programs designed to encourage foreign and domestic investment in priority sectors and locations. The primary mechanism is the Tax Holiday program established under PMK (Ministerial Regulation) No. 69/2024, granting corporate income tax reductions of up to 100 percent for corporate taxpayers making new investments of at least IDR 100 billion in pioneer industries or designated strategic locations. The tax exemption period ranges from five to 20 years, depending on the size of the investment, with larger investments qualifying for longer terms. After the tax holiday period expires, companies are typically entitled to a 50 percent corporate income tax reduction for an additional two years, providing transitional tax relief during the post-holiday period. The Finance Ministry has signaled plans to extend tax holiday incentives into 2026 through new ministerial regulation, adjusting the scheme to comply with global minimum tax standards while maintaining investment incentive support.

Investment Allowance deductions enable investors to deduct a percentage of capital costs from taxable income, reducing effective tax rates without requiring complete income exemption. Depreciation of capital equipment can be accelerated under specified circumstances, allowing faster cost recovery for machinery and equipment. Special Economic Zone (SEZ) incentives include corporate income tax holidays of 5-30 years, import duty exemptions on capital goods and raw materials for productive operations, and VAT non-collection on specified zone transactions. These SEZ-based incentives can be substantially more valuable than general investment incentives, particularly for export-oriented operations.

Small Enterprise Tax Rate provides a 50 percent corporate income tax rate (11 percent effective rate) for businesses with annual turnover not exceeding IDR 4.8 billion, supporting early-stage and small-scale operations. Public Company Discount reduces the corporate income tax rate from 22 percent to 19 percent for publicly-listed companies meeting minimum free float requirements, encouraging capital market participation and broader equity ownership.

The government has committed to aligning tax incentive structures with OECD global minimum tax standards, which establish a minimum 15 percent corporate tax rate for large multinational enterprises. This alignment may result in limitations on tax holiday programs exceeding 15 percent effective rates or other adjustments to ensure compliance with global minimum standards while preserving investment incentive structures.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Indonesia has entered into extensive Double Taxation Agreement (DTA) networks with major trading partners including the United States, Japan, China, Australia, Singapore, Germany, France, United Kingdom, Canada, South Korea, India, and numerous other countries, providing treaty relief reducing withholding tax burdens on cross-border payments. Treaty rates for dividends typically range from 10-15 percent (compared to statutory 20 percent), interest rates frequently reduce to 10 percent (compared to statutory 20 percent), and royalty rates typically range from 10-15 percent (compared to statutory 15 percent). Proper documentation, claims for treaty benefits (including completion of certificate of tax residence forms demonstrating beneficial residence status), and compliance with beneficial ownership requirements are essential for realizing treaty relief. Many DTAs require that treaty relief claim forms (Certificate of Tax Residence or similar documentation) be provided by the receiving party to support treaty rate application.

Transfer Pricing regulations align Indonesia with international standards through Law No. 8 of 1997 on Tax Procedures and implementing ministerial regulations. These regulations require that controlled transactions between related parties be priced at arm's length, comparable to transactions between independent parties in similar circumstances. Transfer pricing documentation requirements have been substantially strengthened in recent years, with businesses maintaining substantial transfer pricing files documenting methodologies, comparables analysis, and economic justifications for inter-company pricing. The DGT has authority to adjust transfer pricing positions determined to be inconsistent with arm's length principles, imposing adjustments, penalties, and interest charges on taxpayers when transfer pricing positions are deemed inadequate. Advance Pricing Agreements (APAs) with the DGT can establish acceptable transfer pricing positions in advance, reducing compliance uncertainty and dispute risk for multinational enterprises planning substantial cross-border transactions.

Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) applies to multinational enterprises with global consolidated revenue exceeding EUR 750 million, requiring that such enterprises provide information on income, profits, taxes paid, and other specified metrics by jurisdiction to the tax authorities. Indonesia participates in the automatic exchange of CbCR information under OECD standards, receiving information from parent companies' home country tax authorities regarding Indonesian operations of multinational groups.

Permanent Establishment (PE) provisions establish that foreign companies maintaining a fixed place of business in Indonesia through which business is conducted are subject to Indonesian corporate income tax on Indonesian-source income. The definition of PE under Indonesian tax law and applicable DTAs is complex, and foreign companies should assess whether business activities establish a PE requiring Indonesian corporate tax registration and filing obligations. Services rendered without a fixed place of business typically do not establish a PE, though exceptions apply, requiring careful analysis of specific business models.

5.7 Compliance & Administration

Corporate taxpayers in Indonesia must register with the Directorate General of Taxes using the taxpayer identification number (NPWP—Nomor Pokok Wajib Pajak) system, which provides the fundamental framework for all tax administration. The NPWP is issued upon first business registration and remains valid indefinitely, serving as the unique identifier for all tax filing and payment transactions. Annual corporate income tax returns (Surat Pemberitahuan—SPT) must be filed within 30 days following the calendar year end (by March 31 for year-end December 31), with late filing subject to administrative penalties. Monthly VAT returns must be filed within 20 days of each month end, with separate reporting for supplies, input VAT, and net VAT liability. Quarterly payroll tax filings are required reporting employee withholdings, while annual personal income tax reconciliation (SPT 1770) must be filed by individuals subject to progressive rate taxation.

Penalties for late filing and underpayment range from administrative penalties of 2 percent monthly interest to criminal sanctions for serious violations or fraud. The DGT has substantially expanded enforcement operations in recent years, increasing audit frequency for large taxpayers and those with complex transfer pricing or international transaction positions. The Indonesian tax authority has increasingly invested in digital compliance infrastructure, offering taxpayers online filing systems (e-SPT and e-Bupot for withholding tax reporting), digital payment platforms (e-Billing for tax payments through authorized banks), and web-based services through the tax authority portal (www.pajak.go.id). These digital systems have improved compliance infrastructure and reduced opportunities for administrative delays or document loss compared to legacy paper-based systems.

Foreign investors are well-advised to engage Indonesian tax accountants and advisors with expertise in transfer pricing, treaty benefits claims, and compliance best practices, as the DGT takes an increasingly sophisticated approach to enforcement and audit. Advance Pricing Agreements (APAs) can be negotiated with the DGT to establish transfer pricing positions in advance, reducing compliance uncertainty for multinational enterprises planning substantial cross-border transactions. Tax amnesty programs have been offered periodically by the government and may be relevant for businesses entering Indonesia or restructuring pre-existing operations, though such programs generally involve significant payment requirements and are available only during designated periods. Professional tax compliance management, particularly for multinational enterprises and complex business structures, is essential to minimize audit risk, penalties, and reputational damage.
