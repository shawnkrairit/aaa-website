Doing Business in Timor-Leste

1. General Information

1.1 Geography & Demographics

Timor-Leste, officially the Democratic Republic of Timor-Leste, is located in Southeast Asia, occupying the eastern half of the island of Timor and several smaller islands. The country is situated approximately 600 kilometers northwest of Australia and serves as a strategic gateway between the Indian and Pacific Oceans. The capital and largest city is Dili, located on the northern coast, which serves as the primary commercial, administrative, and diplomatic hub for the nation.

The country covers an area of approximately 14,874 square kilometers with a population of around 1.3 million people. Timor-Leste is one of the youngest nations in Southeast Asia, having achieved independence in 2002 after centuries of Portuguese colonial rule and a period of Indonesian occupation. The population is primarily composed of Timorese nationals, with diverse ethnic groups including the Tetum, Mambae, and other indigenous communities. The official languages are Tetum and Portuguese, with English increasingly used in business and government contexts, and Indonesian Bahasa serving as a working language in certain sectors.

The demographic profile reflects a young population, with a median age of approximately 19 years, presenting both opportunities and challenges for workforce development and economic expansion. The literacy rate has improved significantly since independence, though education infrastructure and skill development remain ongoing priorities for the government. Timor-Leste's geographic location, combined with its natural resources and strategic positioning within the ASEAN region, makes it an increasingly attractive destination for regional investment and trade integration.

1.2 Political System & Government

Timor-Leste operates as a semi-presidential democratic republic with a unicameral National Parliament comprising 88 members who serve five-year terms. The President serves as the ceremonial head of state and is directly elected, while the Prime Minister, as head of government, is appointed by the President and must retain the confidence of Parliament. The political system reflects Timor-Leste's commitment to democratic governance following its transition from Indonesian administration in 2002, and the country has held multiple successful elections demonstrating the maturation of its democratic institutions.

The government is structured with a Council of Ministers that oversees various portfolios including finance, petroleum, investment promotion, labor, and foreign affairs. Key ministries relevant to business operations include the Ministry of Finance, Ministry of Petroleum and Mineral Resources, Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Environment, and the Ministry of Labor. Governance has progressed significantly since independence, with various reforms aimed at strengthening institutional capacity, reducing corruption, and improving regulatory transparency. The government has established dedicated agencies such as TradeInvest Timor-Leste for investment promotion and SERVE (Serviço de Registo e Verificação Empresarial) for business registration.

In a historic development, Timor-Leste achieved full membership in the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) on October 26, 2025, becoming the bloc's 11th member state and the first new member in 26 years. This accession marks a significant milestone for regional integration and strengthens Timor-Leste's diplomatic and economic positioning. As an ASEAN member, Timor-Leste gains access to regional trade frameworks, investment promotion mechanisms, and enhanced cooperation with neighboring countries. The government has embarked on an ambitious Strategic Development Plan aimed at economic diversification and poverty reduction, with particular emphasis on developing non-oil sectors to ensure long-term economic sustainability beyond petroleum revenues.

1.3 Economy Overview

Timor-Leste's economy is characterized by its extreme dependence on hydrocarbon revenues, which the International Monetary Fund has identified as generating "the most oil-dependent economy in the world." Oil and gas production has historically accounted for approximately 95 percent of merchandise exports and provides the primary source of government revenue through the sovereign Petroleum Fund. The fund, established in 2005, had accumulated assets of approximately USD 18.95 billion as of early 2026, serving as a critical buffer against commodity price volatility and providing the foundation for government spending on infrastructure, education, and social services.

The Timorese economy experienced significant disruption in June 2025 when the Bayu-Undan gas field, which had been the country's sole operational hydrocarbon production facility and the primary revenue generator for two decades, reached the end of its productive life and was shut down. This development intensified debates about economic diversification and long-term sustainability, as oil and gas production is expected to decline further in coming years. The government is intensely focused on developing the Greater Sunrise oil and gas project in partnership with Woodside Energy, with a recent cooperation agreement signed in February 2026 targeting LNG facility development with production expected around 2028-2030. The project is designed to be processed onshore in Timor-Leste, providing substantial economic benefits and employment opportunities.

Real GDP growth was measured at 4.1 percent in 2024, driven primarily by infrastructure development and government spending supported by Petroleum Fund withdrawals. The 2026 General State Budget projects spending near 90 percent of GDP, with withdrawals from the Petroleum Fund accounting for approximately 86 percent of government revenue. However, the fiscal situation presents challenges, with projections indicating that substantial deficits could potentially deplete the Petroleum Fund by the end of the 2030s if current consumption patterns persist without significant economic diversification. The government has implemented a budget aimed at "transforming the economy" with targeted investments in non-oil sectors including agriculture, tourism, fisheries, and manufacturing. The currency is the United States Dollar (USD), which has been the official currency since independence, providing monetary stability and facilitating trade and investment transactions without the need for currency conversion and hedging.

1.4 Infrastructure & Connectivity

Timor-Leste's infrastructure has undergone substantial development since independence, though significant challenges remain in extending services to all regions of the country. The primary transportation hub is Nicolau Lobato International Airport in Dili, which has seen improvements in recent years and expanded air connectivity. In 2025, the long-delayed airport expansion project was initiated, bringing weekly direct international routes to Timor-Leste from Malaysia and China, alongside existing services to Australia, Indonesia, and Singapore. This expanded air connectivity facilitates business travel and supports the tourism sector, which the government has identified as a critical pillar for future economic growth.

Tibar Bay Port, located approximately 10 kilometers west of Dili, represents one of the largest infrastructure projects in Timor-Leste's history. Developed as the country's first public-private partnership, the port is operated by the Bolloré consortium under a concession agreement with the Government of Timor-Leste. The facility comprises a 630-meter wharf with a 15-meter draft accommodating both container and general cargo operations, with berths capable of handling 7,000 TEU and 3,500 TEU vessels respectively. The port's 29-hectare container yard provides capacity to handle one million containers annually, significantly improving Timor-Leste's maritime trade capabilities and supporting regional commerce. The project involved a total investment of USD 278.3 million and is expected to create approximately 500 local jobs.

Road infrastructure connects major municipalities, though conditions and connectivity to remote areas remain areas for improvement. The capital Dili is well-served with paved roads linking to other provincial centers, and ongoing infrastructure projects aim to enhance connectivity throughout the country. Telecommunications services have expanded significantly, with mobile and internet connectivity increasingly available in urban areas and progressively reaching rural communities. The telecommunications sector includes both fixed-line and mobile service providers, with 4G mobile coverage now available in major cities and expanding to secondary centers. Banking infrastructure is concentrated in Dili but is gradually expanding to other municipalities, with Banco Nacional de Comércio de Timor-Leste (BNCTL) operating 29 branches and over 63 ATMs across the country. Power generation primarily comes from diesel generators and renewable sources, with ongoing projects aimed at expanding capacity and improving service reliability, particularly in secondary cities and rural areas.

2. Legal System & Regulatory Framework for Foreigners

2.1 Overview of the Legal System

Timor-Leste's legal system is based on Portuguese civil law traditions, inherited from its colonial history and formalized through constitutional frameworks established at independence in 2002. Unlike common law jurisdictions such as Australia or Singapore, the Timorese legal system relies on comprehensive written codes and statutes that establish the rights, duties, and procedures governing civil, commercial, and criminal matters. Laws are enacted through Parliament and published in the Portuguese language, with subsequent translation into Tetum and English for broader accessibility. The foundational legal instrument is the Constitution of the Democratic Republic of Timor-Leste (Constituição da República Democrática de Timor-Leste), which was adopted in 2002 and establishes the framework for government authority, individual rights, and the hierarchy of legal instruments.

The civil law system is structured through several comprehensive codes, most notably the Civil Code enacted in 2011 (Código Civil), comprising 2,195 articles organized into five books covering general provisions, obligations and contracts, property rights, family law, and succession rights. The Commercial Code, the Penal Code, and the Code of Civil Procedure establish rules governing business transactions, criminal conduct, and dispute resolution respectively. The court system comprises District Courts located in major municipalities (including Dili, Suai, Baucau, and Oecusse) and a Court of Appeals with jurisdiction over appeals and certain specialized matters. The Supreme Court of Justice serves as the highest court and interprets constitutional and legal matters of national significance.

Significantly, Timor-Leste's legal framework incorporates customary law (lei dos usos e costumes) as a valid source of law, particularly in family matters, succession, and land disputes in rural areas. Traditional dispute resolution mechanisms administered by local leaders (liurais) and community elders continue to function alongside the formal court system, reflecting the practical reality that the state court system has limited reach in dispersed communities and reflects respect for indigenous traditions. The government has undertaken legislative and justice sector reforms to clarify and modernize laws, ensure accessibility to justice, and harmonize conflicting historical provisions inherited from Portuguese, Indonesian, and post-independence periods. Legal texts are available through the Government Portal and the Timor-Leste Legal Database, and the judiciary has been working to develop institutional capacity for consistent interpretation and application of laws across the country.

2.2 Foreign Investment Laws & Restrictions

The primary legislation governing foreign private investment in Timor-Leste is Law No. 15/2017 of August 23, 2017 (Private Investment Law), supplemented by Government Decree No. 2/2018 of February 21, 2018 (Regulation of Procedures for Private Investment). These instruments establish the legal framework, incentives, and conditions under which both domestic and foreign investors may undertake business activities in the country. The legislation guarantees full equality before the law for international investors, ensuring that foreign enterprises receive treatment equivalent to that accorded to domestic companies in equivalent circumstances, subject to specific sectoral restrictions and the investment promotion regime.

Foreign investment may be in the form of capital contribution (cash or goods), intellectual property rights, managerial capacity, or debt financing. The legislation requires that private investments be registered with TradeInvest Timor-Leste (the Investment and Export Promotion Agency) and comply with relevant sectoral regulations. Investments may be eligible for incentives under the Private Investment Law, including potential exemptions from certain duties and taxes depending on the sector, project size, and location. Investment approval is facilitated through TradeInvest Timor-Leste, which coordinates with relevant government agencies to provide guidance, support enterprise development, and process investment applications.

However, certain sectors face restrictions on foreign ownership and participation. Foreigners may own up to 100 percent of shares in most sectors, but petroleum and media communications industries restrict foreign ownership to less than 100 percent of shares, requiring local Timorese partners. Land ownership is absolutely prohibited for foreigners; however, the State may grant long-term land leases of up to 50 years for investment purposes, renewable for periods of 25 years up to a maximum of 100 years. This arrangement provides secure land use rights for substantial business projects while maintaining the principle that land ownership is reserved exclusively for Timorese citizens and legally constituted Timorese entities whose share capital is entirely held by nationals.

Additional restrictions apply to certain sensitive sectors, including defense, security, and state monopolies. All investors must comply with applicable environmental regulations, labor laws, tax requirements, and other statutory obligations applicable to their business activities. The regulatory framework has been designed to facilitate investment while preserving national interests, environmental sustainability, and social welfare. Recent developments include Timor-Leste's WTO accession on August 30, 2024, and ASEAN membership on October 26, 2025, which may influence regulatory development and trade-related investment conditions in the coming years.

2.3 Immigration & Work Permits

Foreigners wishing to enter Timor-Leste require appropriate visa authorization through the Immigration Service of Timor-Leste (Serviço de Migração de Timor-Leste). Various visa categories exist for different purposes, including tourist visas (typically valid for 30 days, extendable), work visas (valid for up to 12 months and extendable), temporary residence permits, and permanent residence permits. The work visa is the primary authorization allowing foreign nationals to enter Timor-Leste for employment purposes, either as employees, independent professionals, or business owners. Tourist visa holders are prohibited from undertaking remunerated work and are intended for persons entering for holiday or general visitation purposes.

Employment in Timor-Leste by foreign nationals requires both a valid work visa and a formal work permit issued by the Immigration Service. The work permit process typically commences after the foreign worker has entered the country on a valid work visa and requires employer sponsorship, as any employer seeking to hire a foreign national must be a legally registered entity in Timor-Leste and must demonstrate a genuine occupational need that cannot be satisfied by available local workers. Required documentation includes a valid employment contract from a Timorese employer, proof of accommodation in Timor-Leste, evidence of sufficient financial resources, a clean criminal record, a medical certificate confirming good health, and proof of a return ticket or sufficient funds to purchase transportation home. The processing period varies depending on application volume and case complexity, typically requiring several weeks to a few months for approval.

Foreign workers must also obtain a temporary residence permit, which is valid for up to two years and renewable. The temporary residence permit allows legal residence for employment purposes and must be obtained in conjunction with work permit renewal. Both work permits and residence permits typically require annual renewal as long as employment continues. The fees for visa and permit processing are modest, and recent reforms have aimed to streamline the application process through the Immigration Service offices, which maintain representatives in Dili and other major municipalities. Employers sponsoring foreign workers must comply with applicable labor laws, including wage and benefits requirements, workplace safety standards, and social security contributions. Non-compliance with visa and work permit requirements can result in deportation and potential business penalties for the sponsoring employer.

2.4 Land & Property Ownership

Timor-Leste's land law presents distinctive characteristics shaped by the country's complex historical transitions—from Portuguese colonial rule through Indonesian occupation to independence in 2002. The foundational principle, enshrined in Article 54 of the 1990 Constitution, reserves land ownership exclusively for Timorese citizens and legal entities organized and existing under Timorese law whose share capital is entirely held by Timorese nationals. This constitutional prohibition on foreign land ownership is absolute and may not be circumvented through corporate structures, trusts, or other mechanisms. The rationale reflects post-colonial nation-building objectives and the government's determination to preserve national control over this fundamental resource.

Foreign investors seeking to undertake substantial business projects may access land through leasehold arrangements with the Timorese State or authorized private landowners. State property leases are available for up to 50 years, renewable for periods of 25 years up to a maximum total of 100 years, providing sufficient tenure security for long-term business projects including industrial facilities, hospitality developments, and agricultural enterprises. Private leases may be available for shorter periods subject to agreement with the property holder. The Private Investment Law explicitly guarantees to investors the right to land use for project development and investment purposes, and the State commits to provide necessary land access through appropriate lease arrangements. Lease terms must be formally documented and registered to establish legal clarity and protect the investor's rights.

The land law framework remains complex due to overlapping historical claims arising from Portuguese colonial administration, Indonesian-era allocations, and post-independence restitution efforts. Law No. 13/2017 of June 5, 2017 (Law on the Recognition and Granting of First Ownership Rights on Immovable Property) entered into force on September 3, 2017, establishing procedures for determining and registering land ownership and use rights. This law seeks to clarify property status, resolve competing claims, and establish transparent title registration. However, implementation challenges persist, particularly in rural areas where informal occupation, customary tenure systems, and limited documentation complicate title verification. Prospective investors should conduct thorough due diligence on any land proposed for development, engage experienced local legal counsel to investigate title history, and ensure that any land lease is properly registered. Understanding local customary arrangements and consulting with community stakeholders is prudent to avoid disputes and ensure project acceptance.

2.5 Dispute Resolution

Timor-Leste provides multiple mechanisms for commercial and investment dispute resolution, reflecting the country's commitment to strengthening the rule of law and supporting business confidence. The domestic court system, structured through District Courts and the Court of Appeals, provides the standard forum for civil and commercial litigation. The courts apply the Civil Code and applicable commercial laws, with proceedings conducted in Portuguese and interpreted to other languages as required. The court system has jurisdiction over contractual disputes, property matters, commercial disagreements, and enforcement of judgments and awards. However, the judicial system faces capacity constraints, with limited institutional resources in some districts and potential delays in case resolution, particularly in provincial courts.

To address these limitations and support international business transactions, Timor-Leste has developed an international arbitration framework. The Voluntary Arbitration Law (VAL) applies to both voluntary domestic and international arbitrations conducted in Timor-Leste, the enforcement of national interim measures and arbitral awards, and the confirmation and enforcement of foreign interim measures and arbitral awards. This law provides a modern framework aligned with international best practices for neutral, binding dispute resolution outside the formal court system. Arbitration offers advantages including confidentiality, neutral decision-makers with technical expertise, finality of awards, and reduced timelines compared to litigation. Timor-Leste acceded to the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards on April 17, 2023, facilitating the enforcement of international arbitration awards within the country and enabling recognition of Timorese awards internationally.

In February 2024, Timor-Leste became the 123rd member state of the Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA), marking a significant development in its international dispute settlement capacity. The PCA provides institutional framework and administration for arbitrations between states and between states and private parties, as well as traditional interstate disputes. This membership enhances Timor-Leste's capacity to engage in international investment dispute settlement and demonstrates the country's commitment to international arbitration norms. For labor disputes, the Labor Court provides specialized jurisdiction over individual and collective labor matters that cannot be resolved through workplace conciliation or mediation mechanisms. The government has undertaken legal reforms to enhance judicial efficiency, provide legal aid services, and improve dispute resolution accessibility. Parties to commercial contracts may specify their preferred dispute resolution mechanism, including negotiation, mediation, arbitration, or litigation, allowing flexibility tailored to transaction-specific circumstances.

3. Investment Environment

3.1 Key Sectors & Opportunities

Timor-Leste's economy has historically been dominated by oil and gas production, but government policy increasingly emphasizes economic diversification into non-petroleum sectors to ensure long-term sustainability and employment creation beyond hydrocarbon dependency. Agriculture represents a significant opportunity, employing approximately 80 percent of the active population and encompassing both subsistence farming and commercial production. Coffee is one of the country's traditional export crops and has been the third-largest contributor to GDP over the past decade, with arabica and robusta varieties cultivated in the mountainous regions. The government is promoting high-value agricultural production, including cocoa, mangoes, and vanilla, alongside modern livestock farming and fisheries development. Irrigation systems, storage facilities, and agricultural value-chain development projects are receiving government and donor support to increase productivity and export competitiveness.

The tourism sector is positioned as a critical driver of future economic development and employment creation. Timor-Leste possesses diverse attractions including pristine beaches, mountainous landscapes, World War II historical sites, and rich cultural heritage embodied in traditional crafts, cuisine, and communities. The government has prioritized tourism development through infrastructure improvements, hospitality sector support, and marketing initiatives. The expansion of Nicolau Lobato International Airport and increased international air connectivity from 2025 onward are expected to facilitate tourism growth significantly. Hospitality investments, tour operator services, restaurant and retail development, and cultural tourism experiences all present opportunities for both domestic and foreign investors in this emerging sector.

Fisheries and aquaculture offer substantial potential given Timor-Leste's maritime location and extensive Exclusive Economic Zone. The government views fisheries development as important for food security, employment, and export revenue generation. Current fish processing, cold storage, and aquaculture projects represent investment opportunities aligned with food production and export objectives. The manufacturing and processing sector, while nascent, is targeted for expansion, particularly in agricultural product processing (coffee, cocoa, spices), seafood processing, and light manufacturing. The development of Special Economic Zones, particularly the ZEESM (Zone of Social Market Economy) in Oecusse, is designed to attract manufacturing and services investment through infrastructure provision and regulatory incentives. Energy sector development, including renewable energy projects and support for Greater Sunrise LNG development, presents opportunities in generation, distribution, and services supporting the hydrocarbon industry.

3.2 Government Incentives & Promotion

The government of Timor-Leste actively promotes private investment through TradeInvest Timor-Leste, a public institute tasked with investment promotion, investor facilitation, and export promotion. TradeInvest provides investment information, assists with regulatory navigation, coordinates with government agencies, and advocates for investor interests within government. The agency maintains an investor portal and maintains contacts with potential investors, particularly those interested in priority sectors identified in national development plans.

The Private Investment Law (Law No. 15/2017) provides a framework of investment incentives tailored to encourage specific sectors and project characteristics. Incentive structures may include exemptions from duties on capital goods and raw materials for specific industries, reductions in corporate income tax rates under certain conditions, exemptions from certain import duties, and accelerated depreciation allowances for capital investments. Incentive eligibility typically depends on project registration through TradeInvest, sector classification, investment amount, and location within the country. Large-scale projects and those located in designated priority regions may receive enhanced incentive packages. The government has also committed to providing land access through leasehold arrangements for qualifying investment projects, with terms negotiable depending on project scope and national interest considerations.

The development of Special Economic Zones represents another important incentive mechanism. The ZEESM in Oecusse has been established as a Special Administrative Region and Special Zone of Social Market Economy, with dedicated infrastructure development and regulatory flexibility designed to attract investment in manufacturing, services, tourism, and technology sectors. The Oecusse enclave, located in the western part of Timor Island and surrounded by Indonesian territory, offers geographic advantages and potential for regional hub development. Infrastructure development in Oecusse includes port facilities, airport development, bridge construction, irrigation systems, and hospitality infrastructure aimed at creating an attractive investment environment.

Tax incentives are available through the corporate tax framework, which provides a standard corporate income tax rate of 10 percent for most sectors, though specific rates apply to petroleum operations (30 percent for contractors) and sub-contractors (6 percent). Timor-Leste accession to the WTO and ASEAN membership create expectations for future tariff reduction commitments and potential expansion of trade preference arrangements. Targeted sectors including renewable energy, export-oriented manufacturing, and value-added agricultural processing may be eligible for enhanced incentive packages subject to project-specific evaluation. The government has also established a One-Stop Shop mechanism in Dili to streamline business licensing and permit processes, reducing administrative burden and time requirements for business establishment.

3.3 Free Trade Zones & Special Economic Zones

The ZEESM (Special Zone of Social Market Economy) in Oecusse represents Timor-Leste's primary Special Economic Zone development, established as a Special Administrative Region with dedicated governance structures and incentive regimes. Located in the Oecusse-Ambeno enclave in the western portion of Timor Island, the zone is geographically separated from mainland Timor-Leste by Indonesian territory but maintains maritime connections to Dili and regional ports. The zone's development strategy emphasizes integration of high-end tourism, entertainment services, technical support, and commercial activities, positioning it as a regional hub for business, investment, and tourism. Large-scale infrastructure development has proceeded, including construction of bridges, ports, international airport facilities, irrigation dams, and hospitality facilities designed to support zone operations and attracting both domestic and international investment.

The Oecusse zone benefits from special regulatory treatment compared to mainland Timor-Leste, including potential for streamlined business licensing, reduced tariff barriers for goods transiting through the zone, and infrastructure support provided through public investment. The zone's development is intended to create economic opportunities for local populations, facilitate technology transfer and skills development, and generate employment in services, hospitality, and manufacturing sectors. Access to zone facilities and investment requirements are administered through specific development authorities and regulatory bodies designated by the government to oversee ZEESM operations and investor relations.

Beyond Oecusse, the government is evaluating other potential zones and industrial parks to support economic diversification. Infrastructure development in Dili and surrounding areas aims to create manufacturing and services hubs supporting business operations and export-oriented production. The development of Tibar Bay Port enhances logistics and transport capabilities for zone operations, facilitating containerized cargo handling and general cargo operations. Free trade zone treatment, tariff exemptions for qualifying goods, and streamlined customs procedures provide cost advantages for manufacturing and export-oriented operations. Investors considering zone investments should engage directly with zone administrators and TradeInvest Timor-Leste to understand specific incentives, operational requirements, and administrative procedures applicable to zone activities.

3.4 International Trade Agreements

Timor-Leste accession to the World Trade Organization on August 30, 2024 marked a significant milestone for the country's trade integration and committed the country to compliance with WTO rules and obligations, including Most Favored Nation (MFN) treatment, National Treatment principles, and progressive tariff reduction commitments. WTO membership provides Timorese exporters with access to the rules-based international trade system and dispute settlement mechanisms, enhances predictability for importers and exporters, and aligns the country's trade policy with international standards. As a new WTO member, Timor-Leste is in the process of implementing various WTO agreements and commitments outlined in its accession package.

The current tariff schedule reflects uniform import duties of 5 percent of customs value, established in January 2023 to enhance government revenue collection and reduce import volume. The simple average Most Favored Nation applied tariff rate was 2.5 percent as of 2023, with a simple average final bound rate of 11.5 percent. Timor-Leste's tariff commitments as part of its WTO accession package establish the framework for future tariff negotiations and potential reductions, though specific implementation timelines and product-specific commitments reflect negotiations with other WTO members. The government maintains authority to apply tariffs within WTO-permitted ranges and may provide tariff exemptions or reductions for specific products, inputs, or priority sectors through designated mechanisms.

Accession to ASEAN as a full member on October 26, 2025 obligates Timor-Leste to work toward compliance with the ASEAN Trade in Goods Agreement (ATIGA), which provides for progressive tariff reduction among ASEAN members. ATIGA elimination of tariffs on most products is expected to occur over a transition period, with implementation timelines varying by product classification and the country's development level. As a new ASEAN member, Timor-Leste may be granted extended implementation periods for tariff reduction commitments and may qualify for special and differential treatment recognizing the country's development status. ASEAN membership also opens opportunities for participation in the ASEAN Economic Community (AEC) framework, facilitating trade in services, investment flows, and movement of skilled professionals across ASEAN member states.

The government is evaluating additional trade and investment partnerships, including potential participation in the EU-Pacific Economic Partnership Agreements and other regional trade arrangements. Trade agreements at bilateral, regional, and multilateral levels will progressively shape Timor-Leste's trade policy, tariff treatment, and regulatory frameworks applicable to goods and services. Investors should monitor developments in international trade negotiations and tariff commitments, as these may affect tariff exposure on imported inputs, competitive positioning of exports, and regulatory requirements for operating in Timor-Leste. The Trade Information Portal maintained by the Customs Authority provides comprehensive information on tariffs, regulations, and procedures governing international trade.

3.5 Banking & Finance

Timor-Leste's banking and financial sector is concentrated but expanding, with Banco Nacional de Comércio de Timor-Leste (BNCTL) serving as the sole domestically incorporated commercial bank and the primary financial institution for the country. BNCTL was established in 2011 as the country's first state-owned commercial bank and has expanded operations substantially, currently operating 29 branches and units across all 12 municipalities including the Regional Autonomous Entity of Oecusse-Ambeno (RAEOA) and Ataúro Island. The bank operates an extensive ATM network with over 63 ATMs and approximately 229 point-of-sale terminals, with 45 agent banks facilitating financial access throughout the country. As of September 2022, BNCTL served approximately 380,000 customers, holding a dominant 73 percent market share of domestic banking services.

International banks maintain representative offices or limited operations in Timor-Leste, including operations serving petroleum industry clients and multinational enterprises. Foreign banks may establish branches or subsidiaries in Timor-Leste subject to regulatory authorization by the Banco Central de Timor-Leste (BCTL), the country's central bank established as the monetary authority. Banking regulations and supervision fall under BCTL authority, which implements prudential requirements, liquidity standards, and consumer protection measures aligned with international banking standards. International payment systems, remittance services, and correspondent banking relationships facilitate cross-border transactions, though availability of certain specialized financial services may be limited given the country's emerging market status.

The official currency is the United States Dollar (USD), which Timor-Leste adopted at independence in 2002 to provide monetary stability without the costs and complexities of establishing a separate national currency. The absence of a central bank monetary policy framework reflects the "dollarized" economy, where monetary conditions are influenced by broader U.S. monetary policy and international financial markets rather than domestic central bank decisions. This arrangement eliminates currency exchange risk for dollar-denominated transactions and facilitates trade with Australia and other major regional partners, though it limits policy flexibility for managing inflation or employment objectives through monetary means.

Financial services for business operations include trade finance, working capital financing, commercial loans, and letter-of-credit services primarily through BNCTL and international correspondent banks. Foreign investors seeking to establish bank accounts, arrange financing, or undertake financial transactions should engage directly with BNCTL or international banking partners to understand available services, documentation requirements, and transaction costs. Access to project financing for substantial investments may require engagement with development finance institutions, multilateral development banks, or international commercial lenders operating in the region. The government has committed to improving financial sector infrastructure, developing capital markets, and broadening access to financial services as part of its economic development agenda, with ongoing reforms and capacity development initiatives aimed at supporting this objective.

4. Business Organizations

4.1 Types of Business Entities

Timor-Leste's legal framework provides for several distinct business entity types suitable for commercial operations, allowing investors to select organizational structures appropriate for their business objectives, ownership structure, and operational requirements. The primary entity types recognized under Timorese company law include the Single Shareholder Limited Liability Company (Unipessoal Lda.), Limited Liability Company (Lda.), Joint Stock Company (Société Anonyme or S.A.), Foreign Branch/Representative Office, and State Owned Enterprise (SOE). Each entity type is governed by applicable provisions of the Companies Code and relevant legislative instruments, with specific requirements regarding formation, capitalization, governance, and operational procedures.

The Single Shareholder Limited Liability Company (Unipessoal Lda.) is suitable for sole proprietorships or single-owner ventures and requires a minimum of one shareholder and one director, though the same individual may hold both positions. The entity must maintain minimum share capital of USD 5,000 and maximum capitalization of USD 5,000,000. This structure provides limited liability protection, separating personal assets from business obligations, while maintaining simplified governance requirements. The Limited Liability Company (Lda.) is the most commonly utilized structure for multi-owner ventures and small-to-medium enterprises, requiring between two and thirty shareholders with minimum capital of USD 5,000 and maximum capital of USD 5,000,000. Shareholders enjoy limited liability protection, and the company is managed by directors appointed by shareholders, with governance specified through a partnership agreement or operational bylaws.

The Joint Stock Company (Société Anonyme or S.A.) is designed for larger enterprises with greater capital requirements and more complex ownership structures. This entity type requires a minimum of three shareholders, minimum share capital of USD 50,000, and no maximum capitalization limit, allowing for scalable ownership structures and public share offerings if desired. Joint Stock Companies are governed by a Board of Directors elected by shareholders and subject to more comprehensive corporate governance requirements including disclosure obligations, audit requirements, and shareholder meeting procedures. Foreign companies may establish a Branch (Sucursal), Permanent Representative (Representante Permanente), or Subsidiary (Filial) in Timor-Leste, allowing corporate entities to conduct business in the country through a local operating entity without requiring a separate local incorporation, depending on the entity structure selected.

Selection of the appropriate entity type depends on factors including ownership structure, capital requirements, anticipated growth trajectory, governance preferences, and tax planning objectives. Prospective investors should consult with local legal counsel specializing in corporate law to evaluate entity options and ensure compliance with registration requirements, ongoing operational obligations, and tax implications of each structure.

4.2 Company Registration Process

Company registration in Timor-Leste is administered by SERVE (Serviço de Registo e Verificação Empresarial), the government agency responsible for business licensing, registration, and verification of business entities. The registration process is designed to be relatively straightforward, with a One-Stop Shop mechanism in Dili providing centralized processing of applications and coordination with relevant government agencies. The typical registration process commences with preparation of company documentation including articles of incorporation, bylaws or partnership agreement, shareholder identification, director designation, and registered office information. Documentation must be prepared in Portuguese, with certified translations provided for documents in other languages.

Initial steps include name registration, whereby the proposed company name is verified for uniqueness and compliance with naming conventions. The applicant must confirm that the proposed name is not identical to or confusingly similar to existing registered businesses and does not violate restrictions applicable to state monopolies or regulated industries. Following name approval, the applicant files the formal incorporation documents with SERVE, including the articles of incorporation establishing the company's purpose, share capital structure, shareholder information, director/manager identification, and governance provisions. The articles must be signed by all shareholders or their authorized representatives and must be notarized or authenticated according to legal requirements.

Concurrent with SERVE registration, the company must obtain a Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) from the Autoridade Tributária Timor-Leste (Tax Authority) and may be required to register with the Social Security Fund if the company will employ workers. The process typically requires 5-15 business days from submission of complete documentation to issuance of a Certificate of Registration, though timelines may vary depending on application volume and completeness of documentation. The registration fee is modest, typically in the range of several hundred USD, with modest administrative charges for name registration and certificate issuance. Once registered, the company receives formal registration documentation, a company seal (if desired), and authorization to commence business operations. Subsequent registration of changes including additional shareholders, director changes, registered office relocation, or capital alterations require amendment applications filed with SERVE.

Foreign investors establishing companies in Timor-Leste should engage experienced local corporate counsel to guide the registration process, ensure compliance with requirements, and verify that all documentation is properly prepared and submitted. The One-Stop Shop mechanism in Dili can provide significant convenience for investors physically present in-country, though registration can also be facilitated through local legal representatives for remote applicants.

4.3 Corporate Governance Requirements

Timor-Leste's corporate governance framework establishes obligations for company management, shareholder communication, financial reporting, and compliance with statutory requirements. The governance structure varies depending on the entity type selected, with Limited Liability Companies typically having simpler requirements compared to Joint Stock Companies. For Limited Liability Companies, governance is established through partnership agreements and director resolutions, with day-to-day management typically conducted by designated directors or managers. Shareholder decisions on major matters including capital changes, profit distribution, director appointment/removal, and dissolution are typically made through shareholder meetings. For Joint Stock Companies, governance requires establishment of a Board of Directors elected by shareholders, with board members responsible for strategic direction, operational oversight, and fiduciary duties to the company and shareholders.

Financial reporting obligations apply to all registered business entities, requiring annual preparation of financial statements in accordance with applicable accounting standards. Companies must maintain accounting records documenting transactions, assets, liabilities, and equity positions. Annual financial statements typically include a balance sheet, income statement, and notes to financial statements, with submission requirements varying based on company size and applicable tax regulations. Larger companies or those in regulated sectors may be subject to audit requirements by independent accountants. Tax filing is mandatory annually, with corporate income tax returns and supporting documentation submitted to the Tax Authority by specified deadlines, typically within 90 days of the fiscal year end.

Compliance with labor laws is a critical governance obligation, including maintenance of employment contracts, payroll records, and social security contributions for all employees. Companies must comply with minimum wage requirements, working hour limitations, overtime compensation rules, and workplace safety standards. Compliance with regulatory obligations specific to the company's business sector, including environmental regulations, licensing requirements, and specialized operational standards, is mandatory. Companies must also maintain proper corporate records including shareholder registers, director records, board meeting minutes, and financial documentation, with retention requirements typically specified by law or applicable regulations. Non-compliance with governance requirements can result in penalties, administrative sanctions, or enforcement actions against company officers and directors.

4.4 Employment & Labour Laws

Timor-Leste's labor framework is governed principally by the Labour Code of 2012, which establishes comprehensive rules governing employment relationships, worker rights, employer obligations, compensation, benefits, and dispute resolution. The Labour Code applies to all workers in Timor-Leste, including both Timorese nationals and foreign workers, and is aligned with International Labour Organization (ILO) standards and conventions. The legislation establishes minimum protections and requirements applicable to all employment relationships, with specific provisions addressing contract formation, wages, working conditions, social security, dispute resolution, and termination of employment.

Employment relationships in Timor-Leste must be evidenced by written employment contracts specifying material terms including job duties, compensation, benefits, working hours, and termination conditions. In the event of disputes regarding the existence or terms of oral employment arrangements, the employer bears the burden of proving the absence of a contract or the agreed terms, creating a presumption of employment if a work relationship is established. The standard working week is 44 hours, with a standard working day of 8 hours. Overtime compensation is required at 150 percent of regular hourly wages, with compensation at 200 percent of regular wages required for work on public holidays or designated rest days. Employees are entitled to at least one rest day per week and paid annual leave of at least 15 days annually.

Minimum wage requirements are established by government regulation and adjusted periodically based on economic conditions. As of recent updates, minimum wages vary by sector and experience level, with specific rates applicable to agricultural workers, service sector workers, and other classifications. Employers are obligated to provide all statutory benefits including social security contributions (employer contribution of 6 percent of gross salary) and worker compensation insurance. Employees contribute 4 percent of gross salary to the social security fund, with amounts withheld by the employer from wages. Termination of employment is governed by specific procedures and requires valid cause in most circumstances, with notice periods and severance compensation specified by the Labour Code. Procedural protections apply to termination, including notice requirements and opportunities for the employee to respond to proposed termination.

Foreign workers employed in Timor-Leste must obtain valid work permits prior to commencing employment, with employer sponsorship required for work permit applications. Employers sponsoring foreign workers assume responsibility for compliance with visa and permit requirements and must provide documented evidence of employment necessity and worker capacity. Non-compliance with labor laws, including failure to maintain written contracts, non-payment of wages, failure to remit social security contributions, or violations of working condition requirements, can result in substantial penalties, administrative action, or criminal liability for responsible managers. Labor disputes are resolved through the Labor Court, with jurisdiction over individual and collective labor matters not resolved through workplace mechanisms. Employees alleging wage theft, unlawful termination, or other labor violations may file complaints with the Labor Court, which has authority to order remedies including wage restoration, reinstatement, or compensation for damages.

4.5 Intellectual Property Protection

Intellectual property protection in Timor-Leste remains under development, with formal legislative frameworks for trademark registration, patent protection, and copyright protection still being established. Currently, trademark registration through a formal governmental registry is not available in Timor-Leste. Instead, proprietors seeking to protect trademarks, logos, and other intellectual property marks must utilize a Cautionary Notice system, whereby the mark owner publishes notice of ownership in a recognized local newspaper to assert rights and provide public notice of claimed ownership. The published notice establishes a date-certain evidence of the owner's assertion of rights and may provide certain evidentiary advantages in disputes, though it does not confer legal registration or exclusive rights comparable to those provided by formal trademark registration systems.

Cautionary Notices must clearly identify the mark, the owner's name and contact information, a description of the goods or services associated with the mark, and a statement of claimed rights. Once published, the notice serves to notify the public and potentially third parties of the claimed right, and may deter others from adopting confusingly similar marks. However, absent formal legislative protection, enforcement of Cautionary Notice rights may be limited to contractual claims, unfair competition principles, or passing-off doctrines applicable in civil proceedings. The government of Timor-Leste is developing comprehensive intellectual property legislation that is expected to establish formal trademark registration, patent protection, and copyright frameworks aligned with international standards and treaty obligations. When formal registration systems become available, marks protected through Cautionary Notices are expected to receive priority consideration during formal registration processes.

Copyright protection in Timor-Leste is currently limited, with the country not a member of the Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works. Consequently, the International Copyright Office cannot provide guarantees regarding copyright protection claims for works created in Timor-Leste, either within the country or internationally. The government has enacted a Copyright Law (Law No. 9/2022) establishing certain protections for original works and authors' rights, though application and enforcement mechanisms remain developing. Businesses seeking to protect intellectual property should consult with intellectual property specialists regarding available protection mechanisms, registration strategies in jurisdictions where products will be marketed or manufactured, and contractual protections through confidentiality agreements, non-compete clauses, and licensing arrangements.

Registration of intellectual property in jurisdictions where the business operates (including Australia, Singapore, Indonesia, or other significant markets) provides superior protection compared to reliance on Timorese protections. International protection through the Madrid System for trademarks, the Patent Cooperation Treaty, or other multilateral arrangements provides access to protection frameworks outside Timor-Leste. Businesses are advised to develop comprehensive intellectual property strategies including registration in key markets, maintenance of trade secret protections, and contractual safeguards protecting proprietary information and business methods.

5. Taxation

5.1 Overview of the Tax System

Timor-Leste's tax system is administered by the Autoridade Tributária Timor-Leste (ATTL), the Tax Authority responsible for tax policy implementation, revenue collection, and taxpayer compliance. The tax framework comprises several principal taxes including corporate income tax (CIT), personal income tax (PIT), wage income tax (WIT), supplemental petroleum tax (SPT), import duties, and various other fees and levies. The system is structured to provide government revenue while creating conditions for business expansion and investment. Taxpayers are required to maintain detailed records, file periodic returns, and remit taxes by specified deadlines. The Tax Authority issues guidance on tax obligations, administers dispute resolution procedures for tax controversies, and conducts audits to ensure compliance.

All persons and legal entities earning income from Timorese sources are subject to applicable income taxation, with specific rates and treatment depending on the taxpayer's resident status, income type, and sector of operation. Resident individuals generally benefit from more favorable treatment compared to non-residents, reflecting government policy supporting local workforce development. The concept of "resident" for tax purposes is based on physical presence, with individuals present in Timor-Leste for more than 183 days in a calendar year typically classified as residents. Income sources include employment compensation, business profits, investment returns, and capital gains, with varying tax treatment applicable to each. The tax system permits deduction of business expenses for profit determination, though specific deduction rules vary depending on income type and applicable provisions.

Tax planning is an important consideration for business operations, with careful structuring of employment arrangements, profit distribution, and investment timing potentially achieving tax efficiency within legal constraints. However, aggressive tax avoidance schemes or transactions structured primarily to avoid taxation may be challenged by tax authorities under substance-over-form principles or anti-abuse provisions. Businesses operating in Timor-Leste should maintain comprehensive records, ensure accurate tax reporting, and consult with tax advisors regarding obligations and optimization strategies within legal parameters. The Tax Authority publishes guidance on tax obligations, provides taxpayer services, and maintains a taxpayer registry through which all registered businesses and individual workers must register to obtain Taxpayer Identification Numbers (TINs) required for tax filing and remittance obligations.

5.2 Corporate Income Tax

The standard corporate income tax (CIT) rate for businesses operating in Timor-Leste is a flat 10 percent on taxable income. This rate applies to the net income of companies, calculated as gross revenue less allowable business deductions including operating expenses, depreciation, cost of goods sold, and other ordinary business expenses. The 10 percent rate is among the lowest in the ASEAN region and is designed to attract business investment and support business profitability. Taxable income is determined in accordance with accrual-basis accounting principles, with income recognized when earned (regardless of receipt) and expenses recognized when incurred (regardless of payment), subject to modifications for tax purposes.

However, specific sectors are subject to different CIT rates reflecting policy objectives or resource extraction considerations. The petroleum sector is subject to special taxation treatment, with oil and gas contractors subject to a CIT rate of 30 percent on profits, substantially higher than the general business rate. Petroleum sub-contractors may benefit from a lower 6 percent CIT rate under certain circumstances, reflecting government policy to support service suppliers and sub-contracting relationships in the petroleum sector. In addition to CIT, petroleum contractors are subject to Supplemental Petroleum Tax (SPT) on accumulated net receipts, calculated using a progressive formula that captures greater shares of profits as project economics improve.

Corporate income tax returns must be filed annually by registered companies, typically within 90 days following the end of the fiscal year. The fiscal year generally corresponds to the calendar year (January through December), though companies may request alternative fiscal year periods subject to Tax Authority approval. Returns must include detailed financial statements, calculation of taxable income with supporting schedules, and calculation of tax liability. Timely payment of corporate income tax is required, with installment payment options available for larger taxpayers. Penalties apply to late filing or late payment, with interest accruing on unpaid amounts at rates specified by tax regulations. The Tax Authority conducts audits of corporate returns on a periodic basis, with authority to assess additional taxes, penalties, and interest if non-compliance is discovered.

5.3 Personal Income Tax

Timorese residents earning employment income are subject to progressive wage income tax (WIT) with marginal tax rates increasing as income levels rise. The tax framework includes a basic exemption threshold below which no tax is assessed, with rates applied only to income exceeding the exemption level. For example, a resident employee earning USD 800 per month might enjoy a USD 500 monthly exemption, with the remaining USD 300 (800 - 500) subject to the applicable marginal rate of 10 percent, resulting in approximately USD 30 in monthly tax liability. Marginal tax rates increase with income levels, typically progressing in bands with higher income subject to higher rates, though specific rate schedules are specified in applicable tax regulations and adjusted periodically.

Non-resident individuals earning income from Timorese sources are subject to a flat 10 percent personal income tax rate on all Timorese-sourced income, with no exemptions or graduated rate treatment. This higher effective rate reflects government policy prioritizing taxation of foreign-sourced income and is applied by employers through withholding mechanisms. Employers are obligated to withhold wage income tax at applicable rates from employee compensation and remit withheld amounts to the Tax Authority on a monthly basis. The withholding obligation applies to all wage payments, including salaries, bonuses, commissions, and other employment-related compensation.

Self-employed individuals and independent professionals earning income in Timor-Leste are subject to personal income tax on net business profits at rates applicable to residents or non-residents depending on resident status. Deductible business expenses are allowed, similar to corporate tax treatment, with depreciation, materials, labor, and other ordinary business expenses deductible in calculating taxable income. Quarterly estimated tax payments may be required for self-employed taxpayers with substantial income, with annual reconciliation occurring upon filing of the annual personal income tax return. Investment income including interest, dividends, and capital gains may be subject to separate taxation at specified rates, with certain investments potentially enjoying preferential tax treatment.

5.4 Other Taxes & Duties

In addition to income taxation, Timor-Leste imposes import duties on imported goods, with a uniform tariff rate of 5 percent of customs value applied to most imported merchandise as of 2023. This rate was established through government decree to enhance revenue collection and discourage excessive imports, replacing a prior uniform rate of 2.5 percent. Specific goods may be subject to differential tariff treatment, with certain products facing higher or lower rates, and tariffs may be temporarily suspended or reduced for goods of strategic importance or in response to supply disruptions. Capital goods, raw materials, and intermediate products may be eligible for tariff exemptions under investment incentive regimes, with exemption availability dependent on project registration and classification of goods as qualifying inputs.

Import duties are assessed on goods at the port of entry or designated import processing facilities, based on declared customs value (typically the CIF value—cost, insurance, and freight). Duty assessment procedures comply with Customs Code requirements, with importers responsible for declaring goods, providing supporting documentation, and paying assessed duties prior to clearance. Customs brokers typically facilitate import procedures, declaration preparation, and duty payment. Other import-related charges include administrative fees and port charges, which may apply in addition to tariff duties.

Excise taxes may apply to specific goods including alcoholic beverages, tobacco products, and certain imported luxury items, with rates specified by government regulation. Sales tax or value-added tax is not currently applied in Timor-Leste, reflecting the simplified tax structure. Business licensing fees and various regulatory charges may apply to specific industries, including telecommunications, hospitality, and other regulated sectors. Property transfer taxes may apply to real property transactions, with rates specified by applicable provisions. Payroll-related taxes include social security contributions (employer 6 percent, employee 4 percent as discussed under labor law), which are separate from income taxation but represent significant labor costs for employers.

5.5 Tax Incentives & Exemptions

The Private Investment Law provides a framework of tax incentives designed to encourage investment in priority sectors and geographic regions. Qualifying investment projects may be eligible for exemptions from duties and taxes on imported capital goods and raw materials used in production, reducing import-related costs for establishing manufacturing operations or large-scale projects. Incentive eligibility typically requires project registration through TradeInvest Timor-Leste and classification within priority sectors such as agriculture, tourism, renewable energy, or manufacturing. The specific incentive package varies depending on project characteristics, investment amount, location, and anticipated employment creation.

Companies operating within designated Special Economic Zones, particularly the ZEESM in Oecusse, may be eligible for enhanced tax incentive packages including potential corporate tax reductions, customs duty exemptions, or import duty relief for inputs used in zone operations. Special zone treatment may also include streamlined regulatory procedures and infrastructure support, though specific incentive details are subject to negotiation between the government and zone operators/investors.

Accelerated depreciation allowances may be available for capital investments in machinery and equipment, allowing faster cost recovery compared to standard depreciation schedules. This mechanism provides cash flow benefits by reducing taxable income in earlier years. Research and development expenditures may be eligible for enhanced deductions or investment tax credits, though availability depends on project classification and government designation of qualifying activities. Export-oriented businesses may benefit from tariff exemptions on imported inputs used in production of exported goods, implemented through customs drawback mechanisms or exemptions.

The government has also committed to maintaining the 10 percent CIT rate for general business operations, providing stability and competitive positioning compared to other regional jurisdictions. Tax incentives should be evaluated carefully in the context of overall project economics, with recognition that incentive benefits may be subject to conditions, compliance obligations, and potential withdrawal if conditions are not satisfied. Investors should coordinate with TradeInvest Timor-Leste and Tax Authority representatives to understand available incentives, application procedures, and ongoing compliance requirements.

5.6 International Tax Considerations

Timor-Leste currently does not have a comprehensive system of income tax treaties with other jurisdictions, though negotiations with selected countries for treaty establishment have occurred. The absence of a developed treaty network means that international transactions may be subject to tax in both Timor-Leste (as the source jurisdiction) and the investor's home country (as the residence jurisdiction), potentially resulting in double taxation without treaty relief mechanisms. Investors from countries with tax treaty coverage provisions may benefit from reduced withholding tax rates or other relief mechanisms if treaties are eventually established with Timor-Leste, but such benefits are not currently available in most cases.

Transfer pricing regulations require that transactions between related entities (parent companies, subsidiaries, affiliated businesses) be conducted at "arm's length" prices—prices that would prevail between unrelated parties conducting similar transactions. This principle applies to intercompany sales of goods, provision of services, and transfer of intellectual property. Documentation supporting transfer pricing determinations may be required by tax authorities, and challenged transfer prices can result in assessments of additional tax, penalties, and interest. Businesses with significant intercompany transactions should develop transfer pricing documentation supporting the commercial rationale and economic substance of pricing decisions.

Withholding taxes apply to certain cross-border payments including dividends, interest, and service fees paid to non-resident recipients. Withholding tax rates specified in tax regulations are applied at the source (by the payor), with the recipient entitled to credit withholding tax against home-country tax obligations if such credit is available under home-country law. Withholding tax treatment may be modified by tax treaties if applicable, with treaty rates typically lower than statutory rates. Dividend repatriation is permitted under Timorese law, with profits distributed to foreign shareholders subject to withholding tax at applicable rates.

Investors should be aware of potential tax obligations in their home countries regarding Timorese-source income, including potential foreign tax credit mechanisms available for taxes paid to Timor-Leste. Tax planning strategies should account for home-country tax consequences, particularly for substantial investments or long-term business operations. Consultation with international tax specialists familiar with both Timorese tax law and the investor's home country tax regime is recommended for significant international business arrangements. The Tax Authority provides guidance on international tax matters and may coordinate with foreign tax authorities on treaty-based matters, though the treaty network remains limited.
