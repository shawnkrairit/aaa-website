Madrid Protocol: Protecting Your Trademarks Across ASEAN

January 10, 2026

The Madrid Protocol has fundamentally changed how businesses protect their trademarks across ASEAN. With Thailand's accession in November 2017 and subsequent ratification by most ASEAN members, brand owners can now designate multiple Southeast Asian countries through a single international application filed with the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO).

How the Madrid System Works in ASEAN

A trademark owner with a base application or registration in their home country can file an international application through their national IP office, designating any or all ASEAN member states that are party to the Protocol. WIPO conducts a formal examination and, if the application meets requirements, records the international registration and notifies each designated country. Each country's IP office then conducts its own substantive examination under local law.

Practical Considerations

While the Madrid Protocol streamlines the filing process, it does not eliminate the need for local expertise. Each ASEAN jurisdiction applies its own examination standards, classification practices, and refusal grounds. Office actions from national IP offices require responses prepared in accordance with local law and procedure. This is where having trusted local counsel in each jurisdiction becomes essential.

Common grounds for provisional refusal in ASEAN jurisdictions include conflicts with prior marks, descriptiveness objections, and classification discrepancies between the international and national systems. Responding effectively to these refusals requires counsel who understands both the international registration system and the nuances of local practice.
